# Undernutrition & Malnutrition — research dossier

## 1. Scope (one paragraph)

This Part IV chapter teaches first-year medical students to **recognise the pathological under-tail of malnutrition and refer well** — not to prescribe the regimen. Students learn to **define** undernutrition/malnutrition as a diagnosable disease state (a nutrient deficit that produces measurable changes in body composition, function and outcome), including **disease-related malnutrition (DRM)**, the **GLIM** diagnostic logic (screen, then one phenotypic plus one etiologic criterion), and the **sarcopenia overlap** (a muscle disorder that intersects with, but is distinct from, a nutritional deficit). They build a **"who to suspect" recognition map** — frail/pre-frail community seniors, the socially isolated/bereaved/living-alone, the chronically ill, hospitalised inpatients, and the vulnerable **post-discharge** window — and apply a **screening tool conceptually** (MUST as the teaching exemplar: BMI + unintentional weight loss + acute-disease effect → low/medium/high risk → action/referral), understanding that screening ≠ assessment. They meet the **multidisciplinary team with the dietitian at its centre**, learn **oral nutrition support at recognition level** (food-first → food fortification → oral nutritional supplements/ONS), gain **refeeding-syndrome awareness** (recognise the at-risk patient and refer — do not manage), and learn **texture-modified diets (IDDSI) and dysphagia** with their referral triggers. Singapore is the primary frame throughout — HPB/MOH guidance, the Singapore Longitudinal Ageing Studies, the locally developed 3-MinNS screen, the SingSPEN post-discharge recommendations, and the EatSafe SG / IDDSI adoption. The conceptual seam handed over from the *Healthy Ageing* chapter is explicit: **Healthy Ageing = ageing in health; this chapter = the disease state.** Detailed enteral/parenteral regimens, refeeding *management*, route selection, and eating-disorder care are **signposted as future learning**, not taught.

## 2. Learning objectives (refined)

By the end of this chapter, the first-year student can:

1. **Define** undernutrition/malnutrition as a disease state (nutrient deficit → changed body composition + diminished function + adverse outcome), **classify** it by etiology (starvation-related / chronic disease-related / acute disease- or injury-related), and **outline** the GLIM two-step diagnostic logic (screen, then ≥1 phenotypic + ≥1 etiologic criterion) — without drilling severity grading.
2. **Explain** how sarcopenia (a muscle disorder) overlaps with but is distinct from malnutrition, and why a normal or high BMI does **not** exclude either (sarcopenic obesity; muscle, not just weight, must be considered).
3. **Recognise** the populations at increased risk of malnutrition (frail/pre-frail community seniors, socially isolated/bereaved/living-alone, chronically ill, hospitalised, post-discharge) and **justify** actively screening them rather than waiting for a "starved" appearance.
4. **Apply MUST conceptually** — BMI score + unintentional weight-loss score + acute-disease effect → low/medium/high risk → action/referral — and **explain** that screening flags risk and triggers referral, and is not the dietetic assessment.
5. **Outline** the multidisciplinary nutrition-support team with the **dietitian at its centre**, describe the **food-first → fortification → ONS** hierarchy at recognition level, and **recognise** referral triggers (positive screen, weight loss, poor intake, dysphagia, social driver).
6. **Recognise** the patient at risk of **refeeding syndrome** and the **red flags of dysphagia**, and **refer** — signposting that refeeding management and texture-level prescription (IDDSI) are dietitian/SLT-led future learning.

*(Verb level intentionally capped at recognise/define/classify/describe/explain/outline. No "manage refeeding with electrolyte X" or "prescribe ONS at Y kcal" objectives at first year.)*

## 3. Scope boundaries

**In:**
- Defining malnutrition/undernutrition as a disease state; the three etiologic categories; the role of inflammation in DRM.
- GLIM diagnostic *logic* (two-step; phenotypic + etiologic) at recognition depth; that GLIM sits **downstream** of screening.
- Sarcopenia overlap: EWGSOP2 concept, **Asian (AWGS 2019) cut-offs**, the Singapore sarcopenia CPG and local prevalence; sarcopenic obesity.
- The "who to suspect" at-risk recognition map, with Singapore community/hospital/post-discharge epidemiology.
- **MUST** conceptually (three components → risk band → action); other tools named for setting (NRS-2002 inpatient, MNA/MNA-SF older adults, WHO ICOPE vitality screen, local **3-MinNS**).
- The nutrition-support **MDT** and each profession's piece; dietitian-led assessment/plan; referral triggers and a low-friction referral pathway.
- **Oral nutrition support** at recognition level: food-first, food fortification (energy/protein density without more volume), ONS as an evidence-based **add-on**; honest benefit + caveat for the ONS evidence.
- **Refeeding-syndrome awareness**: pathophysiology sketch, NICE at-risk recognition criteria, that it is monitored/managed by specialists.
- **Texture-modified diets & dysphagia**: red flags → SLT referral; IDDSI as the standardised common language (0–7); the safety-vs-intake trade-off and fortified texture-modified food; Singapore EatSafe SG / IDDSI adoption.
- Awareness that other **special diets** exist (allergy/intolerance, religious/cultural) — recognise-and-refer only.

**Out (owned elsewhere / signposted future learning):**
- **Normal life-stage shifting needs in health** → the *Healthy Ageing* chapter and Part II (**boundary C**: Healthy Ageing = ageing in health; this chapter = the disease state). Do **not** re-teach normal age-related appetite/nutrient-need change here.
- **Detailed enteral/parenteral nutrition** prescribing, route selection, complications → signpost as future clinical learning.
- **Refeeding *management*** (electrolyte/thiamine replacement, calorie titration schedules) → recognise-and-refer only; signpost.
- **Detailed dysphagia grading / IDDSI level *prescription* and the bedside flow/fork tests** → SLT/dietitian-led future learning.
- **Eating disorders** → touched only in passing; owned by behavioural/psychiatric coverage and the childhood/adolescence chapters.
- **Obesity, the metabolic syndrome and Asian BMI cut-offs as a therapeutic topic** → the *Obesity & Metabolic Syndrome* chapter (narrative bookend — the two tails of malnutrition).
- **Bone / osteoporosis management** → the *Bone Health & Osteoporosis* chapter (sarcopenia/bone overlap cross-refs there).
- Referral *mechanics* (the anatomy of a safe referral) → the *Interprofessional Practice, Referral and Practitioner Self-Care* chapter.

## 4. Key concepts to teach (didactic spine)

1. **Malnutrition is a disease state, not "thinness."** A deficit of energy/protein/micronutrients that produces *measurable* adverse effects on body composition, function and outcome — link three features: a nutrient deficit → a bodily change (fat/muscle loss, low BMI) → a functional/outcome consequence (weakness, poor healing, complications, mortality). *(tie: `cederholm2017espenTerminology`)*
2. **Classify by etiology, because inflammation changes everything.** Three types — starvation-related (no inflammation), chronic disease-related (mild–moderate inflammation), acute disease/injury-related (severe inflammation, the cachexia end). Inflammation is why DRM resists feeding alone and is a doctor's problem, not just a catering one. *(tie: `jensen2010etiologyMalnutrition`, `cederholm2017espenTerminology`)*
3. **GLIM is how malnutrition is formally diagnosed — teach the logic, not the grading.** Two steps: screen positive first, then diagnose on **≥1 phenotypic** (weight loss, low BMI, reduced muscle mass) **plus ≥1 etiologic** (reduced intake/assimilation, inflammation/disease burden). "A phenotype of loss plus a cause." Still current: the 2025 five-year update *reaffirms*, not replaces, the framework. *(tie: `cederholm2019glim`, `jensen2019glim`, `jensen2025glimUpdate`)*
4. **Sarcopenia overlaps with, but is distinct from, malnutrition.** A muscle disorder — low strength confirmed by low muscle quantity/quality (EWGSOP2). Reduced muscle mass is a GLIM phenotype and undernutrition accelerates muscle loss, but a person can be sarcopenic at normal/high BMI (**sarcopenic obesity**) — so measure muscle, not just weight. Use **Asian (AWGS 2019) cut-offs**; Singapore has its own CPG (local prevalence ~13.6–25%). *(tie: `cruzjentoft2019ewgsop2`, `chen2020awgs2019`, `limSarcopeniaCpg2022`)*
5. **Build a "who to suspect" recognition map.** Frail/pre-frail community seniors; socially isolated/bereaved/living-alone; chronically ill (DRM mechanism); hospitalised inpatients; the post-discharge window. Knowing the group raises pre-test probability → screen and refer. *(tie: `bapenAtRisk`, `fried2001frailty`, `wei2017slasFrailty`, `wei2018slasOutcomes`, `wyman2025eatingAlone`, `ye2023daha`, `geSocialFrailtySg2022`, `lv2021hfMalnutrition`, `sgPostDischarge2023`)*
6. **Screening is the first-year competency — it flags risk and triggers referral; it is not the assessment.** A fast, low-skill filter anyone can apply; a positive screen → document/monitor/refer, not a care plan. GLIM is deliberately downstream. *(tie: `jensen2019glim`, `sgPostDischarge2023`)*
7. **MUST is the teaching exemplar; match the tool to the setting.** Three additive components (BMI + unplanned weight loss + acute-disease effect) → 0/1/2+ → low/medium/high risk → action pathway. Weight loss and acute illness score *independently of BMI* — a normal/high BMI does not exclude risk. Other validated tools: NRS-2002 (inpatient), MNA/MNA-SF (older adults), WHO ICOPE vitality screen (community), and Singapore's locally developed **3-MinNS**. *(tie: `bapenMUST`, `kondrup2003nrs2002`, `rubenstein2001mnasf`, `kaiser2009mnasf`, `whoIcope`, `lim2009minns`)*
8. **Nutrition support is a team activity with the dietitian at the hub.** Map the MDT — dietitian (assessment/plan), doctor (treats cause, champions nutrition), nurse (screening/monitoring), SLT (swallow/texture), physio/OT (function so feeding "sticks"), medical social worker (social drivers), pharmacist (drug–nutrient/electrolyte safety). The doctor's job is to *spin the wheel by referring*. *(tie: `sgPostDischarge2023`, `niceCG32NST`, `aclm2022comp`, `crowley2019`)*
9. **Oral nutrition support is a ladder: food-first → fortification → ONS.** Fix the *cause* of poor intake (isolation, dentition, mood, dysphagia, finances, drugs) and optimise ordinary food; fortify familiar dishes for more nutrition per mouthful *without more volume*; ONS is an evidence-based **add-on** when food-based measures fall short — not the opening move. Enteral/parenteral are specialist rungs (signpost). *(tie: `espenGeriatricsPractical2022`, `espenGeriatrics2019`, `bapenFoodFirst`, `hpbSeniors`, `milne2009ons`, `cheong2020shield`, `malnutritionPathwayCommunity`)*
10. **Refeeding syndrome: recognise the risk, do not manage it.** Starvation depletes intracellular phosphate/potassium/magnesium; restarting feeding (esp. carbohydrate) triggers an insulin surge that drives these intracellularly (± thiamine depletion) → hypophosphataemia (the hallmark) with cardiac/neurological risk. Use the NICE at-risk criteria to *spot and refer*; management is specialist/future learning. *(tie: `deSilva2020Refeeding`, `niceCg32`, `daSilva2020ASPEN`)*
11. **Dysphagia & texture-modified diets: know the red flags, refer to SLT, and mind the intake trade-off.** Coughing/choking, wet voice, prolonged meals, recurrent chest infections, weight loss/dehydration; one-third of aspiration is *silent* (no cough). IDDSI (0–7, colour-coded) is the standardised common language. Texture modification buys swallow safety but *lowers intake* → offer **fortified** texture-modified food and monitor. Singapore: EatSafe SG / national IDDSI adoption. *(tie: `wirth2016dysphagiaOlder`, `cichero2017iddsi`, `cichero2020iddsi2`, `wu2020tmd`, `espenGeriatricsPractical2022`, `mohEatSafeSg`, `iddsiSingapore`)*
12. **Nutrition is under-taught — recognise-and-refer is the safe posture.** Deficits in nutrition education are global; the evidence-based first-year stance is to screen, recognise and refer well, and to layer food-first + social supports early while dietetic capacity is scarce (a real Singapore constraint). *(tie: `crowley2019`, `aclm2022comp`, `afn2021curriculum`, `wong2021sgScopingReview`, `aacSingapore2025`, `mohMalnutritionTracking2025`, `mohPolyclinicScreening2026`)*

## 6. Singapore context

- **The under-tail begins in the community, not the ward.** In the population-based Singapore Longitudinal Ageing Studies (SLAS), **27.6% (~1 in 4) of community-dwelling older adults were at malnutrition risk** and 2.8% frankly malnourished on the MNA; **46.0% were pre-frail and 4.5% frail** (`wei2017slasFrailty`). Malnutrition and frailty are *related but distinct* syndromes that travel together — malnutrition risk was strongly associated with frailty (OR 2.72; established malnutrition OR 17.4). This is the local burden figure anchoring the whole chapter's Singapore frame.
- **Harm concentrates where frailty and poor nutrition co-occur.** In SLAS-1 (prospective), poor nutrition *alone* was not significantly associated with worse outcomes, but poor nutrition **with** pre-frailty/frailty raised mortality from 0.54 to **3.04 per 100 person-years (HR 1.72, 95% CI 1.01–2.92)** and roughly doubled functional disability (`wei2018slasOutcomes`). Teaching frame: screen the frail senior for malnutrition specifically — that is where intervention can help most, and why Singapore's frailty-focused community programmes (Age Well SG) matter for nutrition.
- **Social eating is a Singapore lever.** In the DaHA cohort, **24.1% of older adults ate alone most of the time although only 6.7% lived alone** — co-residence is *not* a shared meal — and 22.3% were at moderate-to-high malnutrition risk (`ye2023daha`). In a local panel study, **loneliness (not objective isolation) predicted frailty** (OR 2.90) and social participation was protective (`geSocialFrailtySg2022`). Singapore's one-person 65+ households **more than doubled (42,100 in 2014 → 87,200 in 2024)**, with living alone linked to ~double the depressive-symptom rate (`mohSeniorsAlone2026`). Ask *"who do you eat with?"* and *how the senior feels*, then refer to community/social supports.
- **Hospital and post-discharge burden (SingSPEN).** Among hospitalised older Singaporeans (≥65y) **~35% are malnourished at admission**, nutritional status typically declines during a stay, **~50% are malnourished at discharge**, and malnutrition persists **≥4 weeks post-discharge** (`sgPostDischarge2023`). Acute-care prevalence spans **14.7–65.0%** across local studies (`sgPostDischarge2023`, `wong2021sgScopingReview`). The recommended model: **screen everyone within 24 h** (JCI-aligned), let **any HCP refer directly to a dietitian**, and hand over to a multidisciplinary community team — but the paper flags **scarce community dietetic capacity** and **no structured post-discharge nutrition pathway** yet.
- **The locally built screen: 3-MinNS.** Developed and validated at **National University Hospital (Su Lin Lim et al.)** — *not* CGH — using three nurse-assessable parameters (weight loss + reduced intake + muscle wastage), cut-off 3 giving sens 86%/spec 83% vs SGA (`lim2009minns`); nurse-administrable and prognostic (worse LOS/cost/mortality, observationally) (`lim2013nurseScreening`, `lim2014minnsPrognostic`). Present as the concrete *local example* of the MUST-style concept — admission screening is driven by JCI accreditation, not a single national tool mandate.
- **Food-first in local idiom.** HPB's *Dietary Guidelines for Singapore's Seniors* (2012) target the local pattern — soft refined starch (rice/noodles/bread), too little protein and vegetables (~1 in 4 seniors below protein and vegetable recommendations) — advising **nutrient density over quantity, more protein, calcium-rich/fortified foods, softer textures, more fluids** (`hpbSeniors`). The practical lever is **fortifying familiar hawker/home dishes**: add egg, minced fish, tofu or extra chicken to rice porridge; enrich mee sua or soup noodles with egg and protein toppings; choose higher-protein yong tau foo items (fish paste, tofu, egg) over plain vegetables. HPB STAR recipes support this.
- **Community and policy touchpoints.** Under **Age Well SG** the Active Ageing Centre network expanded (154→220 by 2025; **223 centres by April 2025**, ~8 in 10 seniors near one), running communal dining, befriending and home visits for the isolated — the social side of malnutrition prevention, though operators are **manpower-lean** (`mohAgeWellSg2023`, `aacSingapore2025`). National data show ~1 in 4 older adults below minimum calories and ~1 in 2 below protein (`mohMalnutritionTracking2025`); **standardised polyclinic/AAC malnutrition screening is under active discussion (2026) but not a settled mandate** (`mohPolyclinicScreening2026`). The **NNS 2022** salt-heavy, low-wholegrain population backdrop compounds the frail senior's low protein/micronutrient density, tying the two tails of malnutrition together (`mohNNS2022`).
- **Texture standardisation: EatSafe SG.** Singapore has adopted **IDDSI as its national standard** via the MOH Dysphagia Diet Standardisation Committee (with AIC), branded **EatSafe SG**, spanning acute hospitals, community hospitals, nursing homes and social service agencies (`mohEatSafeSg`, `iddsiSingapore`) — so "Level 4" means the same thing in every ward and home, reducing handover errors.

## 7. Pillar intersections

- **nutrition × social connection (primary).** Feeding is inherently social; **eating alone**, bereavement and loneliness drive under-eating and weight loss in older adults (`wyman2025eatingAlone`, `ye2023daha`, `geSocialFrailtySg2022`). Communal dining and befriending (Age Well SG / AACs) are the community answer (`mohAgeWellSg2023`, `aacSingapore2025`). Recognise the driver and refer to social supports + dietitian — do not medicalise isolation.
- **nutrition × physical activity.** Protein and feeding only protect and rebuild muscle when paired with activity/rehab (physio/OT in the MDT); this is the sarcopenia bridge — cross-reference the *Bone Health & Osteoporosis* chapter for the muscle–bone overlap and resistance-exercise/bone-loading content. Nutrition and activity are synergistic, not substitutes.
- **nutrition × mental wellbeing / mood.** Depression and low mood suppress appetite and intake; in Singapore, seniors living alone are ~twice as likely to report depressive symptoms (`mohSeniorsAlone2026`). Mood is both a driver of poor intake and a reason the frail senior warrants nutritional attention — recognise and refer.

## 8. Clinical application & case ideas (case-player-ready — MCQ / choice-node, do-no-harm aware)

*(Per house rule, per-chapter cases are choice/MCQ only — no live AI patient-chat node here; that belongs to the Integrated cases chapter.)*

### Case A — "Mdm Tan, 82, living alone, losing weight" (recognition, MUST, refer)
- **Stem:** Attends polyclinic with her daughter, who notes looser clothes and unfinished meals. Widowed last year; lives alone; mild knee osteoarthritis; eats alone most days. Weight down ~7% over 4 months; BMI 18.9; eating little.
- **Node 1 — What raises pre-test probability?** ✔ Living alone + bereavement + eating alone + weight loss (social-driver at-risk group) / ✘ "She looks fine, BMI isn't <18.5" → feedback: weight loss and eating-alone matter *independently of BMI* (`wyman2025eatingAlone`, `bapenMUST`).
- **Node 2 — Screen (MUST conceptually):** BMI score + >10% weight-loss score → **high risk**. ✔ "High risk → refer to dietitian + document intake" / ✘ "Medium risk, re-screen next year" → feedback on scoring + that a positive screen triggers *action*, not a plan (`bapenMUST`, `jensen2019glim`).
- **Node 3 — First-line action:** ✔ Food-first + social — fortify familiar dishes (egg/fish/tofu in porridge/mee sua), small frequent meals, involve daughter/communal dining, address mood and dentition, **refer to dietitian** / ✘ "Start a supplement drink and nothing else" → feedback: ONS is an *add-on*, fix the cause first (`bapenFoodFirst`, `espenGeriatrics2019`).
- **Do-no-harm trap:** ✘ "Start high-calorie feeding fast" → refeeding-risk feedback: recognise the very-low-BMI/low-intake at-risk patient and refer for cautious, monitored refeeding (`niceCg32`, `deSilva2020Refeeding`).

### Case B — "Mr Lim, 76, post-stroke, coughing at meals" (dysphagia red flags → SLT/IDDSI)
- **Stem:** Discharged 2 weeks ago after a stroke; daughter reports coughing on water, a "wet" voice after meals, and very slow eating; he has lost weight.
- **Node 1 — Red flags?** ✔ Coughing/choking + wet voice + prolonged meals + weight loss = dysphagia pattern → **refer to SLT** / ✘ "No cough on solids, so swallow is safe" → feedback: up to one-third of aspiration is *silent* (`wirth2016dysphagiaOlder`).
- **Node 2 — Who prescribes the texture level?** ✔ SLT assesses swallow and sets the safe **IDDSI** level; dietitian ensures the diet is nutritionally adequate / ✘ "I'll order a pureed diet myself" → feedback: level *prescription* is SLT/dietitian-led (`cichero2017iddsi`, `mohEatSafeSg`).
- **Node 3 — Intake trade-off:** ✔ Texture-modified food should be **fortified** and intake monitored (safety ≠ adequacy) / ✘ "Pureed diet solves the nutrition problem" → feedback: texture-modified diets have *lower* energy/calcium intake (`wu2020tmd`, `espenGeriatricsPractical2022`).

### Case C — "Mr Ho, 68, heart failure, admitted breathless" (DRM + screen-every-admission)
- **Stem:** Admitted with decompensated heart failure; BMI 24; poor appetite for months; recent unintentional weight loss.
- **Node 1 — Could a BMI-24 patient be malnourished?** ✔ Yes — chronic disease + inflammation + reduced intake = disease-related malnutrition; ~46% of HF patients are malnourished / ✘ "BMI 24 is normal, so no" → feedback: normal BMI does not exclude DRM (`lv2021hfMalnutrition`, `cederholm2017espenTerminology`).
- **Node 2 — GLIM logic (recognition):** ✔ A *phenotype of loss* (weight loss) + a *cause* (disease burden/inflammation, reduced intake) → screen positive, refer for GLIM diagnosis by the team / ✘ "I'll diagnose and grade malnutrition myself now" → feedback: screening is yours; GLIM assessment is downstream/dietitian-led (`cederholm2019glim`, `jensen2019glim`).

## 9. Common misconceptions & pitfalls

1. **"Malnutrition means looking starved / only in poor countries."** It is a diagnosable disease state common in high-income, hospitalised and community-dwelling older Singaporeans — and often missed (`cederholm2017espenTerminology`, `sgPostDischarge2023`).
2. **"Only thin/low-BMI patients are at risk."** Weight loss and acute illness score *independently of BMI*; a normal or high BMI does not exclude malnutrition (`bapenMUST`) — and someone can be **sarcopenic at a high BMI** (sarcopenic obesity) (`cruzjentoft2019ewgsop2`).
3. **"Screening = diagnosis."** MUST/3-MinNS flag *risk* and trigger referral; GLIM diagnosis is a separate, downstream, dietitian/team step (`jensen2019glim`, `bapenMUST`).
4. **"The doctor sorts out the feeding plan."** Detailed assessment and planning are **dietitian/MDT-led**; the first-year role is screen → recognise → refer (`sgPostDischarge2023`, `aclm2022comp`).
5. **"A supplement drink fixes undernutrition."** Food-first and addressing the cause (isolation, dentition, mood, swallow, finances, drugs) come first; ONS is an evidence-based **add-on** — and the ONS evidence is honest: **weight and complications improve modestly but there is no significant overall mortality benefit** (`milne2009ons`), and the local SHIELD gains were **driven by the weight component** as an add-on *to* counselling (`shieldChew2021`).
6. **"No cough means the swallow is safe."** Up to one-third of aspiration is **silent** (no cough); refer on the pattern, not just the cough (`wirth2016dysphagiaOlder`).
7. **"A pureed/soft diet has solved the nutrition problem."** Texture modification buys swallow safety but *reduces* energy/calcium intake — offer **fortified** texture-modified food and monitor (`wu2020tmd`, `espenGeriatricsPractical2022`).
8. **"Feed the starved patient up quickly."** That risks **refeeding syndrome** — recognise the at-risk patient (very low BMI, big weight loss, prolonged low intake, low baseline electrolytes, alcohol/insulin/chemo history) and **refer** for cautious, monitored refeeding; management is future learning (`niceCg32`, `deSilva2020Refeeding`, `daSilva2020ASPEN`).
9. **"Sarcopenia is just a European-cut-off thing."** Use **Asian (AWGS 2019)** cut-offs in Singapore; European values overestimate deficits in smaller-framed Asian populations (`chen2020awgs2019`, `limSarcopeniaCpg2022`).
10. **"3-MinNS was developed at CGH."** It is a **NUH / Su Lin Lim** tool; the CGH-linked Samuel Chew is a co-author on the separate post-discharge recommendations, not 3-MinNS (`lim2009minns`, `sgPostDischarge2023`).
11. **Over-reach:** trying to prescribe ONS doses, enteral/parenteral regimens, refeeding electrolyte replacement, or IDDSI levels — all explicitly **future/specialist learning**.
