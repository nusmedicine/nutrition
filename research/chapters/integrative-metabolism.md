# Integrative Metabolism (Fed / Fasted) — research dossier

## 1. Scope (one paragraph)

This chapter is the point where isolated nutrients and pathways become a **working system**, built in
**two layers**. **Part A — the metabolic map** teaches, from scratch, the handful of core pathways and
*how they interconnect* (this book does **not** assume a separate biochemistry course): glycolysis,
gluconeogenesis, glycogen storage/breakdown, the pentose phosphate pathway, β-oxidation, fatty-acid
synthesis, ketogenesis, the TCA cycle, oxidative phosphorylation, and amino-acid/nitrogen handling — all
at **interconnection-map depth** (each pathway as inputs → outputs, its cellular home, its one *named*
control step, and the shared molecules that link it to the rest — **not** enzyme kinetics). The map
organises around **five crossroads** (glucose-6-phosphate, pyruvate, **acetyl-CoA**, oxaloacetate/TCA,
the amino-acid pool) and **four one-way valves** — the most important being **pyruvate → acetyl-CoA
(PDH)**, the irreversible door that explains why **fat cannot make net glucose** and therefore why the
body must defend glucose in fasting. **Part B — the dynamic integration** then brings that same map to
life: how metabolites actually **flow among organs under different states — fed, fasted, prolonged
fasting, exercise, and disease** — thrown by the **insulin : glucagon ratio**. In the fed state insulin
drives storage everywhere; in the fasted state the counter-regulatory hormones defend blood glucose for
the **brain** and **red blood cell** (glycogenolysis → gluconeogenesis) while **lipolysis** fuels
everything else; in **starvation** the liver's **ketones** spare muscle protein — the definitive
correction to "the brain can only use glucose." Part B owns the **inter-organ cycles** (Cori,
glucose–alanine), the **fuel-stores accounting** (glycogen ≈ a day; fat ≈ weeks–months; protein =
functional tissue, not a designed store), the **respiratory-quotient interpretation** of which fuel is
burning, **exercise fuel selection**, and **lipoprotein interconversion**, closing on recognition-level
clinical hooks — **physiological ketosis vs diabetic ketoacidosis**, **alcohol → hypoglycaemia**,
refeeding-syndrome recognition, and diabetes as "the switch, broken" (management → Ch.15). Singapore-first
hooks — **Ramadan** as the fed→fasted→refed cycle lived daily, the honest state of **intermittent-fasting
and keto** claims, and the **"War on Diabetes"** — keep the physiology grounded and food-first.

## 2. Learning objectives (recognition-level)

By the end of the chapter a first-year student can:

*Layer A — the metabolic map:*

- **LO-A1** *Read* the metabolic map as one interconnected network organised around **five crossroads**
  (glucose-6-phosphate, pyruvate, acetyl-CoA, oxaloacetate/TCA, the amino-acid pool), and *distinguish*
  anabolic from catabolic flow and the energy currencies (ATP/NADH/FADH₂ vs NADPH).
- **LO-A2** *State*, for each core pathway (glycolysis, gluconeogenesis, glycogen storage/breakdown,
  β-oxidation, fatty-acid synthesis, ketogenesis, the TCA cycle, oxidative phosphorylation, the pentose
  phosphate pathway), its **inputs → outputs, cellular location, and one *named* committed step** — without
  reciting enzyme mechanisms.
- **LO-A3** *Explain* the **four one-way valves** — especially **pyruvate → acetyl-CoA (PDH)** — and use
  them to justify why **fat cannot make net glucose**, why **gluconeogenesis is not reverse glycolysis**,
  and why opposing pathways are **reciprocally regulated** (never both fully on).

*Layer B — the dynamic integration:*

- **LO-1** *Explain* that the **insulin : glucagon ratio** is the master signal that switches the body
  between the fed (storage) and fasted (mobilisation) states, and *state* that insulin is the master
  **anabolic** hormone acting on **all three** macronutrients (not just glucose).
- **LO-2** *Describe*, organ by organ (liver, skeletal muscle, adipose), what happens to an absorbed
  meal in the **fed state**, and *contrast* **GLUT4** (insulin-responsive, muscle/adipose) with
  **GLUT2** (liver, insulin-independent) as the reason muscle/fat uptake surges with insulin while the
  liver takes up glucose in proportion to concentration.
- **LO-3** *Name* the counter-regulatory hormones in order of speed (glucagon → epinephrine → cortisol/
  growth hormone), and *explain* why the **fasted-state priority is defending blood glucose for the
  brain and red blood cells** (RBCs have no mitochondria; fatty acids are a poor brain fuel).
- **LO-4** *Sequence* the fasted response — **glycogenolysis first, then gluconeogenesis** — and
  *explain the centrepiece fact that muscle glycogen cannot raise blood glucose* (muscle lacks
  glucose-6-phosphatase; only liver and kidney export free glucose), while muscle contributes carbon
  *indirectly* via the Cori and glucose–alanine cycles.
- **LO-5** *Explain* **ketogenesis as fuel integration**: that in prolonged fasting the liver makes
  **ketone bodies** it cannot itself use (no SCOT) and exports them, that ketones (unlike fatty acids)
  fuel the brain, and that the **brain's switch to ketones spares muscle protein** — the survival
  adaptation.
- **LO-6** *Distinguish* **physiological/nutritional ketosis** (controlled, modest, normal pH) from
  **diabetic ketoacidosis** (uncontrolled, very high ketones, acidosis) — the key myth-correction —
  and *account* for the body's fuel stores (glycogen ≈ a day; fat ≈ weeks–months; protein = functional
  tissue).
- **LO-7** *Interpret* the **respiratory quotient** as a readout of which fuel is being oxidised
  (carbohydrate ≈ 1.0, fat ≈ 0.7), *describe* how **exercise fuel selection** shifts with intensity
  (the crossover concept; glycogen depletion = "hitting the wall"), and *trace* **lipoprotein
  interconversion** (chylomicron → remnant; VLDL → IDL → LDL; HDL reverse transport).
- **LO-8** *Recognise* (not manage) the clinical face of a broken switch: diabetes and DKA, **alcohol +
  fasting → hypoglycaemia**, hypoglycaemia counter-regulation symptoms, and refeeding-syndrome risk —
  cross-referencing Ch.15.

## 4. Key concepts to teach (didactic spine)

**Part A — the metabolic map (the basic pathways and how they interconnect).**

*Taught at **interconnection-map depth**: each pathway as inputs → outputs, its cellular home, its one
committed control step *named* (not its kinetics), and how it links to the rest through a few shared
"crossroads" molecules. The goal is a student who can **read the map and predict where carbon and energy
flow**, not one who can recite enzyme mechanisms. This is the foundation Part B stands on. (The one basis
Part A borrows rather than builds is **lipoprotein class identity** — that is Ch.3's, covered before this
chapter; Part B re-enters it only as an interconversion pathway.)*

- **A1. Metabolism is one interconnected network, not a list of separate pathways — organised around
  ~5 crossroads.** The whole map hangs on five hub metabolites where pathways meet: **glucose-6-phosphate,
  pyruvate, acetyl-CoA, oxaloacetate/TCA intermediates, and the amino-acid pool.** Teach the crossroads
  (a few molecule names), not the ~50 enzymes. Two ideas run throughout: **catabolic** (break down →
  converge on acetyl-CoA → CO₂ + reduced cofactors + ATP) vs **anabolic** (build/store → diverge, costing
  ATP + NADPH); and **reciprocal regulation** — opposing pathways are never both fully on (no futile
  cycles). → `crossroadsMap`
- **A2. Crossroads 1 — glucose-6-phosphate (G6P): the carbohydrate roundabout.** Glucose entering a cell
  is trapped as G6P (hexokinase / liver glucokinase). From G6P, carbon flows four ways: **burn**
  (glycolysis), **store** (glycogen), **build** (pentose phosphate pathway → NADPH + ribose-5-phosphate),
  or — in liver/kidney only — **export** as free glucose. Learn this one node and the carbohydrate map
  falls into place. → `g6pHub`
- **A3. Glycolysis — glucose → 2 pyruvate, in the cytosol, and it does NOT need oxygen.** Yields a little
  ATP + NADH; committed control step = **phosphofructokinase-1 (PFK-1)**. Aerobically, pyruvate →
  acetyl-CoA into the mitochondrion; anaerobically, pyruvate → **lactate**, which regenerates NAD⁺ so
  glycolysis keeps running with no O₂ (why red cells and sprinting muscle still make ATP). Oxygen is used
  later, at the electron transport chain — not here. *(The pentose phosphate pathway is a one-box branch
  off G6P (cytosolic; committed step glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase, G6PD): NADPH for biosynthesis/antioxidant defence + ribose for nucleotides — and no ATP.)* → `glycolysisMap`
- **A4. Crossroads 2 — pyruvate: the three-way junction.** From pyruvate, carbon can go to **acetyl-CoA**
  (pyruvate dehydrogenase, PDH — a *one-way* door, see A6), **oxaloacetate** (pyruvate carboxylase — the
  first step of gluconeogenesis), **lactate** (regenerating NAD⁺), or **alanine** (into the amino-acid
  pool). It is the single most important branch point on the map. → `pyruvateNode`
- **A5. Gluconeogenesis is NOT reverse glycolysis — and glycogen is the fast buffer.** Making glucose from
  lactate/glycerol/glucogenic amino acids (liver, some kidney) runs most glycolytic steps backward but
  must **bypass the 3 irreversible ones with 4 different enzymes** (pyruvate carboxylase + PEPCK,
  fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase, glucose-6-phosphatase). Those bypass enzymes are the control points, and
  glycolysis ↔ gluconeogenesis are **reciprocally regulated** so they don't cancel out. Glycogen is the
  short-term glucose bank (glycogenesis ⇄ glycogenolysis, reciprocally controlled); note now — it pays
  off in Part B — that **muscle glycogen can't raise blood glucose** (muscle lacks glucose-6-phosphatase).
  → `gluconeogenesisMap`, `glycogenMap`
- **A6. ★ Crossroads 3 — acetyl-CoA: the universal funnel and the map's key one-way valve.** All three
  fuels converge here: carbohydrate (via pyruvate + PDH), **fat** (via β-oxidation), and ketogenic amino
  acids. From acetyl-CoA, carbon goes to the **TCA cycle** (burn), **fatty-acid synthesis** (store), or
  **ketone bodies** (liver, fasting). **The load-bearing fact:** pyruvate → acetyl-CoA (PDH) is
  **irreversible with no way back**, so **acetyl-CoA — and therefore fat — cannot make net glucose** (only
  the *glycerol* backbone of a triglyceride can). This single one-way valve is *why* the body must defend
  and remake glucose in fasting — the hinge on which all of Part B turns. → `acetylCoaHub`
- **A7. Fat and its reciprocal switch.** Burning fat: fatty acids enter the mitochondrion via the
  **carnitine shuttle (CPT1 = the gated control step)**, and β-oxidation chops them into **many
  acetyl-CoA** + NADH/FADH₂ (why fat is the most energy-dense fuel, ~9 vs ~4 kcal/g). Making/storing fat:
  **acetyl-CoA carboxylase (ACC)** commits acetyl-CoA to fatty-acid synthesis (cytosol; needs **NADPH**
  from the PPP), then 3 fatty acids + glycerol → triglyceride. The elegant switch: ACC's product
  **malonyl-CoA blocks CPT1**, so you don't build and burn fat at once — the cleanest example of
  reciprocal regulation. **Ketogenesis:** when acetyl-CoA floods the fasting liver, it exports the surplus
  as **ketone bodies**; the liver lacks **SCOT** so it cannot burn its own ketones (it makes them for
  everyone else — set-up for Part B). → `fatMap`, `ketogenesisMap`
- **A8. Crossroads 4 — the TCA cycle + oxidative phosphorylation: the central furnace.** Acetyl-CoA +
  oxaloacetate enter the **TCA (citric acid) cycle** (mitochondrial matrix) → 2 CO₂ + **NADH/FADH₂** + a
  little GTP, regenerating oxaloacetate. It is the **final common oxidative pathway for all three fuels**
  (not just carbohydrate) and is **amphibolic** — intermediates are also drained for biosynthesis and
  refilled from amino acids (anaplerosis). The NADH/FADH₂ then feed **oxidative phosphorylation**:
  electrons flow down the electron transport chain, pumping protons whose backflow through ATP synthase
  makes the **bulk of the cell's ATP**, and **O₂ is the final electron acceptor** (→ water) — *this*, not
  glycolysis, is where oxygen is used. → `tcaHub`, `oxphosMap`
- **A9. The energy currency — ATP is spent, not stored.** **ATP** is the immediate, rapidly-recycled
  currency (the body holds only seconds-worth and turns over roughly its own body weight in ATP per day) —
  bulk energy is stored as **glycogen and fat**, cashed into ATP on demand. **NADH/FADH₂** are the
  "rechargeable batteries" carrying electrons to the ETC; **NADPH** (from the PPP) is a *separate* currency
  for building things, not for ATP. Full glucose oxidation yields **~30–32 ATP** (approximate — the old
  "36–38" is outdated) versus **~2** from anaerobic glycolysis alone. → `energyCurrency`
- **A10. Crossroads 5 — the amino-acid pool + the map's logic.** Protein is a **flow, not a store** (no
  designed amino-acid reserve — mobilising it costs functional tissue). To use an amino acid, the
  **nitrogen is removed first** (transamination — think ALT/AST — and deamination) and disposed of as
  **urea** (liver); the carbon skeleton then enters the map as **glucogenic** (→ pyruvate/TCA intermediates
  → glucose) or **ketogenic** (→ acetyl-CoA → ketones, no glucose; **leucine and lysine are the only
  purely ketogenic** ones). Step back and the map has a *logic*: a few crossroads, and **four one-way
  valves** (the three gluconeogenesis bypasses + the PDH door) that give metabolism its **direction** — and
  set up the whole point of Part B, that hormones simply *throw* which way this reciprocally-regulated
  network runs under fed, fasted and fasting conditions. → `aminoAcidMap`, `mapLogic`

**Part B — the dynamic integration (the same map, now thrown by hormones across fed / fasted / prolonged-fasting / exercise / disease states).**

**Part 1 — The two states and the master switch.**

1. **The insulin : glucagon RATIO is the master switch.** After a meal the ratio is HIGH (store); in
   fasting it is LOW (mobilise). It "varies inversely with the need for endogenous glucose production,
   being lowest in total starvation." Teach the *direction*, not a memorised number. → `unger1971ratio`
2. **Fed = anabolic dominance; insulin acts on all three macronutrients.** Insulin simultaneously stores
   glucose (glycogen), stores fat (clears and stockpiles triglyceride), and builds protein — and shuts
   down breakdown. *"Insulin is the store-and-build conductor, not just a sugar hormone."* → `merheb2026insulin`
3. **Organ disposition of a fed meal** (integration, not enzyme steps): **liver** = the glucose buffer/
   hub (takes up glucose via GLUT2, stores glycogen, converts excess to fat exported as VLDL, receives
   portal amino acids); **skeletal muscle** = insulin's biggest glucose sink (**GLUT4** uptake →
   glycogen; amino-acid uptake → protein); **adipose** = fat depot (GLUT4 uptake; **lipoprotein lipase**
   clears chylomicron/VLDL triglyceride into the cell; insulin is **anti-lipolytic**). → `merheb2026insulin`,
   `wang2009lpl`
4. **The clean integration point — GLUT4 vs GLUT2.** GLUT4 (muscle/adipose) needs insulin to reach the
   membrane, so their glucose uptake surges after a meal; GLUT2 (liver) is insulin-independent and
   low-affinity, so the liver takes up glucose roughly in proportion to its concentration. Insulin's
   hepatic job is on the *enzymes*, not the transporter. → `chadt2020glucose`
5. **The switch flips — the fasted state.** Falling glucose → ↓insulin, ↑glucagon (LOW ratio). The
   **counter-regulatory quartet** with a pecking order: **glucagon** (primary, rapid; drives hepatic
   glucose output), **epinephrine** (rapid backup, critical if glucagon fails), **cortisol** and
   **growth hormone** (slower, *permissive*, sustain prolonged fasting). → `cryer1993counterreg`, `rizza1979`
6. **The overriding fasted priority: defend blood glucose for the brain and RBCs.** The brain is
   glucose-dependent (and, honestly, *fatty acids are a poor brain fuel* — they travel albumin-bound
   and are oxidised poorly by neurons, **not** simply "unable to cross" the barrier); the **RBC has no
   mitochondria** so it is absolutely glucose-dependent (glycolysis → lactate only). Everything else
   that *can* burn fat does, leaving glucose for these two — **"glucose sparing."** → `schonfeld2013`,
   `statpearlsAnaerobic`
7. **Order of events — glycogenolysis first, then gluconeogenesis.** Hepatic glycogen (~80–120 g) is
   released as free glucose for the first ~12–24 h; as it depletes, **gluconeogenesis** takes over
   (liver, later kidney), building glucose from **lactate, glycerol and glucogenic amino acids** (this
   chapter names the substrates; the enzyme steps are core biochem). → `statpearlsGlycogenolysis`,
   `statpearlsGngPhys`
8. **★ CENTREPIECE ★ Muscle glycogen cannot raise blood glucose.** Muscle lacks **glucose-6-phosphatase**,
   so its glucose-6-phosphate is trapped for local use; only **liver (and kidney)** can release free
   glucose. Muscle helps blood glucose *indirectly*, by exporting **lactate** (Cori cycle) or **alanine**
   (glucose–alanine cycle) to the liver. *"The liver is the gatekeeper of blood glucose; muscle only
   supplies carbon."* → `statpearlsGlycogenolysis`
9. **Lipolysis is the main fuel supply in fasting.** Adipose triglyceride → **fatty acids + glycerol**;
   fatty acids fuel muscle/heart/liver/most tissues; glycerol feeds gluconeogenesis (the *only* part of
   fat that can make glucose — **fatty-acid carbons cannot be net-converted to glucose**). Insulin is
   anti-lipolytic; catecholamines/cortisol/GH stimulate lipolysis. → `statpearlsLipolysis`

**Part 2 — Prolonged fasting / starvation.**

10. **Fuel-stores accounting — three very different accounts.** **Glycogen** = the wallet (small, fast,
    ~a day's worth (~600 kcal counting liver alone, up to ~1,500–2,000 kcal once muscle glycogen and its
    bound water are included)). **Fat** = the savings account (vast, weeks-to-months, ~100,000–140,000
    kcal, ~80–85% of stored energy). **Protein** = *not a designed store at all* — it is functional
    tissue (muscle, enzymes, immune cells); spending it is borrowing against the body's own machinery.
    This framing is the section's spine. → `cahill2006fuel`, `bergStryerFuel`
11. **Fasting is a staged hand-off (Cahill phases), not a switch.** Fed → post-absorptive (glycogenolysis)
    → early fasting / gluconeogenic (~day 1–3) → prolonged / ketotic protein-sparing (days–weeks). Teach
    as a *continuum with approximate ranges*, and note the classic figures come from **small studies of
    obese subjects** (Owen 1967, n=3) — foundational but not modern large trials. → `cahill2006fuel`,
    `owen1967brain`
12. **Ketogenesis — the liver is a ketone factory that cannot burn its own product.** Flooded with
    acetyl-CoA from fat breakdown, the liver makes **ketone bodies — β-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate,
    and the exhaled acetone** — but lacks **SCOT** (succinyl-CoA:3-oxoacid CoA transferase; gene OXCT1),
    so it **exports** them rather than using them. Ketones are water-soluble and cross the blood–brain
    barrier (the fatty-acid *workaround* for the brain). *(Educator aside: β-hydroxybutyrate is
    chemically a hydroxy-acid, not a true ketone; acetone is the "fruity/nail-polish" breath.)* → `statpearlsKetogenesis`,
    `laffel1999`
13. **The elegant payoff — brain ketone adaptation spares protein.** Over days-to-weeks the brain shifts
    the **majority** of its energy to ketones (from <5% overnight → ~30% by ~day 3 → ~60% by weeks;
    brain glucose demand falls from ~120 toward ~40 g/day). Less glucose needed → less gluconeogenesis →
    **less muscle broken down** → survival extended from weeks toward months. This is the definitive
    refutation of "the brain can only use glucose." → `owen1967brain`, `cahill2006fuel`
14. **Physiological ketosis ≠ ketoacidosis — the key myth-correction.** Fasting/nutritional ketosis is
    *controlled by residual insulin* (β-hydroxybutyrate ~0.5–3 mmol/L in a keto diet, ~5–7 in prolonged
    starvation, **normal blood pH**). **Diabetic ketoacidosis** is *uncontrolled* (near-zero insulin →
    ketones often 10–25 mmol/L in severe cases — but **the discriminator is the acidosis, not the ketone
    number**: DKA is diagnostically defined by an **anion-gap acidosis** with β-hydroxybutyrate ≥3.0–3.8
    mmol/L, so the ranges *overlap*; usually hyperglycaemic) — a medical emergency. *Same molecules; the
    difference is regulatory context and blood pH, not the ketone concentration alone.* *(DKA management → Ch.15; recognition only here.)* →
    `kolb2021ketones`, `statpearlsDKA`, `sheikhali2008bhb`
15. **Refeeding syndrome (recognition only).** Feeding a starved person triggers an insulin surge that
    drives **phosphate, potassium and magnesium into cells** → dangerous **hypophosphataemia**
    (± hypokalaemia, hypomagnesaemia, thiamine deficiency), potentially fatal. First-years: *recognise
    the at-risk patient and refer/monitor*; management is clinical years. → `statpearlsRefeeding`,
    `niceCg32`
16. **"Starvation mode wrecks your metabolism" is overstated (keep light).** Adaptive thermogenesis is
    real but **modest (~100–300 kcal/day typical) and largely reversible**; the dramatic "Biggest Loser"
    persistence finding was **reinterpreted by Hall (2022)** as a *physical-activity-driven compensation*
    (the constrained-energy model), not a permanent, generalizable metabolic wreck. Full appetite/
    weight-regulation depth is **Ch.8**. → `muller2013adaptive`, `hall2022biggestloser`

**Part 3 — Inter-organ integration and reading the switch.**

17. **The metabolic division of labour** (the fuel matrix): **liver** = hub (glucose buffer, ketone
    factory, urea disposal); **muscle** = big burner + local-only glycogen store, exports lactate/
    alanine; **adipose** = fat store + fatty-acid/glycerol supplier; **brain** = glucose then ketones;
    **RBC** = glucose → lactate only; **kidney** = a second gluconeogenic organ in prolonged fasting
    (up to **~40–50%** of glucose production **in prolonged fasting** — teach as a range; only ~20%
    post-absorptively). Adipose also *signals* its fuel reserves — releasing **leptin in proportion to
    fat mass** (the metabolic read-out of energy stores) — but leptin's appetite/adiposity role is
    **Ch.8**. → `statpearlsGngPhys`, `gerich2001renalgng`
18. **The two cycles** (recognition-level): the **Cori cycle** (muscle/RBC lactate → liver → glucose;
    it *relocates* the ATP cost to the well-oxygenated liver so anaerobic tissue keeps working — lactate
    is a **fuel and gluconeogenic precursor, not waste**) and the **glucose–alanine cycle** (muscle
    exports alanine carrying nitrogen → liver makes glucose and disposes of the nitrogen as urea). →
    `brooks2018lactate`, `felig1973alanine`
19. **RQ tells you which fuel is burning — Ch.6 owns the interpretation.** RQ = CO₂ produced / O₂
    consumed: **carbohydrate ≈ 1.0, fat ≈ 0.7**, protein ≈ 0.8–0.82, mixed diet ≈ 0.85. Fed → high RQ
    (carbs); fasted → falling RQ (fat). *(Educator aside: RQ can transiently exceed 1.0 during net
    lipogenesis.)* Ch.2 defined RQ; here we apply it. → `rqValuesStd` *(RQ values are textbook-standard;
    see §11)*
20. **Exercise fuel selection is an intensity/duration continuum.** Phosphocreatine (seconds) → muscle
    glycogen / anaerobic glycolysis (short, high-intensity) → fat oxidation (lower intensity, sustained);
    the **crossover concept** — as intensity rises, fuel shifts from fat toward carbohydrate. **Glycogen
    depletion = "hitting the wall"** (why a marathoner fades near 30 km). → `brooks1994crossover`
21. **Lipoprotein interconversion/clearance — the Ch.3/Ch.5/Ch.6 thread.** Chylomicrons → (LPL strips
    triglyceride) → **remnants** → liver; the liver exports **VLDL → IDL → LDL** (delivers cholesterol);
    **HDL** runs reverse cholesterol transport. Ch.3 owns the class names, Ch.5 owns assembly; here they
    are shown as an interconversion *pathway*. → `feingoldLipoproteins`

**Part 4 — When the switch breaks (recognition, forward-ref).**

22. **Diabetes = the switch, broken.** Insulin deficiency/resistance breaks fed/fasted integration:
    glucose can't be stored or taken up; in **type 1**, the loss of insulin's brake on lipolysis lets
    fatty-acid release and hepatic ketogenesis run unchecked → **DKA**. Recognition only; management,
    and the type 1 vs type 2 distinction in depth, are **Ch.15**. → `ada2024care`, `statpearlsDKA`
23. **Alcohol on an empty stomach → hypoglycaemia (a clean integration hook).** Ethanol metabolism floods
    the liver with **NADH**, which **blocks gluconeogenesis**; if glycogen is already low (skipped meals),
    the liver can make neither source of glucose → hypoglycaemia. → `krebs1967alcohol`
24. **Recognise counter-regulation in action.** Hypoglycaemia produces **adrenergic/autonomic symptoms
    first** (tremor, sweating, palpitations, hunger — from epinephrine, at a *higher* glucose threshold =
    early warning), then **neuroglycopenic** symptoms (confusion) as the brain is starved. → `mitrakou1991`,
    `diabetologia2022thresholds`

## 6. Singapore context

- **Ramadan is the flagship anchor — the fed→fasted→refed cycle lived daily.** Singapore has a large
  Malay/Muslim community (~14% of residents); the ~13–14 h dawn-to-sunset fast (suhoor → iftar / *buka
  puasa*) is a real, culturally resonant enactment of glycogenolysis → gluconeogenesis → lipolysis and
  glucose sparing. **Honesty caveat (verified):** teach it as *intermittent glycogen depletion and
  repletion* with a **shift toward fat oxidation** — **not** dramatic afternoon ketosis. Lessan & Ali
  2019 documents the carbohydrate→lipid oxidation shift (it reports *no* ketone magnitude); Madkour 2023
  found fasting glucose **remained within the normal range** across the month (it actually rose slightly,
  ~99→108 mg/dL). Deep ketosis is a multi-day-starvation feature. → `lessan2019ramadan`, `madkour2023metabolomics`
- **Ramadan + diabetes = a genuine local clinical issue (recognition + refer).** Fasting on insulin or
  sulfonylureas markedly raises hypoglycaemia risk (EPIDIAR ~7.5× in T2D). Singapore has both a
  **local risk-management tool** (the **FAST algorithm** RCT, Lum et al. 2020) and local data that
  **47% of Muslim T2D patients at SGH had a Ramadan hypoglycaemia**, yet many kept fasting or did not
  self-monitor (Zainudin et al. 2017). Recognition-level: recognise the risk, advise breaking the fast
  for a hypo, refer for pre-Ramadan medication adjustment (management → Ch.15). → `salti2004epidiar`,
  `lum2020fast`, `zainudin2017knowledge`
- **⛔ DO NOT USE Röder et al. 2016 as a "local anchor" (superseded by editor guidance 2026-07-04).** The
  original idea was to surface this A\*STAR-authored insulin/glucagon review "for local relevance." **Editor
  principle:** local research is only appropriate when it studies the **socio-cultural** side of diet OR
  **supports evidence-based clinical practice** — *not* basic/mechanistic metabolism cited merely because it
  is Singaporean. Röder is a basic-physiology review, so it was **deliberately omitted** from the drafted
  chapter. Keep it out. (`roder2016pancreatic` stays in `references.bib` but unused.)
- **The "War on Diabetes" as motivator.** MOH declared it in 2016; roughly **1 in 12 residents** live
  with diabetes and **~1 in 8 among Malays**. Use as a recognition-level "why understanding the fed/
  fasted switch matters" hook; forward-ref Ch.15. → `mohWarOnDiabetes`
- **Intermittent fasting / keto are locally popular — teach the honest state.** TRE weight loss is
  mostly reduced calorie intake and is *not superior* to daily calorie restriction (Liu 2022); the keto
  diet works via appetite/calorie control, not metabolic magic, and **nutritional ketosis is not
  ketoacidosis**. Sets up Ch.13's evidence-literacy work without doing it. → `liu2022tre`, `decabo2019fasting`
- **Everyday integration hooks:** the *overnight-fast* (why fasting blood glucose is normal), *alcohol +
  skipped meals → hypoglycaemia*, the marathoner's *wall*, and the debunked *sugar rush* are all
  relatable, food-first entry points to whole-body metabolism. → `krebs1967alcohol`, `mantantzis2019sugar`

## 7. Pillar intersections

- **nutrition × the chapter's spine:** this is where "you are what you eat" becomes "your body decides,
  meal by meal, what to store and what to burn." It converts Ch.2's energy balance and Ch.3's
  macronutrients into a dynamic, hormone-driven system.
- **nutrition × physical activity:** exercise fuel selection (crossover concept, glycogen depletion, the

- **nutrition × sleep / stress / circadian:** cortisol and the counter-regulatory hormones link the

- **nutrition × substances (alcohol):** alcohol's block on gluconeogenesis is a vivid,
  clinically-relevant intersection — a single behaviour that can crash the fed/fasted safety system.
- **nutrition × clinical medicine / evidence literacy:** diabetes as the broken switch (Ch.15), and the
  honest framing of keto/IF (Ch.13), model "physiology → bedside" and "hype vs evidence."

## 8. Clinical application & case ideas (branching, first-year altitude, integration-focused)

- **Case A — "Why is my blood sugar normal overnight if I haven't eaten?"** A student wearing a CGM sees
  a flat overnight trace. Branches: (1) what holds it up first? → hepatic **glycogenolysis** (hours);
  (2) after glycogen? → **gluconeogenesis** (glycerol, lactate, amino acids); (3) which hormone drives
  it? → **glucagon** (insulin low). Beat: the fed/fasted switch is hormonal, and the brain is protected.
  Bust "you'll crash overnight if you don't eat."
- **Case B — "My keto friend says the brain runs on ketones — true?"** Branches: (1) overnight fast —
  what fuels the brain? → mostly glucose, ketones <5%; (2) after days of fasting/keto-adaptation? →
  ketones supply a large fraction (up to ~60%), but glucose is still *required* (RBCs); (3) is
  keto-ketosis dangerous like a diabetic's? → **no — nutritional ketosis (0.5–3 mmol/L, normal pH) is
  not DKA (10–25 mmol/L, acidosis).** Beat: **ketosis ≠ ketoacidosis**; adaptation is gradual and partial.
- **Case C — "My uncle has diabetes and wants to fast for Ramadan — what's the risk?" (SG anchor)** A
  Malay-Singaporean uncle with T2D on a sulfonylurea + insulin. Branches: (1) main danger of a long dry
  fast on glucose-lowering meds? → **hypoglycaemia** (EPIDIAR ~7.5×); (2) early symptoms to recognise? →
  tremor, sweating, palpitations, hunger (adrenergic) before confusion; (3) push through a hypo to keep
  fasting? → **no — break the fast; recognise and refer** for pre-Ramadan medication adjustment
  (forward-ref Ch.15; local FAST algorithm). Beat: recognition + refer; a real Singapore clinical issue.
- **Case D — "Why did the marathon runner 'hit the wall' at 30 km?"** Branches: (1) what ran out? →
  muscle + liver **glycogen** (~2 h worth); (2) why can't fat just take over at race pace? → fat
  oxidation is too slow for that intensity; (3) how to prevent? → carbohydrate-load + fuel during. Beat:
  glycogen is the limiting store; integration under exercise stress.
- **Case E — "He skipped meals, drank heavily, and collapsed — why?"** A young man, no dinner, heavy
  drinking, found confused and sweaty. Branches: (1) blood glucose? → low; (2) why can't his liver rescue
  him? → glycogen already spent (skipped meals) **and** alcohol's NADH **blocks gluconeogenesis** — both
  glucose sources offline; (3) is this "just being drunk"? → no — **alcohol-induced hypoglycaemia**; give
  glucose. Beat: two integration failures stacked. *(Log candidates in `../cases/case-bank.md`.)*

## 9. Common misconceptions & pitfalls

**Layer A — the pathways:**

- **"Glycolysis needs oxygen."** No — it is cytosolic and O₂-independent; oxygen is used later, at the
  electron transport chain. Under low O₂, pyruvate → lactate regenerates NAD⁺ so glycolysis keeps running.
- **"Gluconeogenesis is glycolysis run backwards."** No — three irreversible steps are bypassed by four
  different enzymes; it's a separately-regulated detour, not a reversal.
- **"Fat can be turned into glucose."** No (net). Fatty-acid carbons become acetyl-CoA, and PDH is
  one-way; only the **glycerol** backbone of a triglyceride is glucogenic. The keystone one-way valve.
- **"ATP is the body's energy store."** No — ATP is a **currency**, spent as fast as it's made (only
  seconds-worth on hand). Energy is *stored* as glycogen and fat.
- **"The TCA cycle only burns carbohydrate."** No — it is the final common pathway for all three fuels,
  and a hub for amino-acid synthesis and gluconeogenesis (amphibolic).
- **"Protein is a fuel store."** No — there is no storage protein; every amino acid is functional tissue,
  so mobilising it is a late, costly response (not a first-line store like glycogen/fat).
- **"You use one pathway at a time."** The real rule is **reciprocal regulation** — opposing pathways are
  coordinated (one up, its opposite down); non-opposing pathways run together.
- **"More pathways means memorising everything."** No — the map is **~5 crossroads and 4 one-way streets**,
  navigable by logic, not recall.

**Layer B — the integration:**

1. **"Insulin is just about blood sugar."** No — it is the master **anabolic** hormone across all three
   macronutrients (stores glucose *and* fat, builds protein).
2. **"Insulin is a fasting hormone / glucagon and insulin both raise blood sugar."** They are
   **reciprocal**: insulin = fed/storage (lowers glucose); glucagon = fasted/mobilising (raises it). The
   *ratio* is the switch.
3. **"The liver needs insulin to take up glucose."** No — hepatic uptake is via GLUT2 (insulin-
   independent); insulin acts on the liver's *enzymes*. Muscle/fat (GLUT4) *do* need insulin.
4. **"Muscle glycogen tops up your blood sugar."** No — muscle lacks glucose-6-phosphatase; its glycogen
   is local-only. It helps blood glucose *indirectly* via the Cori and glucose–alanine cycles.
5. **"When you fast, you burn muscle first."** Fat is the **primary** fuel; protein breakdown occurs
   early but is **relatively spared** and declines as ketosis rises.
6. **"The brain can only use glucose."** In prolonged fasting the brain gets the **majority** of its
   energy from **ketones**; a glucose floor remains (RBCs, some neurons), so gluconeogenesis never stops.
7. **"Fatty acids fuel the brain directly / FFAs simply can't cross the barrier."** Fatty acids are a
   **poor** brain fuel (albumin-bound, oxidised poorly by neurons); the brain's fat-derived fuel is
   **liver-made ketones**, not FFAs.
8. **"The liver runs on the ketones it makes."** It cannot — it lacks **SCOT**; it is a net exporter.
9. **"Ketosis and ketoacidosis are the same / keto diets cause DKA in healthy people."** Nutritional
   ketosis (0.5–3 mmol/L, normal pH) is regulated by residual insulin; DKA (10–25 mmol/L, acidosis) is
   uncontrolled insulin deficiency. Healthy people don't get DKA on keto.
10. **"Protein is the body's third energy store."** Protein is **functional tissue**, not a designed
    reserve; the whole point of ketone adaptation is to *minimise* spending it.
11. **"Fat can be turned into glucose."** Only the **glycerol** backbone can; the **fatty-acid carbons
    cannot** be net-converted to glucose.
12. **"Cortisol and growth hormone are the acute rescuers of low blood sugar."** No — **glucagon and
    epinephrine** act fast; cortisol and GH are slow/permissive (prolonged fasting).
13. **"Alcohol raises blood sugar (it's full of carbs)."** Ethanol metabolism **blocks gluconeogenesis**
    (via NADH); with fasting it causes **hypoglycaemia**, not hyperglycaemia.
14. **"The 'fat-burning zone' is the best way to lose fat."** Lower-intensity exercise burns a higher
    *proportion* of fat but **fewer total calories**; higher intensity burns more total energy (and more
    total fat) per session. Long-term fat loss tracks total energy deficit.
15. **"Intermittent fasting / keto burns fat through special metabolic magic."** Weight loss is mostly
    **reduced calorie intake**; TRE is not superior to calorie restriction; "metabolic switching" benefit
    in humans is unproven.
16. **"Sugar gives you an energy rush / makes kids hyperactive."** Debunked — sugar raises fatigue and
    lowers alertness within an hour; the "hyper kid" effect is expectation/environment.
17. **"Starvation mode permanently wrecks your metabolism."** Adaptive thermogenesis is **modest and
    largely reversible**, not a permanent shutdown.
18. **"First-year metabolism means memorising every pathway and intermediate."** No — recognise the
    fed/fasted switch, the organ fuel matrix, the ketone/protein-sparing story, and the honest evidence;
    the enzyme mechanics are the biochem course's job.
