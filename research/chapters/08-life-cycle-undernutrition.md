## 1. Scope (one paragraph)

This chapter introduces first-year medical students to how nutritional requirements shift predictably across the human life cycle — pregnancy and lactation, infancy, childhood, adolescence, and older age — and to the recognition of undernutrition and malnutrition as clinical entities that are frequently missed. The orientation is awareness and recognition, not therapeutic prescription: students should learn to anticipate life-stage-specific needs (e.g. folate and iron in pregnancy, exclusive breastfeeding in infancy, higher per-kilogram protein and micronutrient density in older adults), to identify populations at risk of undernutrition (frail community-dwelling seniors, the socially isolated, the chronically ill, hospitalised patients), to apply a validated screening tool conceptually (MUST), and to understand that nutrition support is delivered by a multidisciplinary team (MDT) with the dietitian at its centre. Singapore is the primary frame — the Health Promotion Board (HPB) Dietary Guidelines for Older Adults, WHO/HPB breastfeeding guidance, and the locally critical issue of gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM), which affects roughly one in five Singaporean pregnancies — with international guidance (WHO, AfN, ACLM) layered on top. Detailed enteral/parenteral feeding regimens, refeeding-syndrome management, and disease-specific medical nutrition therapy are explicitly signposted as **future learning**.

## 2. Learning objectives (refined)

By the end of this chapter, the first-year student should be able to:

1. **Describe** how nutritional requirements change across the life cycle — pregnancy and lactation, infancy, childhood, adolescence, and older age — and give one or two stage-defining nutrients or feeding principles for each stage (e.g. folate/iron in pregnancy; exclusive breastfeeding 0–6 months; higher protein density and bone-health nutrients in older adults).
2. **Recognise** undernutrition and malnutrition as clinical states, list populations at increased risk, and **explain at an introductory level** how a validated screening tool (MUST) flags risk using BMI, recent unintentional weight loss, and acute-illness effect.
3. **Outline** the concept of nutrition support and the role of the **multidisciplinary team** (dietitian, doctor, nurse, speech therapist, pharmacist), recognising that the dietitian leads detailed assessment and planning.
4. **Identify** common special dietary requirements (e.g. texture-modified diets, allergy/intolerance, religious and cultural diets, GDM diet) and **recognise referral triggers** to a dietitian or specialist — without being expected to design the therapeutic plan.
5. **(Singapore-specific)** Explain why GDM is a high-prevalence local concern, describe the universal screening pathway conceptually, and recognise its implications for mother and child.

*Depth note: objectives 2–4 sit at the "recognise / outline / refer" level. Quantitative feeding calculations, refeeding syndrome, and route selection (oral vs enteral vs parenteral) are flagged as future clinical-years learning.*

## 4. Key concepts to teach (didactic spine)

1. **Nutrition is dynamic, not static** — requirements track physiological state and life stage; "one plate fits all" is wrong. *(frames `hpbHealthyPlate` as a baseline that is then modulated by life stage.)*
2. **Pregnancy & lactation** — increased energy (modest, mainly 2nd/3rd trimester), protein, and several micronutrients; **periconceptional folic acid** to prevent neural tube defects, **iron** for expanded red-cell mass, **iodine**, **calcium/vitamin D**; avoid alcohol, limit high-mercury fish and unpasteurised/high-listeria foods. *(WHO antenatal nutrition; HPB.)*
3. **GDM as a Singapore-defining antenatal issue** — high local prevalence (~18.9% in the GUSTO multi-ethnic birth cohort), **universal** 75 g OGTT screening at 24–28 weeks, diagnostic IADPSG thresholds, and downstream risk (macrosomia, neonatal hypoglycaemia, future maternal T2DM). *(GUSTO/Chong et al.; ACE GDM guidance.)*
4. **Infancy** — **exclusive breastfeeding for ~6 months** (WHO/HPB), then **complementary feeding from ~6 months** (not before 4, not after 6) with continued breastfeeding to 2 years; breast-milk benefits; iron status from ~6 months. *(WHO/HPB; SMJ Singapore feeding consensus.)*
5. **Childhood & adolescence** — growth and bone accrual drive calcium, vitamin D, iron (esp. menstruating adolescent girls), and adequate energy/protein; adolescence as a window for both deficiency and emerging diet-related disease.
6. **Older age** — energy needs fall but **nutrient density must rise**; **higher per-kg protein** (HPB guidance ~1.0–1.2 g/kg/day) to counter sarcopenia and frailty; calcium/vitamin D and B12; appetite, dentition, swallowing, social and economic factors all reduce intake. *(HPB Dietary Guidelines for Older Adults; HPB National Nutrition Survey.)*
7. **Defining malnutrition/undernutrition** — undernutrition = deficiency of energy, protein and/or micronutrients producing measurable adverse effects on body/function; it is common, under-recognised, and not confined to low-income settings (hospitalised and older adults are key groups).
8. **Recognising at-risk populations** — frail/older adults (especially living alone), the socially isolated, those with chronic disease, dysphagia, malabsorption, cancer, or poverty; recent unintentional weight loss and reduced intake are red flags.
9. **Screening as the entry point** — the **MUST** tool conceptually: (a) BMI score, (b) unplanned weight-loss score, (c) acute-disease effect score → low/medium/high risk → action/referral. Screening ≠ assessment.
10. **Nutrition support is a team activity** — the **MDT**: dietitian (detailed assessment, plan), doctor, nurse (screening, monitoring), speech-and-language therapist (swallow), pharmacist; "food first" then oral nutritional supplements, with enteral/parenteral routes as specialist escalation (**future learning**).
11. **Special diets & referral triggers** — texture-modified (dysphagia), allergy/intolerance, religious/cultural (halal, vegetarian/vegan), and condition-specific (GDM) diets; **knowing when to refer** is the first-year competency, not designing the regimen.
12. **Nutrition is under-taught** — students should expect to build on this; recognition + appropriate referral is the safe, evidence-based first-year posture. *(`crowley2019`.)*

## 6. Singapore context

- **HPB Dietary Guidelines for Older Adults (50+):** Singapore's first dietary guidelines aimed specifically at seniors. Core messages: prioritise **nutrient-dense quality over quantity**; **more protein** (HPB guidance targets ~1.0–1.2 g/kg/day to protect against muscle loss); calcium-rich/fortified foods for bone loss; more dark-green and brightly-coloured vegetables; softer textures for chewing/swallowing difficulty; more fluids to counter constipation. HPB "STAR" recipes demonstrate affordable, lower-salt/lower-saturated-fat, protein- and fibre-rich local meals. *(`hpbSeniors`, `mohNNS2022`.)*
- **GDM (locally critical):** The GUSTO multi-ethnic birth cohort (KKH + NUH, recruited 2009–2010) found GDM in **~18.9% of pregnancies** using 1999 WHO diagnostic criteria — among the highest reported in the region. Critically, had only NICE-style high-risk women been tested, **nearly half of GDM cases would have been missed** (overall sensitivity of selective screening ~52%), which is a central argument for **universal screening** in Asian populations. Ethnic variation in GDM risk is well described across Singapore studies, but specific prevalence-by-ethnicity figures depend on cohort and diagnostic criteria and should be cited to their primary source rather than treated as fixed rates. ACE guidance: **universal 75 g OGTT at 24–28 weeks**, IADPSG 2010 thresholds, and **postpartum OGTT follow-up** with subsequent periodic diabetes screening given elevated lifetime T2DM risk — a direct link to the *War on Diabetes* (`mohWarOnDiabetes`). *(`gusto_gdm`, `aceGDM2018`.)*

  *Caveat for teaching: the ~18.9% GUSTO figure was derived using 1999 WHO criteria, not the current IADPSG thresholds — present it as "roughly 1 in 5" and note it is cohort/era/criteria-dependent, not a fixed national rate. IADPSG thresholds (which detect more cases) and 1999 WHO criteria are different diagnostic standards; don't imply the 18.9% was generated by IADPSG.*

- **Infant feeding:** HPB (aligned with WHO) recommends **exclusive breastfeeding for ~6 months**, complementary foods from around 6 months (not before 4, not after 6), continued breastfeeding to 2 years; supported by the Baby-Friendly Hospital Initiative and the 2023/2024 Singapore consensus statements on feeding and eating in infants and young children. *(`whoBreastfeeding`, `smjFeeding2023`.)*
- **National Nutrition Survey 2022** anchors population gaps (low wholegrain intake; ~9/10 exceeding recommended sodium) that compound life-stage vulnerability, especially in older adults. *(`mohNNS2022`.)*
- **Hawker-food framing:** teach using everyday local foods — e.g. for a frail senior, *fortifying* familiar dishes (adding egg/tofu/fish to porridge or mee sua, choosing yong tau foo with more protein items) rather than prescribing unfamiliar "diet foods"; for GDM, lower-GI swaps (brown rice/wholegrain bee hoon, less sweetened drinks — tie to `hpbNutriGrade`).

## 7. Pillar intersections

- **Nutrition × social connection (primary):** Feeding is inherently social. **Older adults living alone** are at high undernutrition risk partly because eating alone reduces appetite, meal frequency, and food variety; bereavement and isolation are red flags. Conversely, communal eating (family meals, community/active-ageing meal programmes, religious/festival meals) supports intake — relevant to both senior nutrition and infant/child feeding contexts (responsive feeding, family modelling of food choices).
- **Nutrition × (future) physical activity:** Protein adequacy in older adults only protects muscle when paired with activity — sets up later sarcopenia/resistance-exercise content (signpost).
- **Nutrition × mental wellbeing / stress:** Appetite and intake are modulated by mood and stress (`dallman2003` as a light touch — note its mindfulness/comfort-food evidence is exploratory) — relevant to both pregnancy and frail elders.

## 8. Clinical application & case ideas (case-player-ready)

### Case A — "Mdm Tan, 82, living alone, losing weight" (undernutrition recognition & referral)

- **Opening:** Mdm Tan attends polyclinic with her daughter who notices her clothes are looser and she "doesn't finish her meals." Lives alone since her husband died last year; mild knee osteoarthritis; recently widowed.
- **Branch 1 — what do you ask/measure?**
  - *Good path:* unintentional weight loss (clothes/scales), appetite, what she actually ate yesterday, who she eats with, chewing/swallowing, mood, finances/mobility to buy food → recognises social isolation as a driver.
  - *Weaker path:* jumps to prescribing a supplement drink without history → feedback: screen and understand cause first.
- **Branch 2 — screening:** Apply **MUST** conceptually: low BMI score + >10% unplanned weight loss score → **high risk**. *(Teaching point: screening triggers action, not a full plan.)*
- **Branch 3 — first action:** "Food first" + social — fortify familiar local foods (egg/fish/tofu in porridge or mee sua), more frequent small meals, involve daughter/meal programme; **refer to dietitian**; consider depression and dentition. Escalation to oral nutritional supplements / enteral feeding = **future learning, dietitian-led**.
- **Learning point surfaced:** undernutrition in a non-poor, developed setting; isolation as a modifiable driver; recognition + referral is the first-year win.

### Case B — "Mrs Devi, 31, 26 weeks pregnant" (pregnancy needs + GDM screening)

- **Opening:** First pregnancy, Indian ethnicity, BMI 27, family history of T2DM, attends routine antenatal visit.
- **Branch 1 — key nutritional needs:** identifies folate (ideally periconceptional — was she on it?), iron, iodine, calcium/vitamin D, adequate but not excessive energy gain; food-safety advice (avoid alcohol, high-mercury fish, unpasteurised foods).
- **Branch 2 — risk recognition:** raised BMI and family history raise pre-test probability of GDM; **but Singapore screens universally**, so all women are offered the OGTT regardless of risk profile (the teaching point is precisely that risk-based selection misses cases).
- **Branch 3 — screening pathway:** **75 g OGTT at 24–28 weeks**; diagnosis if FPG ≥5.1, 1h ≥10.0, or 2h ≥8.5 mmol/L (any one, IADPSG). Result returns positive.
- **Branch 4 — what now (recognition level):** explain implications (macrosomia, neonatal hypoglycaemia, future maternal T2DM → needs **postpartum OGTT follow-up**); first-line is dietary modification (lower-GI local swaps, distributing carbohydrate) with **MDT** (obstetrician, dietitian, diabetes nurse). Insulin titration / detailed glucose targets = **future learning**.
- **Learning point surfaced:** why Singapore screens everyone (GUSTO showed selective screening would have missed ~half of cases); life-cycle nutrition meets the War on Diabetes.

*(Both cases are intentionally recognition-and-referral shaped; the "wrong" branches over-reach into therapeutic territory and get gently corrected. Each is concrete enough to render as a branching case-player scenario: fixed patient facts, 3–4 decision nodes, and good/weak paths with feedback already specified.)*

## 9. Common misconceptions & pitfalls

1. **"Older adults need to eat less of everything."** They need *fewer calories but more nutrient density and more protein per kg* — under-eating protein drives sarcopenia/frailty.
2. **"Eat for two in pregnancy."** Energy needs rise only modestly (mainly later trimesters); *quality* (folate, iron, iodine) matters far more than doubling quantity.
3. **"Malnutrition means looking starved / only happens in poor countries."** Hospitalised and community-dwelling older adults in high-income Singapore are commonly affected and often missed; a normal or high BMI does not exclude it.
4. **"Only thin/low-BMI patients are at risk."** Acute illness and recent unintentional weight loss matter independently of current BMI — hence MUST includes a weight-loss and acute-disease component.
5. **"GDM screening is only for high-risk women."** Singapore screens **universally** because risk-based screening would have missed nearly half of local cases (GUSTO).
6. **"Breastfeeding plus water is fine before 6 months."** Exclusive breastfeeding means no water/other fluids before ~6 months; complementary foods start *around* 6 months (not before 4).
7. **"The doctor sorts out the feeding plan."** Detailed assessment and planning are **dietitian/MDT-led**; the first-year role is to screen, recognise, and refer.
8. **"A supplement drink fixes undernutrition."** "Food first" and addressing the cause (isolation, dentition, mood, swallowing, finances) come before — and alongside — oral supplements.
9. **Overstepping at first-year level:** trying to prescribe enteral regimens, refeeding protocols, or insulin doses — these are explicitly **future learning**.

## 11. Evidence gaps / open questions (residual)

- **GDM prevalence figure provenance (resolved, with caveat):** the ~18.9% figure is from the GUSTO Singapore birth cohort (KKH + NUH, 2009–2010, Chong et al.) using **1999 WHO criteria** — *not* IADPSG. Teach as "roughly 1 in 5" and flag that it is cohort/era/criteria-dependent. Do **not** present it as an IADPSG-derived or current national rate.
- **Ethnic prevalence-by-group numbers (deliberately not asserted):** the earlier draft's "Chinese ~21% / Indian ~22% / Malay ~12%" could not be cleanly verified to the GUSTO 18.9% source (those proportions resemble *cohort composition*, not GDM rates) and have been removed. If the chapter wants per-ethnicity GDM rates, cite a specific primary paper and its diagnostic criteria.
- **ACE GDM edition:** ACE GDM guidance first published 2018, updated 2022; confirm whether a later revision exists before publication and that IADPSG thresholds remain current.
- **HPB Older Adults protein target:** confirm the exact numeric protein target in the current HPB Dietary Guidelines for Older Adults document (the ~1.0–1.2 g/kg/day figure reflects HPB/clinical messaging — cite the guideline document directly when added).
- **Exclusive breastfeeding duration debate:** "~6 months" is the standing WHO/HPB recommendation, but there is ongoing academic debate (allergy/iron windows) — teach the recommendation *with* the caveat (`smjFeeding2023`).
- **MUST vs other tools:** MUST is the teaching exemplar; note other validated tools exist (NRS-2002, MNA for older adults) — flag MNA as the older-adult-specific option for future learning.
- **Local malnutrition prevalence:** the post-discharge recommendations paper reports ~50% of hospitalised patients malnourished at discharge; consider a community/older-adult prevalence figure if one is needed.
- **First-year depth audit:** ensure no drift into refeeding syndrome, EN/PN prescribing, or GDM pharmacotherapy.
