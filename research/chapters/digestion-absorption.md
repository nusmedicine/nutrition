# Digestion and Absorption — research dossier

## 1. Scope (one paragraph)

This chapter follows one meal down the gut and answers, at first-year recognition level, three linked questions: *what happens to food as it travels the tract, how is it broken into absorbable units, and how do those units get into the body?* It covers the alimentary tube (mouth → oesophagus → stomach → small intestine → large intestine) and the four accessory organs (salivary glands, liver, gallbladder, exocrine pancreas); GI motility (peristalsis, segmentation, sphincters, gastric emptying, the migrating motor complex) and approximate transit times; chemical digestion by named enzymes at named sites, converging on the three end-products (monosaccharides; amino acids + di-/tri-peptides; free fatty acids + monoglycerides); the GI hormones **as regulators of secretion and motility only** (gastrin, secretin, CCK, GIP, motilin); the absorptive apparatus (surface-area amplification → villus → enterocyte → four transport modes with SGLT1 as the worked example); nutrient-specific absorption; and — the chapter's owned centrepiece — **bile, mixed micelles, chylomicron assembly, and enterohepatic circulation**. Colonic fermentation gets a brief anatomical mention only. The applied capstone is **oral rehydration therapy (ORT)**: glucose drags sodium, water follows — a bedside proof of the physiology. Singapore-first hooks (near-universal adult lactase non-persistence, hawker-meal fat handling, the gluten-free fad, ORS "science in the sachet") anchor the physiology in lived, food-first experience.

## 2. Learning objectives (recognition-level)

By the end of the chapter a first-year student can:

- **LO-1** *Trace* a meal through the GI tract, *naming* each organ and accessory gland and *stating* its broad job ("store, mix, start digesting"; "makes bile"; "stores/concentrates bile"; "secretes enzymes + bicarbonate").
- **LO-2** *Distinguish* the two core motility patterns (peristalsis vs segmentation), *name* the four sphincters as one-way gates, and *explain* in words why gastric emptying is regulated (fat/volume slow it) and what the migrating motor complex does between meals.
- **LO-3** *Match* each major digestive enzyme to its source, site, and substrate→product, and *explain* the "safety-locked zymogen" concept (pancreatic proteases are made inactive and only switched on once they reach the gut, so the pancreas doesn't digest itself).
- **LO-4** *Describe* how the small intestine's folds/villi/microvilli amplify surface area, and *distinguish conceptually* passive diffusion, facilitated diffusion, and active transport using **SGLT1 (Na⁺–glucose cotransport)** as the worked example.
- **LO-5** *Tell the fat-absorption story end-to-end* (bile emulsifies → mixed micelle ferries → re-esterified → chylomicron → lacteal/lymph) and *explain* why most dietary fat (which is long-chain) is the exception that bypasses the first liver pass.
- **LO-6** *Explain* bile's life cycle and **enterohepatic circulation** (most bile salts reclaimed at the terminal ileum), and *state* the site-specific absorption facts (iron → duodenum; B12+intrinsic factor → terminal ileum; fat-soluble vitamins → with bile), cross-referencing Ch.4.
- **LO-7** *Identify* the five GI hormones (gastrin, secretin, CCK, GIP, motilin) as regulators of secretion/motility — cell of origin → trigger → one-line job.
- **LO-8** *Explain* why oral rehydration therapy works (glucose-coupled sodium absorption keeps working in diarrhoea, and water follows) and why it is regarded as a landmark of 20th-century medicine.

## 4. Key concepts to teach (didactic spine — "follow the food")

**Part 1 — The tube and accessory organs (anatomy spine).**
1. **One continuous tube, mouth to anus.** Everything in the lumen is topologically "outside" the body until absorbed — the unifying idea. → `openstaxAP`, `marieb`
2. **Mouth.** Mechanical breakdown + saliva (~0.5–1.5 L/day) begins starch digestion (salivary amylase) and lubricates the bolus; swallowing = voluntary → involuntary reflex. → `openstaxAP`
3. **Oesophagus.** A conduit; peristalsis propels the bolus; the lower oesophageal (cardiac) sphincter guards the stomach (reflux hook). → `openstaxAP`
4. **Stomach — store, mix, start digesting.** Cardia/fundus/body/pylorus. Parietal cells → HCl (**gastric-content pH ~1.5–3.5** — state as a range) + intrinsic factor; chief cells → pepsinogen (→ pepsin, acid-activated) + gastric lipase; mucous/bicarbonate layer protects the wall; pyloric sphincter meters chyme. (Name cell types at recognition level.) → `openstaxAP`, `statpearlsStomach`
5. **Small intestine — the main event, three segments.** Duodenum (receives bile + pancreatic juice; most chemical digestion; iron absorbed here — x-ref Ch.4), jejunum (bulk nutrient absorption), ileum (B12 + bile-salt reclaim at the terminal ileum — x-ref Ch.4 for B12; own the bile-salt point). Ileocaecal valve guards colon entry. → `openstaxAP`, `marieb`
6. **Large intestine — water/electrolyte reclamation & storage.** Caecum → colon → rectum → anus; reabsorbs water/electrolytes, compacts faeces; internal (involuntary) + external (voluntary) anal sphincters. Colonic microbial fermentation = **brief anatomical mention only** (own it in Ch.7). → `openstaxAP`, `marieb`
7. **Accessory organs — who secretes what, and where.** Salivary glands (parotid/submandibular/sublingual → amylase, lingual lipase, lubrication); liver (makes bile continuously; metabolic hub, but defer fed/fasted to Ch.6 — here "makes bile, receives portal nutrients"); gallbladder (stores/concentrates bile, releases on CCK signal); exocrine pancreas (~1.5 L/day enzyme-rich, bicarbonate-rich juice). → `openstaxAP`, `sherwood`, `pancreapediaHCO3`

**Part 2 — Motility (how the food moves).**
8. **Two core movements.** Peristalsis = propulsive travelling waves; segmentation = non-propulsive mixing (mainly small intestine). *"Peristalsis pushes; segmentation shuffles."* → `openstaxAP`, `sherwood`
9. **Sphincters = one-way gates.** LES, pyloric, ileocaecal, internal/external anal. → `openstaxAP`
10. **Gastric emptying is regulated, not passive.** A mixed meal empties over a few hours; **fat and volume slow it** via duodenal feedback (CCK etc.) — the physiological basis for "meal pacing" and satiety timing (forward-ref Ch.8). → `openstaxAP`, `statpearlsStomach`
11. **The migrating motor complex — the "interdigestive housekeeper."** Between meals, cyclical waves (roughly every 1.5–2 hours; a strong "sweeping" phase) clear residue/bacteria; **motilin** (M cells) triggers the sweep. *"The gut cleans house when you're not eating."* → `deloose2012mmc`, `statpearlsMotilin`, `sherwood`
12. **Approximate transit times (coarse ranges, not exact).** Stomach = hours; small intestine = hours; colon = up to a day or more; whole gut ~1–3 days. Frame as *ranges that vary with diet/fibre/fluid/activity*; fibre→transit forward-refs Ch.7. Avoid implying hard caps (wireless-motility-capsule studies show upper bounds run considerably higher — kept to the source note). → `openstaxAP`, `rao2011transit`

**Part 3 — Chemical digestion (what/where/into-what).**
13. **Digestion is mechanical + chemical; enzymes are scissors specific to one macronutrient.** Only broken-down units are small enough to absorb. → `statpearlsDigestion`
14. **Mouth.** Salivary (α-)amylase begins starch digestion (all salivary glands, not "parotid only"), works near neutral pH, **short-lived — largely stops in the acidic stomach**; lingual lipase (minor in adults) starts fat. → `statpearlsDigestion`, `openstaxAP`
15. **Stomach.** Parietal cells → HCl (denatures protein, activates pepsin, antimicrobial) + intrinsic factor (own the *production site*; B12 absorption site = Ch.4); chief cells → pepsinogen → pepsin (autocatalytic) → peptides. → `statpearlsStomach`, `statpearlsIF`
16. **Duodenum — pancreas + bile arrive.** Pancreatic **bicarbonate** neutralises acid ("the antacid step," secretin-triggered); pancreatic **amylase** (starch → di/oligosaccharides), **lipase + colipase** (triglyceride → FFAs + monoglycerides), and **proteases secreted in an inactive form**. The high-value concept: these protein-cutting enzymes are **safety-locked** and only switched on *once they reach the gut*, not inside the pancreas — otherwise the pancreas would digest itself. *(Educator-only aside: the switch is enteropeptidase on the brush border, which activates trypsin, which then activates the other zymogens — chymotrypsinogen, procarboxypeptidase, proelastase. First-years need only "the gut flips the switch," not the named cascade.)* → `statpearlsDigestion`
17. **Brush border — the final snip, right before absorption.** Disaccharidases (**lactase, sucrase, maltase (+isomaltase)**) → monosaccharides; peptidases → amino acids/small peptides. *"Digestion's last cut happens on the doorstep."* → `statpearlsDigestion`, `openstaxAP`

*Site-by-site spine table (render in-chapter):*

| Site | Cell / secretion | Enzyme(s) | Substrate → product |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mouth | salivary glands | salivary amylase; lingual lipase | starch → maltose/dextrins; fat (minor) |
| Stomach | chief cells | pepsin (from pepsinogen) | protein → peptides |
| Stomach | parietal cells | HCl; intrinsic factor | denature/activate; IF for B12 (Ch.4) |
| Duodenum | pancreas (exocrine) | amylase; lipase+colipase; proteases (secreted inactive, switched on in the gut) + HCO₃⁻ | starch, fat, protein → smaller units |
| Brush border | enterocyte membrane | lactase/sucrase/maltase; peptidases | disaccharides→monosaccharides; peptides→AAs |

**Part 4 — GI hormones as secretion/motility regulators.**
18. **The organising frame.** Enteroendocrine cells are scattered singly through the mucosa (the gut is often called the body's largest endocrine organ). Shared logic: food in one segment triggers a hormone that prepares the *next* segment (feed-forward) or brakes the *previous* one (feedback). Each hormone = cell of origin → trigger → one job. → `statpearlsGIhormones`, `sherwood`

*Core five (render as a table students can reconstruct; triggers kept at the "fat / protein / acid / glucose" grain for parallelism):*

| Hormone | Made by | Trigger | Job (secretion/motility) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Gastrin** | G cells (gastric antrum) | protein in the stomach; distension; vagus | ↑ gastric acid; promotes mixing |
| **Secretin** | S cells (duodenum) | acidic chyme entering the duodenum | ↑ pancreatic/biliary **bicarbonate**; ↓ gastrin/acid |
| **CCK** | I cells (duodenum/jejunum) | fat and protein in the duodenum | ↑ pancreatic **enzymes**; **gallbladder contraction** (bile release); **slows gastric emptying** |
| **GIP** | K cells (duodenum/jejunum) | glucose and fat in the duodenum | **incretin** (signals the pancreas that glucose is coming) |
| **Motilin** | M cells (upper SI) | fasting (between meals) | drives the **MMC** housekeeper sweep |

→ `statpearlsGIhormones`, `statpearlsMotilin`, `sherwood`, `guytonhall`

19. **Three phases of control (recognition only): "ready, digest, brake."** Cephalic (sight/smell/thought → vagus primes acid before food arrives — *the ~30% share is educator-only, no memorised number*), gastric (distension + protein → gastrin → acid; largest phase), intestinal (duodenal feedback braking — secretin/CCK/GIP slow emptying and secretion). → `openstaxAP`, `guytonhall`
20. **The incretin concept (one sentence, forward-ref only).** Oral glucose raises insulin more than the same glucose given intravenously — the gut warns the pancreas that food is coming, and **GIP and GLP-1 carry that signal**. That everyday gut signal is the basis of major modern medicines, but the full story and its drugs live in **Ch.15/Ch.8**. *(No percentages, no glucose-dependence/hypoglycaemia detail here — that is Ch.15.)* → `statpearlsGIhormones`, `nauck2016incretin`
21. **Name-only appetite hormones (→ Ch.8).** Ghrelin (hunger "go"; made in the stomach); GLP-1 and PYY (L cells, distal ileum/colon; satiety "stop," also slow emptying). *"Ghrelin says go, the rest say stop — full story in Ch.8."* → `steinert2017guthormones`

**Part 5 — Absorption and the enterocyte.**
22. **The small intestine is huge *inside*, not outside.** Folds → villi → microvilli amplify surface area "hundreds-fold." **Evidence-honesty upgrade:** the real absorptive area is **~30–40 m² (small intestine ~30 m²) — "about half a badminton court," NOT a tennis court (~260–300 m²)**, per Helander & Fändriks 2014. Teach the corrected number *and* name the myth. Do **not** present a tidy "3×10×20 ≈ 600×" stack (component multipliers vary). → `helander2014`, `lumenSmallIntestine`
23. **The enterocyte is the polarised gatekeeper.** Apical (brush-border) membrane faces the lumen; basolateral faces blood/lymph; absorption = crossing both. Brush border also carries the final digestive enzymes. → `statpearlsAbsorption`
24. **Four ways in, one star.** Passive diffusion (fats, water); facilitated diffusion (fructose via GLUT5; basolateral exit via GLUT2); primary active transport (a pump keeps the sodium gradient charged); **secondary active transport** — that sodium gradient is the battery powering **SGLT1**, which drags glucose/galactose uphill (sodium rides in *with* the sugar). SGLT1 = the star, and the ORT case leans on exactly this "sodium rides in with glucose" idea. → `roder2014`, `statpearlsAbsorption`
25. **Carbohydrate.** Glucose/galactose → SGLT1 (apical, Na⁺-coupled); fructose → GLUT5 (facilitated, no energy); all exit basolaterally via GLUT2 → portal blood → liver. → `roder2014`, `statpearlsAbsorption`
26. **Protein.** Free amino acids via Na⁺-coupled carriers; and **we absorb small peptides too (di-/tri-peptides), via their own carrier** (PepT1) — "not just single amino acids." → portal blood → liver. → `statpearlsAbsorption`
27. **FAT — the owned centrepiece.** Bile salts **emulsify** droplets (surface area for lipase); FFAs + monoglycerides + bile salts + phospholipids form **mixed micelles** that ferry lipids to the brush border; lipids **diffuse in** (the micelle is a shuttle, not absorbed); inside, they are **re-esterified** to triglyceride and packaged with cholesterol and proteins into **chylomicrons** (a lipoprotein — the classes and their apolipoproteins are Ch.3); too big for blood capillaries, chylomicrons exit into the **lacteal** → thoracic duct → blood. **THE story to lead with: dietary fat is mostly long-chain, so it takes the lymph route and bypasses the first liver pass** (why plasma looks milky after a fatty meal). *Footnote/aside only:* short- and medium-chain fatty acids are the small exception — they enter **portal blood directly**. → `joveLipid`, `statpearlsAbsorption`, `medlibretexts_lipid`
28. **BILE & ENTEROHEPATIC CIRCULATION (owned).** Bile is made by the liver from cholesterol, stored/concentrated in the gallbladder, released into the duodenum after a fatty meal (CCK trigger — x-ref hormones). Bile salts are thrifty: **most (~95%) are reabsorbed at the terminal ileum** (active transport) and returned to the liver via the portal vein; the pool (~2–4 g; some texts 3–5 g) recycles **several times a day** (present as a range); the small fraction lost in stool is replaced by hepatic synthesis. Clinical hook: terminal-ileum loss → bile-salt malabsorption + fat malabsorption + diarrhoea, **and the same site handles B12** ("two casualties, one site"). → `chiang2013`, `boyer2013`
29. **Water & electrolytes follow solute osmotically.** The gut receives ~8–9 L/day (diet + secretions); the small intestine absorbs most, and the colon mops up the rest (only ~100 mL lost in stool). Water is **not pumped** — it follows the osmotic gradient set by absorbed Na⁺/nutrients. The mechanistic bridge to ORT. → `statpearlsAbsorption`, `ourworldindataORT`
30. **Site-specific absorption (x-ref Ch.4).** Iron → duodenum; B12 → terminal ileum (needs intrinsic factor); fat-soluble vitamins (A/D/E/K) → with fat/bile/micelles (so fat/bile problems → their deficiency); calcium → duodenum/jejunum (vitamin-D-dependent + paracellular). → `statpearlsB12`, `statpearlsIF`, `odsIron`
31. **THE APPLIED STAR — ORT.** In cholera/rotavirus diarrhoea, secretory pathways dump fluid but **SGLT1 keeps working**. Glucose + sodium (+ water) ride SGLT1 in; water follows osmotically; the child rehydrates by mouth. WHO **reduced-osmolarity ORS = 75 mmol/L Na⁺, 75 mmol/L glucose, total 245 mOsm/L** (WHO/UNICEF adopted 2003, production guidance 2006; lowered from the old 90/111/311 formula because it cut stool ~25% and IV need ~30%). A 1978 *Lancet* editorial called the underlying glucose-enhances-sodium-absorption discovery (Robert Crane, 1960s) "potentially the most important medical advance this century." → `whoORS2006`, `jogh2024ORS`, `ourworldindataORT`, `wangensteen`

## 6. Singapore context

- **Lactase non-persistence is the standout local hook — and now pinned to a primary SG source.** In Singapore-born Chinese adults, **73/77 (95%) were lactase-deficient** by lactose breath-hydrogen testing, yet only **~32% reported symptoms** on ordinary daily milk (`yap1989`). Malaysian multiracial data (a reasonable regional proxy — **label as Malaysian, not SG**) give Chinese 88.5% / Malay 83.1% / Indian 90.5% deficient, only ~20% symptomatic (`goh2018lactase`). Use as the relatable, population-anchored way to teach brush-border digestion and the **deficient ≠ intolerant ≠ allergic** distinction. Note: the SG figure is Chinese-specific — describe as "Singapore Chinese" / "East-Asian-descent," not "all Singaporeans." **Recency caveat:** `yap1989` is 37 years old (n=77) but remains the best population-level SG data; a more recent SG cohort is a genuine gap (§11) — keep it, don't replace. Symptom relief framing: "Drink 3 glasses of milk" advice is culturally naïve; frame calcium via lactose-free milk, yoghurt, hard cheese, calcium-set tofu, fortified soy milk, ikan bilis/small fish with bones, leafy greens (calcium detail = Ch.4). When lactose isn't digested, colonic bacteria ferment it and produce gas — *that is the one-clause boundary; the gas/symptom mechanism is Ch.7.*
- **ORT — "the science in the sachet."** ORS is in every polyclinic and pharmacy, used for acute gastroenteritis/rotavirus/hand-foot-mouth-season diarrhoea in children — the natural Singapore-first, global-health hook for SGLT1. (No local incidence figure verified — keep epidemiology general; see gaps.)
- **Hawker/meal framing.** A heavy oily meal (*char kway teow*, *nasi lemak*, fatty laksa) is a strong CCK/secretin trigger → gallbladder squeeze, enzyme + bicarbonate flood, slowed gastric emptying (why a greasy meal "sits heavy"). Fast eating is culturally common (quick hawker lunches) → tie gastric emptying + the modest chewing/satiety evidence to a *slow down, chew more* nudge (honestly caveated; mechanism → Ch.8).
- **Fibre & transit.** Singapore's low wholegrain intake → practical link to sluggish transit/constipation; keep to "fibre + fluid + activity speed things along," mechanism owned by Ch.7. Local high-fibre swaps: brown/red rice, wholemeal, vegetables, thunder-tea rice. *(Confirm the NNS 2022 "~4% of staples wholegrain" figure against `mohNNS2022` at write-time.)*
- **IBS ≈ 8.6% in Singapore** (Rome II; Gwee 2004) — common and QoL-impacting; cite as order-of-magnitude with explicit criteria-dependence (Rome IV yields lower figures).
- **Gluten-free fad is imported here too** (bread, noodles, roti prata, bakery culture) — anchor the myth-bust locally; classic coeliac is rare in ethnic-Chinese/SE-Asian populations but likely under-diagnosed as wheat intake rises.
- **GLP-1 medicines are highly salient locally.** Semaglutide (Wegovy/Ozempic) and tirzepatide (Mounjaro, dual GIP/GLP-1) are **HSA-registered in Singapore** for T2DM/weight management (private/unsubsidised as of 2026) — makes the incretin *concept* immediately relevant. Forward-ref Ch.15/Ch.8 for how they work; re-cite to an official HSA/MOH source at write-time.
- **Chilli/spicy food myth-bust (light).** Spicy food does **not** cause ulcers or "burn a hole" in a healthy stomach — ulcers are caused by *H. pylori* and NSAIDs (`lanas2017pud`); capsaicin tends to *reduce* acid and is gastroprotective (though it can worsen symptoms in people who already have reflux/ulcers). *(The "capsaicin speeds transit" claim is under-sourced — dropped.)* Relatable myth-busting entry point.

## 7. Pillar intersections

- **nutrition × clinical medicine (ORT):** the flagship — a molecular transporter (SGLT1) directly explains a global life-saving therapy; models "physiology → bedside" and evidence honesty.
- **nutrition × clinical medicine (site matters):** terminal-ileum loss → bile-salt *and* B12 malabsorption ("two casualties, one site"); intrinsic-factor loss → pernicious anaemia (recognition-level links between a gut structure and a downstream deficiency; mechanism/management = Ch.4/clinical years).
- **nutrition × evidence literacy:** the surface-area correction ("even textbook facts get corrected"), the gluten-free fad, "leaky gut"/detox debunking, and honest probiotic/enzyme framing — pairs with Ch.13.
- **nutrition × behaviour:** meal pacing/chewing, meal timing, and the "slow-and-steady" gallstone message (food-first nudges; deeper appetite/energy-balance physiology → Ch.8/Ch.2/Ch.14).

## 8. Clinical application & case ideas (branching, first-year altitude)

- **Case A — "The drip-free rescue" (ORT / SGLT1).** A 2-year-old with 2 days of watery diarrhoea, mildly dehydrated but drinking; parents ask "doesn't she need a drip?" Node 1: why treat by mouth? (target: SGLT1 still works → glucose+salt let sodium in, water follows; distractors: "plain water is enough" → no glucose/Na⁺ to drive SGLT1; "only IV works" → over-medicalising). Node 2: why glucose *and* salt? (glucose is the "handle" sodium rides on). Beat: transporter → therapy → landmark. Signpost SGLT2-inhibitor drugs as a different story (clinical years).
- **Case B — "Milky plasma after a fatty meal" (fat absorption / chylomicrons).** Blood drawn soon after a fatty meal looks faintly milky. Node 1: what is it? (target: chylomicrons via lymph; distractors: cholesterol crystals/infection). Node 2: why lymph, not portal vein? (dietary fat is mostly long-chain → too big for capillaries → lacteals; optional honesty aside: short/medium-chain fats *do* take the portal route — size decides). Beat: bile → micelle → re-esterify → chylomicron → lymph. Forward-ref Ch.6 for chylomicron fate.
- **Case C (micro) — "Two casualties, one site" (terminal ileum).** A patient with terminal-ileum resection develops fat malabsorption/diarrhoea AND B12 deficiency. Recognise that the terminal ileum recycles bile salts *and* absorbs B12. Recognition-level; refer.
- **Case D — "Doctor, I think I'm gluten intolerant."** Branches: (1) go gluten-free now → misses coeliac + loses fibre; (2) dismiss entirely; (3, target) recognise possible coeliac, advise **keep eating gluten and get tested first (tTG-IgA)**, discuss NCGS uncertainty and the fad, food-first fibre. Recognise-don't-manage; no serology-interpretation or gluten-challenge dosing. *(If a lactose-intolerance micro-branch is added here, the interactive must respect the boundary: "colonic bacteria ferment the undigested lactose, producing gas — mechanism in Ch.7," and stop there.)*
- **Case E — Bloating/IBS + "should I take probiotics and enzymes?"** Target: red-flag screen → food-first (soluble fibre titrated + hydration + regularity) → honest probiotic/enzyme evidence (state strain/indication specificity in one sentence; do not reproduce the AGA prescribing table) → refer if alarm features (weight loss, PR bleeding, iron-deficiency anaemia, older onset, family GI-cancer history).
- **Case F (stretch) — "The heavy oily meal that sat like a brick."** Patient describes a fatty hawker meal sitting heavy → identify CCK-mediated slowed gastric emptying + gallbladder/enzyme response (recognition, not management); optional link to the crash-diet/gallstone slow-loss message.

## 9. Common misconceptions & pitfalls

1. **"Most digestion/absorption happens in the stomach."** No — the **small intestine** is the main site of both; the stomach mainly stores, mixes, and starts protein digestion.
2. **"Digestion starts in the stomach."** It starts in the **mouth** (salivary amylase, lingual lipase, mastication).
3. **"The small-intestine surface area is a tennis court (~200–300 m²)."** Overturned — **~30–40 m² total** ("half a badminton court"; Helander 2014). Teach the honest number.
4. **"Everything absorbed goes straight into the blood/liver."** Water-soluble units do (portal vein → liver first), but **long-chain fat → chylomicrons → lymph (lacteals)** first, bypassing the liver; short/medium-chain fat is the small exception (portal).
5. **"Micelles are absorbed."** The micelle is a **ferry** — it delivers FFAs/monoglycerides to the membrane; bile salts stay in the lumen and are recycled downstream.
6. **"Bile is made in the gallbladder."** The **liver** makes it; the gallbladder only stores/concentrates it.
7. **"Bile salts are used up each meal."** **~95% are recycled** (enterohepatic circulation).
8. **"Glucose absorption is passive."** It's **secondary active** (SGLT1, powered indirectly by the sodium pump). Fructose *is* facilitated (GLUT5) — a useful contrast.
9. **"Plain water is the same as ORS."** Plain water lacks the **glucose + sodium** that drive SGLT1; the glucose is the cotransport handle, not calories.
10. **"Vitamin B12 is absorbed in the stomach."** The stomach supplies **intrinsic factor**; absorption is in the **terminal ileum**.
11. **"Gut hormones are mainly about appetite."** Here they are **secretion/motility regulators** (gastrin, secretin, CCK, GIP, motilin); the appetite role is named-and-forward-referenced to Ch.8.
12. **"Secretin increases acid."** The opposite — it drives **bicarbonate** and reduces gastric acid/gastrin. ("GIP = gastric inhibitory peptide" is a historical misnomer; its real job is as an **incretin**.)
13. **"Bigger/fattier meals empty faster."** Opposite — fat and volume *slow* gastric emptying via duodenal feedback.
14. **"Lactase deficiency = lactose intolerance = milk allergy."** Three separate ideas: ~95% of SG Chinese adults are deficient, only ~a third get symptoms, a cup of milk is usually fine, and allergy is a different (protein/immune) entity.
15. **"Gluten-free is healthier for everyone."** No robust benefit without coeliac/wheat allergy (IBS/NCGS are debated exceptions); can be *worse* (fibre/iron/calcium/folate loss, cost). And test for coeliac **before** removing gluten.
16. **"You need a detox/cleanse; leaky gut causes my symptoms and I can supplement it shut."** Liver + kidneys detoxify; "leaky gut syndrome" as a treatable disease is unproven (no gold-standard test); the barrier concept is real, the marketing isn't.
17. **"Enzyme/probiotic supplements fix bloating for everyone."** Little evidence in healthy people; enzymes help only narrow cases (lactase, α-galactosidase/Beano); probiotics are strain- and indication-specific (state in one sentence — the detailed indications are Ch.13/clinical territory, not reproduced here).
18. **"Enzymes work anywhere."** They're pH-sensitive (acid-loving pepsin; pancreatic enzymes need a bicarbonate-neutralised duodenum) — motivates the antacid step.
19. **"You must chew each bite 32 times."** The chewing–satiety link is real but **modest and inconsistent** — a gentle nudge, not a rule.
20. **"First-year means memorising every transporter, enzyme, and number."** No — recognise the enterocyte, the four modes with SGLT1 as star, the fat/bile story; exact kinetics/stoichiometry are later.
