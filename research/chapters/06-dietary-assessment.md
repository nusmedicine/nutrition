# Dietary Assessment, Food Labels and Talking to Patients — research dossier

## 1. Scope (one paragraph)
This chapter is the practical "entry point" of the nutrition strand: it equips a first-year student to *find out what a patient actually eats*, *read what is on a label*, and *open a respectful conversation about food* — safely. Having met dietary patterns and the supporting evidence in earlier chapters (where the headline trials — PREDIMED, DASH, DPP, Look AHEAD, DiRECT — live, each with its caveats), the student now learns the bedside-and-clinic skills that make that knowledge usable: a structured but rapid diet history, a working knowledge of food labels and Singapore's Nutri-Grade mark, the *concept* (not yet the full execution) of anthropometric and malnutrition screening, and the opening steps of a brief behaviour-change conversation. Crucially, it threads counselling earlier than most curricula do — students practise a safe opener now rather than waiting — and it builds in a do-no-harm reflex: recognising disordered eating and knowing when food-focused advice could cause harm. The aim is competent *first contact*, not specialist assessment.

## 2. Learning objectives
By the end, a first-year student can…
- [ ] **LO1.** Take a basic, structured diet history and perform a rapid dietary-intake screen, choosing an appropriate method (24-h recall, food-frequency screen, or food diary) and naming each method's main bias.
- [ ] **LO2.** Interpret a packaged-food Nutrition Information Panel and the Singapore Nutri-Grade mark, including the A–D sugar thresholds, and use them to guide a practical "swap."
- [ ] **LO3.** Use a diet history plus simple anthropometry/screening concepts to flag likely nutrients of *deficit* and *excess*, and recognise when a validated malnutrition-screening tool (e.g. MUST) is indicated — *referring on rather than scoring fully at this stage.*
- [ ] **LO4.** Open a respectful, patient-centred food conversation using the opening steps of a brief-counselling frame (assess what the patient eats and is ready for, then advise with permission) — avoiding judgement and jargon.
- [ ] **LO5.** Screen for and respond safely to possible disordered eating, and articulate the do-no-harm caveat: when weight- or restriction-focused advice may be contraindicated and warrants referral.

## 3. Scope boundaries
- **In:** structured diet history + rapid screeners; reading the Singapore Nutrition Information Panel and Nutri-Grade mark; A–D thresholds; the *concept* of anthropometry (BMI with Asian cut-offs, waist circumference) and malnutrition screening (MUST as the exemplar); identifying broad nutrients of likely deficit/excess from a history; the opening steps of brief counselling (assess → advise, with permission) introduced early; basic disordered-eating awareness and do-no-harm caveat.
- **Out (covered elsewhere):** full dietary-pattern evidence and the headline trials — PREDIMED, DASH, DPP, Look AHEAD, DiRECT — with their caveats (earlier nutrition chapters; *signposted here, not re-argued*); the QQH/*My Healthy Plate* model as a teaching anchor (earlier chapters); detailed micronutrient biochemistry and deficiency syndromes (basic-science / clinical chapters); full motivational interviewing, Stages-of-Change, goal-setting and the complete 5 A's cycle (behaviour-change chapter); formal nutrition-focused physical examination and clinical malnutrition management/refeeding (later/clinical years); eating-disorder *diagnosis and treatment* (psychiatry); diabetes/lipid pharmacotherapy (signposted only — `aceT2DM2023`, `aceLipid2023`).

## 4. Key concepts to teach (didactic spine)
1. **Why assessment comes first.** You cannot counsel what you have not assessed; dietary assessment is the shared entry point for every lifestyle pillar (and for alcohol/substance history). Graduates are consistently under-prepared in clinical nutrition — this chapter is the corrective (`crowley2019`, `aclm2022comp`, `afn2021curriculum`).
2. **Methods of diet assessment and their biases.** 24-h recall (quick, but a single day ≠ habitual intake; recall bias); food-frequency / rapid screeners (capture habitual pattern, less precise); food diary / weighed record (richest, but burden + reactivity + social-desirability under-reporting of "unhealthy" foods). First-years pick *fit-for-purpose*, not gold-standard (`measurementToolkitDiet`).
3. **A practical, structured diet-history skeleton.** Typical day (wake→sleep), meal pattern, drinks (very high yield locally — sugary beverages), snacks, eating-out/hawker frequency, who cooks/buys, portion cues, supplements, and red-flag questions. Keep it conversational, not interrogative.
4. **Reading a food label.** The Singapore Nutrition Information Panel: serving size vs per-100 g/ml, energy, sugar, sodium, saturated/trans fat; "per serving" traps and serving-size manipulation; ingredient-list ordering. Read patterns over single nutrients (`aha2021diet`).
5. **Nutri-Grade A–D.** Mandatory front-of-pack mark for beverages (pre-packaged from Dec 2022; freshly prepared incl. hawker/bubble-tea from 30 Dec 2023). Graded on **sugar** then downgraded (never upgraded) on **saturated fat** per 100 ml. Sugar bands: **A ≤1 g, B >1–5 g, C >5–10 g, D >10 g per 100 ml**. **C and D must display the mark; D cannot be advertised.** Teach it as a *nudge to swap*, not a verdict (`hpbNutriGrade`). *(Note: the A/B saturated-fat ceiling, often quoted as ≤1.2 g/100 ml, is flagged for primary-source re-verification — see §11.)*
6. **Anthropometry & malnutrition screening — the concept.** BMI with **Asian cut-offs (≥23 overweight, ≥27.5 obese)** and waist circumference for cardiometabolic risk (`sgObesityBMI`); the *other* tail — under-nutrition — and that a validated tool such as **MUST** (BMI + unintentional weight loss + acute-disease effect) flags risk and triggers referral. First-years *recognise indications*; they do not yet manage (`bapenMUST`).
7. **From history to nutrients of deficit/excess.** Linking pattern to likely surplus (sodium, free sugars, saturated fat — locally near-universal) and likely shortfall (wholegrains, fibre, fruit/veg, calcium/iron in some groups), anchored to local survey data (`mohNNS2022`, `who2020diet`).
8. **Talking to patients — the early counselling primer.** Why thread it now: skills need rehearsal. Introduce the **5 A's** frame (Assess, Advise, Agree, Assist, Arrange) as the structuring scaffold widely recommended for brief primary-care behaviour counselling; first-years practise only the opening moves — **assess** what the patient eats and is ready to change, then **advise** one concrete step *with permission*, in plain language and non-judgementally (`fiveAsNutrition`). Brief MI-style counselling is learnable and improves dietary adherence (`frost2018mi`); the full 5 A's cycle, MI and Stages-of-Change are deferred to the behaviour-change chapter (`mastellos2014ttm`).
9. **Do no harm: disordered-eating awareness.** Food, weight and "clean eating" talk can trigger or worsen disordered eating. Recognise red flags; know the **SCOFF** screen and its limits (sensitive but not diagnostic — raises suspicion only; a negative does not exclude); avoid weight-/restriction-centric advice when an eating disorder is possible; refer (`scoffMorgan1999`, `kutzScoff2020`).

## 6. Singapore context
- **Nutri-Grade is the local hook.** Uniquely, since **30 December 2023** the mark applies to **freshly prepared** drinks — *kopi/teh*, *bandung*, sugarcane juice, and especially **bubble tea** — sold in F&B outlets, hawker stalls, workplaces, schools and healthcare settings, not just supermarket cartons. A typical sweet bubble tea sits at **C or D**; *kopi-o kosong* or *teh-c siu dai* moves down the scale. This makes "grade your usual drink, then pick one swap" an authentic, locally specific exercise (`hpbNutriGrade`). The local RCT supports the mechanism but with a realistic ceiling: the label shifted purchases toward less sugar, yet did not by itself improve overall diet quality (`nutriGradeRCT2023`) — useful for teaching that a label is a nudge, not a cure.
- **Epidemiology to anchor history-taking.** National Nutrition Survey 2022: only ~4% meet the wholegrain-staples recommendation and ~9 in 10 exceed sodium limits — so a Singapore diet history should *expect* high sodium (instant noodles, sauces, soup bases) and refined staples, and probe sugary drinks specifically (`mohNNS2022`).
- **HPB/MOH instruments to reference:** *My Healthy Plate*/QQH as the counselling anchor for "Advise" (`hpbHealthyPlate`); Healthier Choice Symbol and Nutri-Grade as label tools; Asian BMI cut-offs in Healthier SG preventive protocols (`sgObesityBMI`); War on Diabetes framing for why early dietary screening matters and which groups carry higher risk (`mohWarOnDiabetes`).
- **Hawker-food examples for teaching:** chicken rice (oily rice, skin, chilli-sweet sauce), *char kway teow* (sat fat), *laksa* (sodium + coconut), economy rice as a *teachable swap canvas* (more veg, less gravy, smaller rice). Use these for portion and "small swap" practice rather than prohibition.

## 7. Pillar intersections
- **Assessment is the shared entry point for ALL pillars** — the same conversational history that surfaces diet also surfaces **physical activity, sleep, stress and substance use (including a routine alcohol history)** and **social connection** (who shops/cooks, eating alone). Teach the diet history as one branch of a single lifestyle history so students reuse the skill everywhere.
- **Alcohol/substances:** the drinks history naturally folds in alcohol (units, frequency) — model a non-judgemental ask here.
- **Stress & sleep:** comfort-eating and short-sleep appetite effects can be flagged in the same history (`dallman2003`, `sleepRestriction`) — note as cross-links, don't deep-dive.
- **Behaviour change:** this chapter *seeds* the counselling pillar (the assess/advise opening moves) that the dedicated chapter completes.

## 8. Clinical application & case ideas
**Case A — OSCE-style diet-history station: "Weight gain & bubble tea."**
- *Stem:* 24-y-o office worker, BMI 26 (overweight by Asian cut-off), gained 5 kg in a year, asks for "a diet."
- *Branch 1 (method choice):* student elects a 24-h recall vs asks about a typical week → feedback tags the bias they introduced (single-day vs habitual).
- *Branch 2 (drinks probe):* if the student forgets to ask about beverages, the SP reveals 2 bubble teas/day only on direct questioning → teaches that the highest-yield question is often the one not asked.
- *Branch 3 (counselling opener):* student must ask permission ("Would it be OK to talk about your drinks?") then advise one concrete swap. Judgemental or jargon-laden openers lose points.
- *Branch 4 (safety fork):* SP mentions skipping meals + guilt after eating → student should pivot to gentle SCOFF-style questions and **not** prescribe restriction; correct path = supportive screen + referral consideration.

**Case B — "Grade the drink, counsel the swap."**
- Student is given three real Singapore beverages (sweet bubble tea, *teh-c*, packaged isotonic drink), assigns a Nutri-Grade A–D from sugar per 100 ml, then runs a 60-second swap conversation.
- *Branches:* patient pushback ("but I need energy") → student reframes without lecturing; patient reveals reduced intake/unintentional weight loss → student recognises a *malnutrition* signal (wrong tail) and switches from "cut down" to "screen/refer" (MUST concept).

**Case C — do-no-harm vignette (short):** a thin, high-achieving student-athlete asks how to "eat cleaner and lose more weight." Correct play: recognise possible disordered eating, avoid reinforcing restriction, screen gently, signpost support.

## 9. Common misconceptions & pitfalls
- **"A single 24-h recall = their usual diet."** No — one day ≠ habitual intake; name the bias.
- **"People report their diet accurately."** Under-reporting of "unhealthy" foods and portions is the norm; ask non-judgementally to reduce it.
- **"Per serving = the whole pack."** Serving sizes can be small or manipulated; always check per-100 g/ml.
- **"Nutri-Grade A = healthy / D = bad food."** It grades *beverages on sugar & saturated fat only* — not overall nutrition, and not solid foods. It is a nudge, not a moral label; even good labels don't fix overall diet quality on their own (`nutriGradeRCT2023`).
- **"Lower number on the scale is always the goal."** Some patients are *under*-nourished; weight-loss framing can harm. Always consider both tails.
- **"BMI thresholds are universal."** Use **Asian cut-offs** locally; BMI also misses body composition.
- **"Counselling = telling people what to do."** Advice without permission and without listening backfires; assess and ask before you advise.
- **"Screening tools diagnose."** A positive SCOFF raises suspicion; a negative does **not** exclude disordered eating.
- **"Eating-disorder screening is for psychiatry only / can wait."** Do-no-harm screening belongs at first contact — before any restriction advice.

## 10. Proposed interactive elements
- **Quiz topics:** match assessment method ↔ its main bias; assign a Nutri-Grade from a sugar value; read a tricky NIP (per-serving trap); pick the safest counselling opener; spot the disordered-eating red flag and choose the safe next step; identify which tail of malnutrition a vignette shows.
- **Diagram(s):** (a) the diet-history skeleton as a labelled "day-in-the-life" timeline with high-yield probes; (b) a Nutri-Grade A–D sugar-threshold ladder with local drink exemplars; (c) a simple "assess → advise (with permission) → [agree / assist / arrange or refer]" flow that also forks to the do-no-harm/SCOFF branch.
