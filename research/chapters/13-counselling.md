# Behaviour-Change Counselling for Dietary Change — research dossier

## 1. Scope (one paragraph)

This chapter equips first-year students with the foundational counselling skills to help a patient move from "I should eat better" to a single, concrete, agreed dietary change. It teaches a structured, empathic brief consult built on four interlocking tools: the **5As** scaffold (a sequence to organise the conversation), **introductory motivational interviewing (MI)** (a conversational style to elicit the patient's own reasons for change), **SMART goal-setting** (a way to convert a vague intention into a checkable plan), and the **Transtheoretical / Stages-of-Change (TTM/SOC) model** (a heuristic for tailoring how — not whether — to advise, taught explicitly *with* its evidence limits). Cultural tailoring is threaded throughout using Singapore hawker-food and beverage examples (kopi/teh, char kway teow, economy rice). The chapter is deliberately bounded to a brief, respectful clinic interaction and repeatedly reinforces a **disordered-eating do-no-harm caveat**: counselling about food can cause harm if it tips a patient toward restriction, weight preoccupation, or food moralising, and students must learn to recognise red flags and refer rather than press. Specialist behaviour-change therapy, intensive multi-session programmes, and eating-disorder management are signposted as future learning.

## 2. Learning objectives (refined)

1. **Apply the 5As brief-counselling scaffold** (commonly taught as Ask, Advise, Assess, Assist, Arrange; note the canonical USPSTF ordering is Assess, Advise, Agree, Assist, Arrange) to a simple dietary-change scenario, obtaining permission before advising and finishing by arranging follow-up.
2. **Demonstrate three introductory MI skills** — open questions, reflective listening, and affirmations (OARS minus Summary at first-year depth) — to elicit and gently strengthen *change talk* rather than argue for change.
3. **Convert a vague intention into one SMART dietary goal** (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) co-written *with* the patient, recognising that goal-setting is a helpful tool, not a guarantee of behaviour change.
4. **Use the Stages-of-Change model to tailor the *style* of advice** to a patient's readiness, while stating its evidence limitation: SOC is a useful organising heuristic but does **not** reliably predict sustained weight loss.
5. **Recognise disordered-eating red flags and weight-stigmatising language**, and respond with a do-no-harm approach: normalise eating, avoid "good/bad food" framing, and refer when concerned.

## 3. Scope boundaries

**In:**
- The 5As as a brief-consult scaffold for one dietary change.
- MI *spirit* (partnership, acceptance, compassion, evocation) and 3–4 core micro-skills; the concept of change talk vs. sustain talk.
- Writing one SMART goal collaboratively; the idea of an if-then "implementation intention."
- TTM/SOC stages (precontemplation → contemplation → preparation → action → maintenance) used *only* to tailor tone, taught with humility about its weak outcome evidence.
- Cultural tailoring to Singapore food culture; enlisting household/peers.
- Disordered-eating do-no-harm: red-flag recognition, non-stigmatising language, when to refer.

**Out (covered elsewhere):**
- Full MI training, the four MI processes in depth, and competency assessment → future/clinical-years learning.
- Intensive multi-session lifestyle programmes (e.g., DPP-style 16-session curricula) → Ch. on diabetes prevention / lifestyle medicine intensity. (Note: the strong dietary-outcome evidence — including the USPSTF behavioural-counselling base — comes from *intensive* multi-contact interventions, not a single brief visit; see §5 and §11.)
- Pharmacotherapy and bariatric/metabolic surgery for obesity → MOH ACE / specialist chapters (signpost only).
- Diagnosis and management of eating disorders → mental health / specialist referral (this chapter teaches *recognise and refer* only).
- The nutritional *content* of a healthy diet (what to recommend) → My Healthy Plate / dietary-patterns chapters; here we focus on *how to help someone change*.

## 4. Key concepts to teach (didactic spine)

1. **Why counselling skills matter as much as nutrition knowledge.** Knowing the right diet does not make patients change; clinicians are frequently under-prepared to translate knowledge into behaviour [crowley2019]. The clinician's job in a brief consult is to start a change, not finish it.
2. **The 5As scaffold as the backbone of a brief consult.** Ask (permission + current behaviour), Advise (clear, personalised, with permission), Assess (readiness/confidence), Assist (problem-solve + co-set a goal), Arrange (follow-up/referral). The 5As is a widely used and recommended structure for brief lifestyle counselling; note honestly that the bodies endorsing it (e.g., USPSTF) base their *effectiveness* estimates on **intensive, multi-contact** delivery rather than a single visit [usptfBehavioural; frost2018mi].
3. **MI spirit and change talk.** A collaborative, non-confrontational style that *evokes* the patient's own motivation improves dietary adherence and is learnable and transferable across behaviours, with small-to-moderate effect sizes [frost2018mi]. Teach OARS micro-skills and listening for change talk; warn against the "righting reflex" (rushing to fix/lecture).
4. **From intention to SMART goal.** Specific, actionable goals show promise for dietary change relative to vague advice, though the goal-setting literature has acknowledged methodological limits [shilts2004goalsetting]; pairing a goal with an if-then plan ("implementation intention") has a medium-to-large effect on goal attainment (d≈0.65) [gollwitzer2006implementation]. Caveat: SMART is a useful tool, not proven superior to other goal frameworks, and over-reliance on the acronym is a known pitfall.
5. **Stages of Change — a heuristic, taught with humility.** Use the stage to choose *how* to talk (raise awareness in precontemplation; build confidence in preparation), but state plainly that TTM/SOC has limited, low-certainty evidence for producing sustainable weight loss [mastellos2014ttm].
6. **Cultural tailoring.** Effective counselling meets the patient's real food environment — hawker centres, communal dishes, kopitiam drinks — and works *within* it rather than prescribing alien foods [aclm2022comp; lianov2010].
7. **Disordered-eating do-no-harm (reinforced).** Dietary counselling can trigger restriction, weight preoccupation, and food moralising; this demands non-stigmatising language and red-flag vigilance. Importantly, the best current evidence is reassuring on average — structured behavioural weight management does **not** increase eating-disorder symptoms for most adults (a modest *reduction* is typically seen), but a small minority (roughly 0–6.5% in trials) develop new symptoms, so monitoring and a low threshold to refer remain essential [jebeile2023edrisk; canPaeds2025dieting].

## 6. Singapore context

- **HPB–MOH Clinical Practice Guidelines** are the verifiable local anchor for *how* brief counselling is structured in Singapore primary care. The HPB–MOH CPG on *Treating Tobacco Use and Dependence* (and the companion *Obesity* CPG) operationalise the **5As brief-intervention model** and motivational-interviewing-style engagement for Singapore health professionals [hpbMohCPG]. This lets us teach the 5As as endorsed Singapore practice. (Honest note: these CPGs were developed for tobacco and obesity; we extend the *same* scaffold to a general dietary-change consult. A separately named "HPB Health Choices Toolkit" could not be independently verified this session — see §11/§12; do not cite it until confirmed.)
- **HPB health-coaching / digital coaching and community programmes** apply MI and health-coaching principles to help Singaporeans build and sustain habits — consistent with MI being a nationally used counselling style. (Treat as practice context, not as an outcomes citation.)
- **My Healthy Plate (QQH)** as the *content* of "Advise" — quarter wholegrains, quarter protein, half fruit & vegetables — gives a culturally neutral, visual target patients already recognise [hpbHealthyPlate].
- **Nutri-Grade beverages** as a ready-made SMART-goal lever: "switch my Nutri-Grade D kopi to a C or order *kosong*/*siu dai*" is specific, measurable, and locally meaningful [hpbNutriGrade].
- **Local epidemiology to motivate (not shame):** National Nutrition Survey 2022 shows ~9 in 10 residents exceed sodium recommendations and wholegrain intake is very low [mohNNS2022]; the **War on Diabetes** frames why small dietary shifts matter, and notes higher T2DM risk among Malay and Indian residents — a cue to tailor culturally and avoid one-size-fits-all advice [mohWarOnDiabetes].
- **Hawker-food examples** (for "Assist" and SMART goals): *siu dai*/*kosong* kopi-teh; choosing *thosai* or *yong tau foo* (clear soup) over fried options; sharing one plate of char kway teow; adding a side of vegetables to economy rice; swapping a sugary bandung for plain water or kopi-o-kosong. These work *within* hawker culture rather than telling patients to stop eating out.

## 7. Pillar intersections

- **nutrition × social connection** — Dietary change rarely happens alone. Teach students to *enlist the household and peers* (who cooks? who orders the shared dishes? who joins the kopitiam run?) and to *anticipate sabotage* ("auntie insists you finish the rice"; colleagues pressuring a second teh). A goal that fits the family meal and the social ritual survives; one that fights it fails.
- **skills transfer across all pillars** — The 5As + MI + SMART scaffold is *behaviour-agnostic*. Make explicit that the identical conversation structure applies to physical activity, sleep, alcohol, stress, and tobacco — students are learning one transferable counselling engine, used here on diet but reusable in every lifestyle-medicine pillar [frost2018mi; aclm2022comp].

## 8. Clinical application & case ideas (case-player-ready branching scenarios)

### Case A — "The two kopi gao a day" (MI + SMART goal to cut sugary kopi/teh)
**Setup:** Mr Tan, 52, taxi driver, HbA1c borderline, drinks 2–3 sweetened *kopi gao* and a packet drink daily. He's not asking to change.
**Branch 1 — Ask (permission):** Player offers an open question + asks permission ("Would it be OK to talk about your drinks?") → patient engages.
- *Trap branch:* Player launches into "You must stop sugar" (righting reflex) → patient defensive ("It's my only pleasure driving all day") → score down; coaching note on the righting reflex.
**Branch 2 — evoke change talk:** Reflective listening ("Sounds like the kopi keeps you going, *and* the sugar worries you a bit") elicits change talk ("My father had diabetes…") → player reinforces with an affirmation.
**Branch 3 — Assess readiness/confidence:** "On 0–10, how ready / how confident?" Patient says ready 7, confident 4 → player explores the confidence gap (not the readiness).
**Branch 4 — Assist → SMART goal:** Co-write: *"Order kopi-**siu dai** instead of kopi gao for my morning cup, every workday, starting Monday"* (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) + if-then plan ("If the stall auntie forgets, I'll remind her *siu dai*").
- *Trap branch:* Player imposes "Cut out all kopi" → unrealistic, patient disengages → coaching note on Achievable.
**Branch 5 — Arrange:** Agree a 2-week check-in / link to HPB resources.
**Learning payoff:** one small, owned, locally framed change beats a lecture.

### Case B — "How much char kway teow is too much?" (5As for ambivalence about hawker portion sizes)
**Setup:** Ms Lim, 38, BMI 27 (≥27.5 is the Asian obese cut-off; 27 sits in the Asian "obese I"/high-overweight band — date and band per the 2016 HPB-MOH CPG) [sgObesityBMI], eats large hawker portions, ambivalent: "I know I overeat but the food is so good."
**Branch 1 — Ask & Assess stage:** Player identifies *contemplation* (ambivalent, not yet planning) → tailors *style* to raise confidence and tip the balance, **not** to hand over an action plan she's not ready for. (Teaching beat: SOC used as a tailoring heuristic — and the case explicitly flags its evidence limits in a coaching note [mastellos2014ttm].)
**Branch 2 — Advise with permission + My Healthy Plate:** Visual QQH framing ("half the plate veg") rather than "eat less."
**Branch 3 — Assist (portion strategies within hawker culture):** Share one plate; "*less oil/less noodles*" requests; add *xiao bai cai*; order a clear-soup option some days — keeping her in the hawker centre, not out of it.
**Branch 4 — DO-NO-HARM gate (mandatory):** If the player's language moralises food ("char kway teow is *bad*", "you've let yourself go") **or** the patient discloses skipping meals to "make up" for eating / preoccupation with weight → scenario routes to a *red-flag* branch: player must switch to non-stigmatising, weight-neutral language ("foods aren't good or bad; let's find a sustainable balance") and, if disordered-eating signs are present, **pause weight/portion advice and refer** rather than press a goal [canPaeds2025dieting; jebeile2023edrisk]. Choosing to push a weight-loss goal here scores down with a safety coaching note.
**Branch 5 — Arrange:** Offer follow-up, normalise that ambivalence is OK and change can be revisited.

## 9. Common misconceptions & pitfalls

- **"More information changes behaviour."** Knowledge alone rarely shifts habits; evoking the patient's own motivation does more [frost2018mi; crowley2019].
- **The righting reflex.** Jumping to fix/correct/lecture provokes resistance; MI's value is in *resisting* this urge.
- **Advising without permission.** Skipping "Ask" and "may I share…?" undermines partnership.
- **Treating Stages-of-Change as fact/prediction.** SOC is a heuristic for *tailoring*, not a validated predictor of sustained weight loss; presenting it as proven overstates the evidence [mastellos2014ttm].
- **"A brief clinic visit will fix this."** The strongest counselling outcomes (including USPSTF's evidence base) come from *intensive, multi-contact* programmes; a single 5As consult realistically *starts* change rather than delivering it [usptfBehavioural].
- **SMART-goal worship.** The acronym is a scaffold, not magic; an over-engineered goal the patient didn't choose won't stick. Specificity helps; ownership matters more [shilts2004goalsetting].
- **Setting too many / too-big goals.** One small, achievable change beats five aspirational ones.
- **Food moralising ("good vs bad foods").** Dichotomous framing fuels restriction and disordered eating — avoid it [canPaeds2025dieting].
- **Weight stigma.** Weight-focused, shaming language harms rapport and health; weight alone does not equal health status.
- **Missing disordered-eating red flags.** Although structured weight management does not raise ED symptoms for most patients, a minority are affected; failing to screen and refer when red flags appear is a do-no-harm failure [jebeile2023edrisk; canPaeds2025dieting].
- **Counselling as a one-off.** Without "Arrange" (follow-up/referral), brief advice rarely sustains change.

## 10. Proposed interactive elements

- **Quiz topics:** (a) Order the 5As steps and match a clinician utterance to each; (b) classify utterances as *change talk* vs *sustain talk*; (c) spot the righting reflex in a transcript; (d) fix a "weak" goal into a SMART one (kopi/teh example); (e) given a patient quote, identify the Stage of Change *and* state one limitation of the model; (f) red-flag recognition: pick the response that is non-stigmatising and triggers referral.
- **Diagram idea(s):** (1) A single **"5As wheel"** with MI spirit at the hub and SMART goal sitting inside "Assist," showing the tools nest together rather than compete. (2) A small **readiness ruler (0–10)** graphic with a "confidence vs readiness" two-axis cue.
- **Case:** Interactive branching **Case A (kopi/teh MI → SMART goal)** as the primary case-player scenario, with the **do-no-harm safety branch** from Case B embedded as a mandatory gate so every student encounters the refer-don't-press decision.

## 11. Evidence gaps / open questions

- **Brief vs intensive dosing:** Strong dietary outcomes come largely from *intensive* multi-session programmes; the USPSTF evidence base itself rests on median ~12 contacts. The marginal effect of a single brief 5As clinic consult on hard dietary outcomes is modestly evidenced and under-studied at the first-visit level — teach the brief consult as *initiating* change.
- **HPB "Health Choices Toolkit" (UNVERIFIED):** A specifically named "HPB Health Choices Toolkit" describing a 3As/5As + MI structure could not be independently verified this session. The verifiable Singapore anchors are the **HPB–MOH Clinical Practice Guidelines** (tobacco; obesity), which do operationalise the 5As. **Action: confirm the exact HPB resource name/URL or cite the CPGs only.**
- **TTM/SOC validity:** Whether stage-tailoring adds value over non-staged counselling remains unresolved; Cochrane evidence is low-certainty [mastellos2014ttm].
- **SMART goals head-to-head:** Limited evidence that SMART specifically outperforms other goal frameworks; "best" goal structure is unsettled [shilts2004goalsetting].
- **Eating-disorder risk magnitude/direction:** Current meta-analytic evidence is *reassuring on average* (no increase, modest reduction) but identifies a vulnerable minority [jebeile2023edrisk]; magnitude and moderators in *primary-care brief-counselling* settings (vs structured programmes) remain under-quantified. Safest first-year stance: conservative vigilance and referral.
- **`canPaeds2025dieting` year/edition:** The CPS *Dieting in adolescence* statement is real; recent media coverage (2025) suggests a current revision, while an earlier version predates it. **Confirm the exact statement year/edition before final BibTeX.**
- **Singapore-specific outcome data:** Little published outcome data on MI/5As delivered in Singapore hawker-food contexts; local effectiveness is an open question.
- **Cultural tailoring efficacy:** Evidence that culturally tailored dietary counselling improves adherence in multi-ethnic Singapore specifically is thin — flagged as a research opportunity.
- **Headline-trial caveats (not currently cited in body):** This chapter does not lean on PREDIMED/DPP/DiRECT. If any are added later, carry their caveats — PREDIMED (2018 re-analysis; ~30% *relative* RR on a composite, stroke-dominated) [estruch2018predimed]; DPP (58% reduction from an *intensive 16-session* programme, not a clinic visit) [dpp2002]; DiRECT (46% remission only in *early ≤6y, non-insulin, BMI 27–45* patients) [lean2018direct].
