# Bone Health & Osteoporosis — research dossier

## 1. Scope (one paragraph)
This Part IV chapter teaches first-year medical students to **recognise the dietary levers for bone health across the life course and refer appropriately** — it does **not** teach osteoporosis pharmacotherapy. The organising metaphor is **"build the bank in youth, then defend the balance"**: peak bone mass is essentially set by the late 20s (≈90% laid down by the end of adolescence), only ~20–40% of it is lifestyle-modifiable, and everyone slowly loses bone thereafter, with a sharp acceleration around menopause in women. Against that spine, students learn the three nutrient levers — **calcium, vitamin D and protein** — plus **adequate energy/body weight**, and how each acts (calcium as substrate, vitamin D as the enabler of calcium *absorption*, protein as both matrix and IGF-1 driver). The Singapore-first anchors are the **paradox of common vitamin D insufficiency in a sunny, equatorial country** (indoor lifestyles, sun avoidance, covering, skin pigmentation), a **local calcium gap** (mean adult intake ~794 mg/day; about half of older adults below 70% of the RDA), a genuinely **dairy-optional Asian food-first list** (calcium-set tofu/tau kwa, ikan bilis and canned sardines with soft bones, low-oxalate greens like kai-lan and bok choy, fortified soy milk), and Singapore's high — though now falling — **hip-fracture burden**. The clinical endpoint is **recognise-and-refer**: know the levers, know the red flags, and signpost DXA/FRAX/OSTA and specialist management. This chapter closes Part IV as the **deficiency/frailty pair** alongside the Undernutrition & Malnutrition chapter.

## 2. Learning objectives
By the end of the chapter, students can:
- [ ] **LO1 — Explain** the life-course model of bone mass ("build the bank, then defend it"): peak bone mass is reached by the late 20s with ~90% laid down by the end of adolescence, only ~20–40% is lifestyle-modifiable, and bone is then lost slowly through adulthood with menopausal acceleration in women.
- [ ] **LO2 — Describe** the roles of calcium (substrate), vitamin D (enables calcium *absorption* via active 1,25-(OH)₂D) and protein (collagen matrix + IGF-1 + muscle/fall protection) in bone, and **recognise** that calcium *bioavailability* — not just content — determines the lever.
- [ ] **LO3 — Recognise** the dietary risk factors (low calcium, vitamin D insufficiency, inadequate protein, low body weight/underfuelling; plus high alcohol and non-dietary smoking) and protective factors (adequate calcium + vitamin D, sufficient protein, fruit and vegetables, and — cross-referred — weight-bearing/resistance loading).
- [ ] **LO4 — Explain** the Singapore vitamin D paradox and the local calcium gap, and **outline** a dairy-optional, food-first calcium plan using culturally familiar local foods (calcium-set tofu/tau kwa, ikan bilis, canned sardines with bones, low-oxalate Asian greens, fortified soy milk).
- [ ] **LO5 — Identify** when to refer and **signpost** (not run) the case-finding/diagnostic tools — OSTA, FRAX and DXA (T-score ≤ −2.5 or a fragility fracture = osteoporosis) — recognising that osteoporosis pharmacotherapy and menopausal hormone therapy are specialist-owned.
- [ ] **LO6 — Appraise honestly** the evidence that blanket calcium/vitamin D supplementation of already-replete community adults does **not** prevent fractures (and slightly raises kidney-stone risk), so the message is *dietary adequacy + risk-based referral*, not pills for all.

*(Verb level capped at recognise/classify/describe/explain/outline/appraise — no "manage with drug X". Map to programme outcomes in `../00-overview/learning-outcomes.md`.)*

## 4. Key concepts to teach (didactic spine)
The ordered concepts that become the chapter's sections — each tied to evidence.

1. **The life-course spine: "build the bank, then defend it."** Bone mass is accrued through childhood/puberty, plateaus at a genetically-set peak by the late 20s (~90% laid down by the end of adolescence), then is slowly withdrawn lifelong. The higher the peak, the more reserve before the fracture threshold. *(tie: `hereford2024peakBoneMass`, `bailey1999saskatchewanAccrual`)*
2. **Peak bone mass is mostly inherited — manage expectations without nihilism.** Only ~20–40% of adult peak bone mass is lifestyle-modifiable (majority heritable, up to ~70%); calcium and physical activity carry the strongest (grade A) evidence. *(tie: `nofPeakBoneMass2016`, `hereford2024peakBoneMass`)*
3. **Why the youth deposit pays off — as YEARS OF DELAY, never a fracture-rate figure.** A modelled 10% higher peak BMD delays osteoporosis onset ~13 years, vs ~2 years each for later menopause or slower age-related loss. *(tie: `hernandez2003peakBmdModel`)*
4. **Everyone leaks bone — in both sexes, starting early.** Trabecular loss begins in young adulthood (men and women); cortical loss starts midlife in women and after ~75 in men. So the diet levers matter across adulthood, not just after menopause. *(tie: `riggs2008boneLossSites`)*
5. **The menopause acceleration.** Around the final menstrual period women lose ~7% lumbar-spine and ~6% femoral-neck BMD over the transmenopause (~2%/yr, derived). One-sentence mechanism: oestrogen withdrawal **raises RANKL** (say RANKL rises; do **not** say "OPG falls"). Hormonal context → Menopause & Midlife chapter. *(tie: `greendale2012swanBone`, `eghbali2003rankl`)*
6. **Calcium: substrate, requirements and the Singapore gap.** Life-course RDA (Singapore): 1000 mg adolescents 10–18 y, 800 mg adults 19–50 y, 1000 mg adults 51+ and pregnancy/lactation. Mean SG adult intake ~794 mg/day (among the highest in Asia, still below RDA); ~half of older adults short. *(tie: `hpbRDACalcium`, `hpbNNS2010`, `balkGlobalCalcium2017`, `ye2023daha`)*
7. **Bioavailability, not just content, decides the lever.** Low-oxalate greens ~50% absorbed, calcium-set tofu ≈ milk (~30%), spinach only ~5% (oxalate-bound). Absorption falls with dose size (favour ≤500 mg/sitting), depends on vitamin D, and declines with age. *(tie: `weaverTofuMilk2002`, `weaverVegCalcium1999`, `odsCalcium`)*
8. **A dairy-optional Asian food-first list.** Calcium-set tofu/tau kwa, ikan bilis and canned sardines with soft bones, low-oxalate greens (kai-lan, bok choy, chye sim), fortified soy milk — mapped to per-serving calcium. *(tie: `healthhubCalciumSG`, `weaverVegCalcium1999`)*
9. **Vitamin D: the enabler of calcium absorption (recognition-level mechanism).** Active 1,25-(OH)₂D acts via intestinal VDR/RXR on the TRPV6 channel to drive calcium uptake; deficiency → ↓absorption → secondary hyperparathyroidism → bone resorption → osteomalacia/osteoporosis. Deficiency-disease detail owned by the Micronutrients chapter. *(tie: `laird2010vitDbone`)*
10. **The Singapore vitamin D paradox — common and unequal.** ~1 in 3 mid-life SG women deficient (≤20 ng/mL) despite equatorial sun; Indian (aOR 5.58) and Malay (aOR 3.83) > Chinese; drivers are behavioural + pigmentation, not climate; skin synthesis also declines with age. *(tie: `cheong2021vitDsg`, `sgVitDeficiency`, `nimitphong_2013_vitd_sea`, `maclaughlin1985skinVitD`, `sghVitDsg2026`)*
11. **Vitamin D sources and the "paradox of prevalence," not a treatment threshold.** Few foods contain it naturally (oily fish best; fortification-dependent); guideline bodies now advise **against routine testing and empiric supplementation** in healthy adults. Teach *insufficiency is common by lab cutoff* — test-and-treat genuine shortfall in high-risk groups, don't blanket-dose. *(tie: `odsVitaminD`, `hpb_healthhub_rda`, `demay2024endoSocVitD`)*
12. **Protein is a bone nutrient, not a bone threat.** Half of bone volume is the type-I collagen matrix; protein drives IGF-1 and preserves fall-preventing muscle; higher intake *with adequate calcium* tracks ~11% lower hip fracture. The acid-ash "protein leaches calcium" myth is backwards (extra urinary calcium comes from better *absorption*). *(tie: `groenendijk2019protein`, `iof2024proteinNutrients`)*
13. **Adequate energy/body weight is itself a lever.** Low BMI (20 vs 25) nearly doubles hip-fracture risk (RR 1.95), largely BMD-mediated and used in FRAX; underfuelling/frailty are red flags. RED-S specifics → Childhood & Adolescence chapter. *(tie: `delaet2005bmiFracture`, `ye2023daha`)*
14. **The whole-diet pattern beats any single nutrient.** Fruit/veg (potassium, alkaline salts, vitamin K for osteocalcin, magnesium) plus a reliable calcium source, enough protein and energy; <5 servings/day fruit-veg is a hip-fracture risk flag. Overall pattern → What a Healthy Diet Looks Like chapter. *(tie: `iof2024proteinNutrients`, `iofNutritionProf`, `nofPeakBoneMass2016`)*
15. **The solid risk factors vs the contested box — taught honestly.** Solid: low calcium/vitamin D/protein/body weight, high alcohol (J-shaped), smoking (non-dietary, major). Contested/soft-on-fractures: **salt** (real calciuria, weak fracture link — the win is mostly BP), **caffeine** (tiny, offset by milk), **cola** (observational, confounded by milk displacement). *(tie: `iofRiskFactors`, `teucher2008sodiumBone`, `mohNNS2022`, `heaney2002caffeineBone`, `liu2012coffeeFracture`, `tucker2006colaBMD`, `ke2023alcoholFracture`, `law1997smokingHipFracture`)*
16. **Protective supplementation — honest reconciliation.** Calcium + vitamin D cuts fractures in the *deficient/institutionalised* (~15% total, ~30% hip), but **not** in already-replete community dwellers; VITAL and USPSTF confirm the null (and a small kidney-stone harm). So: correct real deficiency, don't blanket-supplement. *(tie: `weaver2016cavitd`, `zhao2017cavitdNoBenefit`, `leboff2022vitalFractures`, `vital2019`, `uspstfCalcium2018`)*
17. **Loading is the strongest modifiable lever — name it, don't prescribe it.** High-intensity resistance + impact training raised BMD in postmenopausal women (LIFTMOR); the exercise *prescription* belongs to the physical-activity pillar. *(tie: `watson2018liftmor`)*
18. **Singapore epidemiology: high but falling rates, rising counts.** Historically among Asia's highest hip-fracture rates; age-standardised rates now declining but absolute cases rising with ageing; ~2:1 female:male; Chinese highest ethnic risk; hip fracture is lethal (SMR ~2 women/~3.3 men); DXA osteoporosis common in the old. *(tie: `koh2001hipfracSG`, `yong2019hipfracSG`, `chandran2019burdenSG`, `yong2020mortalitySG`, `gani2025osteoconsensusSG`, `aceOsteoporosis2025`)*
19. **When to refer — recognise-and-refer, signpost the tools.** HPB's two public levers (high-calcium diet + weight-bearing exercise); OSTA (age + weight screen → who gets DXA); FRAX (10-yr probability, Singapore-calibrated); DXA T-score ≤ −2.5 or a fragility fracture = osteoporosis; pharmacotherapy/HRT signposted to specialists/2025 consensus. *(tie: `hpbOsteoporosisAwareness2009`, `aceOsteoporosis2018`, `koh2001osta`, `fraxSingapore`, `gani2025osteoconsensusSG`, `healthhubCalciumSG`)*

## 6. Singapore context
- **The vitamin D paradox is the signature local twist.** In a sunny, equatorial city-state, ~1 in 3 mid-life women are vitamin D deficient (`cheong2021vitDsg`), and a small healthy-adult study puts ~42% deficient (`sgVitDeficiency`) — driven by **indoor lifestyles, cosmetic sun avoidance, covering/veiling and skin pigmentation** (`nimitphong_2013_vitd_sea`), not climate. The burden is **unequal**: Indian (aOR 5.58) and Malay (aOR 3.83) women carry several-fold higher odds than Chinese women — a concrete equity point. Local clinical commentary reports ≥30% deficiency and up to ~90% of pregnant women insufficient in a 2024 KKH study, with KKH now giving vitamin-D-containing multivitamins to preconception/pregnant patients (`sghVitDsg2026`). HPB's vitamin D RDA is only **2.5 mcg (100 IU) with an explicit tropical-sun caveat**, applying to ages 7 and above (`hpb_healthhub_rda`) — reflecting *designed* reliance on skin synthesis.
- **The calcium gap is real and local, not a Western problem.** Mean adult intake is ~794 mg/day — **among the highest in Asia** (most Asian nations <400–500 mg/day; `balkGlobalCalcium2017`) but still below the RDA — with 35.2% of adults inadequate in 2010 (`hpbNNS2010`). In older Singaporeans the gap is wider: ~49.5% of men and 55.3% of women are inadequate, and ~half fall below 70% of the RDA (`ye2023daha`). Singapore's RDAs (`hpbRDACalcium`): **1000 mg** adolescents 10–18 y, **800 mg** adults 19–50 y, **1000 mg** adults 51+ and in pregnancy/lactation.
- **Dairy is optional here.** A partly lactose-intolerant, multi-ethnic population can meet calcium from **calcium-set tofu/tau kwa** (absorbed like milk; `weaverTofuMilk2002`), **ikan bilis and canned sardines eaten with the soft bones**, **low-oxalate greens** (kai-lan, bok choy, chye sim; `weaverVegCalcium1999`) and **fortified soy milk** — HPB gives per-serving figures (`healthhubCalciumSG`): sardine w/bones 270 mg, ikan bilis 270 mg, tau kwa 150 mg, kai-lan 195 mg, chye sim 140 mg, fortified soy milk 500 mg (vs low-fat milk 380 mg). Practical swaps: yong tau foo with tofu, kopi/teh with milk, ikan bilis in stock. **Caveat:** high-oxalate spinach/bayam looks calcium-rich but is only ~5% absorbed (`odsCalcium`) — the Micronutrients chapter owns this contrast.
- **High but falling fracture rates; rising counts.** Singapore historically had **among the highest hip-fracture rates in Asia** (~152/100k men, ~402/100k women in 1991–98; `koh2001hipfracSG`). In the new millennium **age-standardised rates are declining** (−4.3/100k/yr women, −1.1 men, 2000–2017) yet **absolute admissions keep rising** as the population ages (`yong2019hipfracSG`); ACE confirms the decline continued to 2022 (`aceOsteoporosis2025`). The ratio is ~**2:1 female:male**, **Chinese** carry the highest ethnic risk (Chinese women 264/100k, 1.4× Malay, 1.9× Indian), and hip fracture is **lethal** — SMR ~2.05 women / ~3.28 men (`yong2020mortalitySG`). Osteoporotic fractures are projected to rise **~58% by 2035** (`chandran2019burdenSG`). Teach the rate-vs-count distinction explicitly.
- **The Singapore guidance stack the student signposts:** HPB's simple two-lever public message — high-calcium diet + weight-bearing exercise (`hpbOsteoporosisAwareness2009`); the **ACE Appropriate Care Guide** for primary-care case-finding and referral (`aceOsteoporosis2018`, updated `aceOsteoporosis2025`); and the **2025 Singapore consensus** for specialist management (`gani2025osteoconsensusSG`). Case-finding uses **OSTA** (Asian, age + weight; `koh2001osta`) and **FRAX** with **Singapore-calibrated ethnicity models** (`fraxSingapore`); **DXA** T-score ≤ −2.5 (or a fragility fracture) defines osteoporosis. **Source the numeric calcium RDA to the HPB/HealthHub/ACE tables, NOT to the 2012 HPB seniors' release** (`hpbSeniors`), which is qualitative only.

## 7. Pillar intersections
- **nutrition × physical activity.** Mechanical loading is the single strongest *modifiable* lever for bone: LIFTMOR showed supervised high-intensity resistance + impact training raised lumbar-spine and femoral-neck BMD in postmenopausal women with low bone mass, safely (`watson2018liftmor`). Here we **name** loading and cross-refer the *prescription* to the physical-activity pillar — nutrition and loading are partners (calcium is the substrate, loading is the signal to use it). HPB's own public message pairs a high-calcium diet with weight-bearing exercise (`hpbOsteoporosisAwareness2009`).
- **nutrition × the life course (ageing).** Skin vitamin D synthesis more than halves with age (`maclaughlin1985skinVitD`), calcium-absorption efficiency declines (`odsCalcium`), appetite and protein intake fall, and low body weight/frailty compounds fracture risk (`delaet2005bmiFracture`, `ye2023daha`) — so the "sunny country = replete" assumption fails hardest in the old. Complements the **Healthy Ageing chapter**.
- **nutrition × women's midlife physiology.** The menopausal RANKL-driven acceleration (`greendale2012swanBone`, `eghbali2003rankl`) is when the diet levers matter most intensely; the **hormonal context and HRT belong to the Menopause & Midlife chapter** — forward-reference it, don't re-teach.
- **nutrition × substances.** Smoking is a major, quantifiable bone risk (`law1997smokingHipFracture`) and heavy alcohol raises fracture risk in a J-shaped way (`ke2023alcoholFracture`) — recognise and cross-refer to cessation/moderation support, not "treated" within nutrition.

## 8. Clinical application & case ideas (MCQ-friendly, do-no-harm aware)
*(Per house rule, in-chapter cases are choice/MCQ only — the live AI patient-chat lives only in the Integrated Cases chapter. Log in `../cases/case-bank.md`.)*

**Case A — "Madam Lim, 58, post-menopausal, dairy-averse."** *(flagship MCQ case)*
- *Stem:* Chinese woman, 3 years post-menopause, BMI 19, lactose-intolerant so avoids milk, mostly indoor, covers up outdoors, walks little. Diet: rice + vegetables, occasional tofu, little fish. No fracture yet.
- *Q1 — Which is her **single biggest dietary lever**?* ✔ "A reliable daily calcium source (calcium-set tofu, ikan bilis, kai-lan, fortified soy milk)" / ✘ "A high-dose calcium + vitamin D supplement for everyone her age" → feedback: food-first; blanket low-dose supplements don't prevent fractures in replete community adults and slightly raise stone risk (`zhao2017cavitdNoBenefit`, `uspstfCalcium2018`).
- *Q2 — She says "spinach is full of calcium."* ✔ "Spinach calcium is oxalate-bound and only ~5% absorbed; choose kai-lan/bok choy" / ✘ "Yes, spinach is an excellent calcium source" (`odsCalcium`, `weaverVegCalcium1999`).
- *Q3 — Low BMI (19) matters because…* ✔ "Low body weight nearly doubles hip-fracture risk, largely via lower BMD" / ✘ "It has no bearing on bone" (`delaet2005bmiFracture`).
- *Q4 — Next step?* ✔ "Use OSTA (age + weight) to decide DXA, give food-first calcium/vitamin D + loading advice, refer if high risk" / ✘ "Start a bisphosphonate now" → feedback: recognise-and-refer; pharmacotherapy is specialist (`koh2001osta`, `aceOsteoporosis2018`).

**Case B — "Priya, 34, covered, indoor office worker."** *(vitamin D paradox)*
- *Stem:* Indian woman, works indoors, covers up, avoids sun for skin tone; fatigue, aches. Sunny Singapore.
- *Q1 — Most likely nutrient issue?* ✔ "Vitamin D insufficiency despite the tropical sun (behavioural + pigmentation drivers)" / ✘ "Impossible in sunny Singapore" (`cheong2021vitDsg`, `nimitphong_2013_vitd_sea`).
- *Q2 — Do we screen the whole population's vitamin D?* ✔ "No — test-and-treat genuine/high-risk shortfall; routine testing/empiric dosing not advised in healthy adults" / ✘ "Yes, everyone should be tested and supplemented" (`demay2024endoSocVitD`).
- *Pitfall trap:* choosing "megadose vitamin D fixes bones for everyone" → feedback: vitamin D *enables calcium absorption*; supplementing already-replete people doesn't cut fractures (`leboff2022vitalFractures`).

**Case C — "Ah Seng, 72, frail, poor appetite."** *(protein + energy lever)*
- *Stem:* Underweight older man, eats little, skips fish/tofu, worried protein "leaches calcium from bones."
- *Q1 — Correct the myth:* ✔ "With adequate calcium, more protein is neutral-to-beneficial and tracks lower hip-fracture risk (~11%); extra urinary calcium comes from better absorption, not bone" / ✘ "He's right — cut protein to protect bone" (`groenendijk2019protein`).
- *Q2 — Practical plan:* ✔ "Boost protein + energy across meals (tofu, fish, eggs, dahl), shore up calcium, flag malnutrition risk" — cross-ref Undernutrition & Malnutrition chapter / ✘ "Just add a calcium tablet" (`ye2023daha`, `iof2024proteinNutrients`).

**Case D — "The worried coffee-and-cola question."** *(contested factors, honesty)*
- *Q — Which claim is best supported?* ✔ "Heavy salt raises urinary calcium (mechanism real) but the fracture link is weak — cutting salt mainly helps BP"; distractors: "coffee causes osteoporosis" (tiny, offset by milk), "cola dissolves bone via phosphoric acid" (observational, milk-displacement confounded) (`teucher2008sodiumBone`, `heaney2002caffeineBone`, `tucker2006colaBMD`, `mohNNS2022`).

## 9. Common misconceptions & pitfalls
- **"Sunny Singapore means everyone has enough vitamin D."** → ~1 in 3 mid-life women are deficient; drivers are behavioural + pigmentation, and skin synthesis falls with age (`cheong2021vitDsg`, `nimitphong_2013_vitd_sea`, `maclaughlin1985skinVitD`).
- **"You need dairy for calcium."** → Calcium-set tofu absorbs like milk and low-oxalate greens even better; ikan bilis, sardines-with-bones and fortified soy milk close the gap (`weaverTofuMilk2002`, `weaverVegCalcium1999`, `healthhubCalciumSG`).
- **"Spinach is a great calcium source."** → Its calcium is oxalate-bound and only ~5% absorbed vs ~27% for milk — content ≠ availability (`odsCalcium`).
- **"Protein leaches calcium from bone (acid-ash)."** → Backwards: the extra urinary calcium after a protein meal comes from *increased intestinal absorption*, and higher protein *with adequate calcium* tracks lower hip fracture (`groenendijk2019protein`). Do **not** overcorrect to "protein only helps those with low calcium."
- **"Everyone over 50 should take calcium + vitamin D for their bones."** → In already-replete community adults, blanket low-dose supplements don't prevent fractures and slightly raise kidney-stone risk; benefit is for genuine deficiency (`zhao2017cavitdNoBenefit`, `uspstfCalcium2018`, `leboff2022vitalFractures`).
- **"A 10% higher peak BMD cuts fractures by X%."** → The model output is **years of delay** (~13 years), **not** a fracture-rate reduction — never convert it to a % fracture cut (`hernandez2003peakBmdModel`).
- **"Bone loss is only a post-menopausal, female problem."** → Trabecular loss begins in young adulthood in **both** sexes; men lose bone too (cortical loss mainly after 75) (`riggs2008boneLossSites`).
- **"Diet can fix any bone; it's all lifestyle."** → Only ~20–40% of peak bone mass is modifiable; the majority is heritable — real but bounded benefit (`nofPeakBoneMass2016`).
- **"Coffee/salt/cola are proven bone destroyers."** → Salt's fracture link is weak (win is BP), caffeine is offset by a splash of milk, and the cola–BMD signal is observational and confounded by milk displacement (`teucher2008sodiumBone`, `heaney2002caffeineBone`, `tucker2006colaBMD`).
- **"Falling hip-fracture rates mean the problem is shrinking."** → Age-standardised *rates* fall while absolute *counts* rise with ageing — distinguish rate from count (`yong2019hipfracSG`, `chandran2019burdenSG`).
- **"Say OPG falls at menopause."** → Demonstrated change is a **rise in RANKL**; the source used OPG only as a detection probe — don't assert an OPG fall (`eghbali2003rankl`).
