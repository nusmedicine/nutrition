# Research Dossier — Chapter 11 "Adulthood: working life & activity" (Part II, net-new)

*House-format dossier. IRON RULE applied: only "confirmed" verdicts, or a "corrected" verdict's corrected form, are printed as fact. Rejected/uncertain claims are dropped or demoted to qualitative statements and flagged.*

---

## 1. Scope

This chapter covers the **healthy working-age adult (≈20s–50s)** — the life stage where linear growth is finished and the physiological story shifts from *building* to *maintaining*. It sits after *Childhood & Adolescence* and before *Menopause & Midlife Health* and *Healthy Ageing*. Its two deliberately under-covered, chapter-owned domains are **exercise / sports nutrition for the everyday active adult** and the **working-life food environment** (shift work, daily hawker/eating-out, stress eating, time scarcity, alcohol). The altitude is strictly **recognise / advise-simply / refer** for a generalist clinician: how to fuel ordinary training, keep muscle and weight steady through midlife, navigate a hawker-dominated food environment, give a calibrated modern alcohol message, and know when to hand off. It is **not** elite sports dietetics, weight-loss prescribing, or metabolic-syndrome management. The unifying teaching device is an **internal ↔ external driver** template: what changes *inside* (maintenance, muscle/protein, stable-adult metabolism) and *outside* (the food/activity environment) → resulting needs → red flags → when to refer → Singapore context.

---

## 2. Learning objectives

By the end of the chapter, the early medical student should be able to:

1. **Explain** why muscle mass and total/basal energy expenditure are broadly *stable* from ~20–60 y, and therefore why midlife weight creep reflects a small chronic energy surplus and declining activity/muscle — **not** an inevitable "slowing metabolism."
2. **State** practical, down-scaled fuelling targets for the everyday active adult — protein ~1.2–1.6 g/kg/day (spread across meals), carbohydrate scaled to training load, and individualised hydration (<2% body-mass loss) — and distinguish these from athlete-calibrated ranges that should *not* be over-prescribed.
3. **Describe** the working-life food environment as the dominant driver of adult diet quality (hawker/eating-out defaults, shift work, stress and time scarcity, short sleep) and identify low-risk, evidence-aligned behavioural levers.
4. **Deliver** a calibrated, modern alcohol message: no established safe level for cancer risk, the collapse of the "cardioprotective moderate drinking" claim, energy content, and the ALDH2 "flush" as a Singapore-salient risk marker.
5. **Recognise** red flags and **apply** the screen–advise–refer principle (e.g. AUDIT-C for unhealthy alcohol use; onward referral for suspected disordered eating, established obesity, or shift-work metabolic risk), keeping deeper management in scope for other chapters.
6. **Locate** advice within Singapore's national guidance and programmes (SPAG activity dose, Nutri-Grade, Healthier Dining Programme, National Steps Challenge, MOH/HPB alcohol limits).

## 3. Didactic spine (internal ↔ external template)

### INTERNAL — what changes inside: maintenance, not growth

**A. The stable adult metabolism (the myth-buster that anchors the chapter).**
Fat-free-mass-adjusted **total and basal energy expenditure are stable from ~20 to 60 y**, including through pregnancy, and decline only after ~60 (≈−0.7%/yr) — the Pontzer doubly-labelled-water life-course dataset (n=6,421). *(Verified; note the paper does not specifically address menopause — soften any "and menopause" clause.)* Teaching consequence: midlife weight creep is a **small chronic positive energy balance**, most plausibly from falling physical activity/NEAT and muscle plus rising intake — not a falling resting metabolic rate.

**B. Muscle: peak, plateau, slow drift.**
Muscle mass is **near-static from 18–60 y** (mass loss only ~0.4–0.5%/yr, accelerating later); **strength is lost 2–5× faster than mass** ("dynapenia") (Mitchell 2012). The popular "1%/yr from age 30" overstates mid-adult loss. The working-age window is about **maintenance and building reserve**, upstream of clinically meaningful sarcopenia (→ *Healthy Ageing*).

**C. Why muscle matters metabolically.**
Skeletal muscle is the largest sink for post-meal glucose (>80% of an oral glucose load; principal site of insulin-stimulated disposal) (Merz & Thurmond 2020) — the mechanistic bridge motivating muscle preservation as upstream metabolic protection. *Cite to motivate only; the physiology is Part I.*

**D. Protein for the active adult (chapter-owned detail).**
- RDA 0.8 g/kg/day is a floor, not an optimum; **1.4–2.0 g/kg/day** is sufficient to build/maintain muscle in exercising people (ISSN; Kerksick 2018; Thomas 2016 gives 1.2–2.0).
- Hard-outcome anchor: added protein + resistance training raises fat-free mass (+0.30 kg) and strength (+2.49 kg 1RM), **plateauing at total intake ~1.62 g/kg/day** (95% CI 1.03–2.20) (Morton 2018). Practical target for the everyday active adult: **~1.2–1.6 g/kg/day**.
- **Resistance loading is the necessary stimulus** — protein without loading does little; hypertrophy is dose-graded up to ~10 sets/muscle/week, achievable with **2 sessions/week** (Schoenfeld 2017).
- Muscle-strengthening is **preventive medicine**: ~10–17% lower all-cause/CVD/cancer/diabetes risk, most benefit at ~30–60 min/week (Momma 2022, associational).
- **Distribution:** ~0.25–0.4 g/kg (≈20–40 g) per meal across ≥4 meals modestly beats dinner-skewing (Areta 2013; Mamerow 2014; Schoenfeld & Aragon 2018). Honest caveat: the distribution story rests on acute MPS surrogates that may not predict long-term hypertrophy — *spread it out, but don't obsess*.
- **Reassurances:** higher protein (≥1.5 g/kg) does not harm kidneys in healthy adults (Devries 2018, GFR surrogate; **excludes CKD**); animal vs plant source barely matters when total protein is adequate (Lim/Kim NUS 2021 — small % edge for animal in <50 y).

### EXTERNAL — what changes outside: fuelling, the food environment, alcohol

**E. Exercise fuelling for the everyday active adult.**
- **Carbohydrate by training load** (Thomas 2016, Table 2): light 3–5, moderate ~1 h/day 5–7, endurance 1–3 h/day 6–10, extreme 8–12 g/kg/day. Most working adults doing 30–60 min sit near general-population advice — **the high bands are for serious endurance athletes and must not be prescribed to recreational exercisers.**
- **During-exercise carbohydrate by duration:** none <45 min; 30–60 g/h for 1–2.5 h; up to ~90 g/h only for ultra-endurance >2.5–3 h. Myth-buster: a ~1 h gym/park session needs **water, not a sports drink**.
- **Recovery:** ~20–30 g protein + carbohydrate post-exercise; aggressive refuelling (1–1.2 g/kg/h CHO, first 4 h) is only for two sessions <8 h apart — **not** the once-daily exerciser.
- **The "anabolic window" is wide:** once total daily protein is adequate, precise peri-workout timing has no independent effect on strength/hypertrophy; the MPS elevation lasts ≥24 h (Schoenfeld 2013; ISSN 2017). Reassure patients: **no mandatory 30-minute shake.**
- **Hydration:** individualise to sweat losses (0.3–2.4 L/h; plan ~0.4–0.8 L/h), keep body-mass loss <2%, prehydrate ~5–7 mL/kg. **Sweat sodium averages ~35 mmol/L (range 10–70; ~0.8 g Na/L)** — sodium replacement matters mainly for prolonged (>~3 h) or heavy/salty sweaters *(corrected from the brief's ~50 mmol/L figure)*. Over-drinking low-sodium fluid is the main cause of exercise-associated hyponatraemia — a real risk in cautious recreational exercisers; thirst-guided drinking usually suffices (Sawka 2007).

**F. The working-life food environment.**
- **Shift work** (Singapore's 24/7 economy): night-shift work is associated with higher **type 2 diabetes** incidence (pooled HR 1.30, 95% CI 1.18–1.43; dose-response with years) (Xie 2024) and, in women, modestly higher **coronary heart disease** (HR 1.15–1.18 for ≥10 y; attenuates after stopping) (Vetter 2016). **Mechanism:** controlled circadian-misalignment experiments worsen glucose/insulin/BP and appetite hormones (Scheer 2009); **daytime-weighted eating prevents the glucose intolerance** of simulated night work (Chellappa 2021 — the strongest interventional support for "eat your main meals in the day, avoid 00:00–06:00"). IARC classes night-shift work Group 2A ("probably carcinogenic") — cite only to model calibrated causal language.
- **Meal timing:** late isocaloric eating raises hunger, lowers waking energy expenditure (~−59 kcal/day) and shifts adipose toward storage (Vujović 2022); regular/earlier-weighted patterns are generally more favourable (AHA statement, St-Onge 2017, largely observational; breakfast-skipping–mortality association Rong 2019, confounded — do **not** teach causation).
- **Stress eating:** psychological stress modestly increases unhealthy-food and decreases healthy-food intake, moderated by emotional/restrained eating (Hill 2022 meta-analysis, small effect).
- **Short sleep:** partial sleep deprivation raises energy intake ~+385 kcal/day with no rise in expenditure (Al Khatib 2017); sleep extension (~+1.2 h) cuts intake ~−270 kcal/day (Tasali 2022 RCT).

**G. Alcohol in working/social adult life.**
- **No established safe level:** WHO/IARC position — alcohol is a Group 1 carcinogen with no identifiable safe threshold; ~half of alcohol-attributable cancers in the WHO European Region arise from light/moderate drinking (Anderson 2023; WHO 2023). Alcohol causes **≥7 cancers** (oral cavity, pharynx, larynx, oesophagus, liver, colorectum, female breast) via acetaldehyde/oxidative stress/raised oestrogen (WHO fact sheet). Teach relative vs absolute risk.
- **The cardioprotection claim has collapsed:** the observational J-curve is driven by abstainer/"sick-quitter" bias and confounding (AHA 2025); **Mendelian randomisation finds no protection** — genetically higher intake raises BP and coronary disease, harmful even at light intake (Biddinger 2022). The GBD 2016 analysis put the risk-minimising level at **zero** drinks/week (landmark but contested).
- **Dose-dependent cancer risk from light intake:** breast RR ~1.05 (light) → ~1.23 (moderate–heavy); oesophageal RR ~1.39 already at light intake (Jun 2023). *(The "one-third of EU breast cancers from ≤1–2 drinks/day" figure needs Rovira & Rehm 2021, not Jun.)*
- **Empty energy:** 7 kcal/g, poorly compensated → additive to hawker/eating-out calories (Traversy 2015); heavy drinking tracks with weight/abdominal gain, light-moderate less consistently.
- **Guideline thresholds** (harm-reduction ceilings, *not* safe/recommended amounts): Singapore ≤2/day (men), ≤1/day (women), standard drink = 10 g; US DGA 2020–2025 same, "less is better"; UK CMO ≤14 units/week.

### RESULTING NEEDS + RED FLAGS + WHEN TO REFER

- **Needs:** enough protein spread across meals (~1.2–1.6 g/kg/day); resistance work ≥2 days/week + 150–300 min/week aerobic; carbohydrate/fluid matched to *actual* (usually modest) training; a daytime-weighted, lower-sodium approach to hawker eating; minimal alcohol; adequate sleep.
- **Red flags → refer:** hazardous/harmful alcohol use (positive AUDIT-C → full AUDIT ≥8 → refer; **alcohol flush + drinking** = raised oesophageal-cancer risk, ask and advise); features of disordered eating or extreme dieting (→ *Childhood & Adolescence* / clinical); established obesity or metabolic syndrome (→ Part IV); CKD in a patient asking about high protein; shift workers with metabolic drift.
- **Do-no-harm:** don't prescribe athlete carbohydrate loading, mega-dose protein, mandatory recovery shakes, or "drink red wine for your heart"; don't frame prevention as repeated crash dieting.

## 5. Singapore context

- **Diet is set by the food environment, not the home kitchen.** ~77% of adults usually eat out; ~61% eat ≥1 daily meal at hawker centres (Naidoo 2017). NNS 2022: ~9 in 10 exceed the 2,000 mg sodium limit (mean 3,620 mg/day), only ~4% of staples are wholegrain, mean 2,410 kcal with 61% over target, fat 100 g (36% saturated), SSBs = 52% of sugar. Concrete dish anchors (Yeo 2021): Chinese dishes ~238 mg Na/100 g; a single "economical" noodle serving ~79% of daily sodium; energy density Indian>Chinese>Malay.
- **Lower Asian BMI action points** (≥23 overweight, ≥27.5 obese) mean "a few kg of creep" crosses risk thresholds at lower absolute weight than in the Western cohorts (Mozaffarian, Lee) supplying the evidence. Working-age obesity (BMI≥30) rose 10.5%→12.7% (2019-20→2023-24).
- **Activity:** SPAG 2022 = 150–300 min/week + muscle-strengthening ≥2 days/week; PA sufficiency 78.5% (2023) with **commuting the largest contributor (45.6%)** — activity is "built into" the working day. National Steps Challenge / Healthy 365 is the local gamified nudge (+1,579 steps/day; Yao 2022).
- **Shift work** is common (healthcare/transport/F&B/security); associated with T2DM (Xie) and metabolic syndrome (Sooriyaarachchi, OR 2.17). Practical message: **daytime-weighted eating, avoid 00:00–06:00** (Chellappa 2021).
- **Policy levers to name:** Healthier Dining Programme (≤500-kcal labels, ~2,700 stalls; typical eat-out meal 700–800 kcal), Nutri-Grade (mandatory in food courts/workplaces since Dec 2023; extends to sodium/sat-fat mid-2027), HPB Workplace Health Promotion.
- **Alcohol norms:** MOH limits ≤2/≤1 std drinks/day (standard drink = 10 g); HealthHub now cites WHO "no safe level." Binge drinking 13.7% (higher in 18–34; Lee 2020). The **ALDH2 flush** is the high-yield, examinable, Singapore-Chinese-salient teaching point.
- **Time scarcity:** long-hours culture normalises convenience eating and inactivity (Leu 2023) — the human framing for the whole external-driver section.

## 6. Common misconceptions

1. **"Your metabolism inevitably crashes at 40."** No — FFM-adjusted expenditure is stable ~20–60 y (Pontzer). Midlife creep is a small chronic surplus + declining activity/muscle, not a falling BMR.
2. **"Active adults need protein powder / must hit a shake in 30 minutes."** Most reach 1.2–1.6 g/kg/day from food; the anabolic window is ≥24 h wide — total daily protein ≫ precise timing (Schoenfeld 2013; ISSN).
3. **"More protein is always better."** Benefit plateaus ~1.6 g/kg/day for muscle (Morton 2018); mega-dosing isn't optimal (though there's no "wasted-surplus" ceiling either — Trommelen).
4. **"Protein wrecks your kidneys."** Not in healthy adults up to ≥1.5 g/kg (Devries 2018) — the exception is pre-existing CKD.
5. **"A daily glass of red wine is good for your heart."** The cardioprotection signal is bias/confounding; MR finds no protection and harm even at light intake (Biddinger; AHA 2025). No established safe level for cancer (WHO/IARC).
6. **"You can out-train a bad diet / a 1-hour run needs a sports drink."** Water suffices for typical ~1 h sessions; day-to-day intake, and the food environment, dominate weight — activity alone is a weak lever once overweight (Lee 2010).
7. **"Muscle loss and weight gain are just ageing — nothing to do."** Resistance work 2×/week meaningfully preserves muscle (Schoenfeld 2017) and is preventive medicine (Momma 2022); small sustainable habits and self-weighing beat repeated crash dieting (Graham; Zheng).

## 8. Proposed bespoke island + quiz topics + MCQ-only case

**Bespoke island (one idea prose can't teach): "Fuel-to-Activity Dial" + "Decade of Creep" twin visualiser.**
An interactive that does two things a paragraph can't:
- **Fuel dial:** the student sets a realistic adult profile (body mass; sessions/week; minutes/session; intensity). The island outputs *scaled* targets — carbohydrate band (from the Thomas 2016 3–5 / 5–7 / 6–10 ladder), protein g/day (1.2–1.6 g/kg, with per-meal 20–40 g distribution), and a fluid plan (0.4–0.8 L/h, <2% BM). **Pedagogical payoff:** for a "3× 45-min moderate gym sessions" adult, the dial lands on *general-population* carbohydrate advice and **"water, not a sports drink"** — visually inoculating against athlete over-prescription. A toggle shows what the *endurance-athlete* bands would (wrongly) recommend, making the down-scaling lesson explicit.
- **Decade-of-creep slider:** a small-surplus visualiser — the student adds ~50–100 kcal/day (a bubble tea; two after-work beers) and/or removes ~2,000 steps, and watches ~0.5–1 kg/yr accumulate over a decade against Singapore's ≥23/≥27.5 BMI action lines, with a "small change" lever showing how −100 kcal/day or +2,000 steps flattens the curve (Hill energy gap; Graham small-change). Reinforces "it's the environment and small habits, not a broken metabolism."

**MCQ quiz topics (bank):** (1) carbohydrate band for a recreational vs endurance exerciser; (2) practical protein target/distribution for an active adult; (3) the anabolic-window myth; (4) protein & kidneys in a healthy adult vs CKD; (5) hydration target and hyponatraemia risk; (6) why midlife creep ≠ slow metabolism; (7) shift-work meal-timing advice; (8) interpreting "no safe level" (relative vs absolute risk); (9) ALDH2 flush → which cancer + what to do; (10) reading a hawker menu / Nutri-Grade drink.

**MCQ-only case (recognise-and-advise altitude, with a do-no-harm fork):**
*Rahmat, 34, a rotating-shift ICU nurse and weekend recreational runner (10–15 km/week), asks whether he should "carb-load and take protein shakes," mentions after-work drinks 3–4 nights/week, notes weight has crept +4 kg over 3 years, and that he "flushes red" after one beer.*
- **Q1 (fuelling):** best advice for his weekend runs → *water for a ~60-min run; general-population carbohydrate; no routine sports drink or carb-loading.* (Distractor: prescribe 8–10 g/kg/day loading = **over-prescription trap**.)
- **Q2 (shift/meal timing):** most evidence-aligned tweak → *shift main meals to daytime, avoid heavy eating 00:00–06:00.*
- **Q3 (protein):** reasonable target → *~1.2–1.6 g/kg/day spread across meals*, and reassurance his kidneys are fine (no CKD). (Distractor: mega-dose 3 g/kg.)
- **Q4 (alcohol — the do-no-harm fork):** given the flush + regular drinking, the **scope-appropriate action** is to *ask about flushing, advise cutting down (raised oesophageal-cancer risk), screen with AUDIT-C, and refer if hazardous* — **not** to reassure him that "moderate drinking protects your heart." (This is the deliberate wrong-fork that tests the modern alcohol message and referral altitude.)
