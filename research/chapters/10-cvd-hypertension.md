# Nutrition in Cardiovascular Disease and Hypertension — research dossier

## 1. Scope (one paragraph)

This chapter teaches first-year medical students how dietary *patterns* — not isolated nutrients — modify blood pressure and atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease (ASCVD) risk, and where nutrition sits within a lifestyle-first management approach. The didactic spine runs: (1) cardiovascular disease (CVD) is Singapore's leading cause of death (~31% of deaths, 2022) and most risk is modifiable; (2) two evidence-rich whole-diet patterns — DASH and the Mediterranean diet — lower blood pressure and cardiovascular events respectively, each with endpoint-specific caveats; (3) the two nutrients students most associate with the heart, sodium and dietary fat, matter mainly through *pattern quality* (replacement and ordering behaviour, not just restriction); (4) cardiovascular risk is *estimated*, not measured, and the tool must fit the population — Singapore uses a locally recalibrated SG-FRS-2023 rather than imported US equations, a teaching contrast about why population-specific calibration exists. Lipid pharmacotherapy targets and treatment algorithms are deliberately kept at **awareness level** (signposted to the MOH ACE Lipid guidance), not threshold recall. The aim is a student who can explain *why* a heart-healthy pattern works, anchor advice to Singapore tools (My Healthy Plate, HPB sodium/Nutri-Grade messaging), and recognise when to refer to a dietitian or escalate to guideline-directed specialist care.

## 2. Learning objectives (refined)

Original: *["Summarise the dietary evidence for CVD and hypertension","Explain how DASH and Mediterranean patterns lower blood pressure and CV risk","Describe the role of dietary fat and sodium","Recognise where dietitian referral fits"]*

Refined, first-year-calibrated and assessable:

1. **Summarise** the strength *and limits* of dietary-pattern evidence for hypertension and ASCVD, citing DASH (blood pressure) and the Mediterranean diet (cardiovascular events) as the two landmark patterns — *including* their caveats (controlled feeding vs free-living; PREDIMED's 2018 retraction/re-analysis; which endpoints actually moved).
2. **Explain** the mechanisms by which the DASH and Mediterranean patterns lower blood pressure and cardiovascular risk (potassium/fibre/whole-food matrix; fat *quality* and replacement; the sodium–blood-pressure relationship).
3. **Describe** the role of dietary fat (saturated→unsaturated replacement; pattern over single nutrients) and sodium (population excess in Singapore; ~9 in 10 exceed limits) — framing both as *pattern levers*, not standalone fixes.
4. **Contrast** at a conceptual level why Singapore uses a locally recalibrated risk score (SG-FRS-2023) rather than imported US pooled-cohort equations, and articulate that lipid *targets and drug algorithms are specialist detail to be looked up* (ACE Lipid guidance), **not memorised thresholds**, at first-year level.
5. **Recognise** where lifestyle counselling ends and dietitian referral / guideline-directed pharmacotherapy begins.

## 4. Key concepts to teach (didactic spine)

1. **CVD is Singapore's biggest, mostly preventable killer.** ~31% of deaths (2022); ~37% of adults 18–74 have hypertension and ~half (53%) are undiagnosed → nutrition is population-scale prevention, not a niche add-on. *(`nphs2022`)*
2. **Patterns beat nutrients.** The AHA 2021 guidance reframes heart-healthy eating around an *overall dietary pattern* (10 features), de-emphasising single-nutrient targets and flagging ultra-processed foods. *(`aha2021diet`)*
3. **DASH lowers blood pressure — proven in a controlled feeding trial.** A fruit/veg/low-fat-dairy-rich pattern reduced BP ~5.5/3.0 mmHg overall and ~11/5.5 mmHg in those with hypertension — *without* weight loss or sodium change. Caveat to teach: this was an 8-week *controlled feeding* study measuring **blood pressure**, not cardiovascular events. *(`appel1997dash`)*
4. **Sodium and DASH are additive.** DASH-Sodium showed the lowest BP when the DASH pattern is combined with sodium reduction — sodium is a lever *within* the pattern, not a competing strategy. (Brief ~30-day feeding intervention.) *(`sacks2001dashsodium`)*
5. **The Mediterranean diet lowers cardiovascular *events*, with a stroke-dominated signal — taught WITH its caveat.** PREDIMED reported a ~30% *relative* risk reduction in the composite endpoint (MI, stroke, or CV death). The original 2013 paper was **retracted and re-published in 2018** after randomisation irregularities (some participants assigned non-randomly by household; one site randomised whole clinics); the re-analysis upheld the composite benefit, but it was **driven mainly by stroke, with no significant effect on MI alone**. Teach this as a model of how good science self-corrects — and respect the relative (not absolute) framing. *(`estruch2018predimed`)*
6. **Dietary fat: quality and replacement, not just "low fat."** Replacing saturated with unsaturated fat (liquid plant oils over tropical/animal fats) is the heart-relevant move; "low-fat" that swaps fat for refined starch/sugar is not protective. *(`aha2021diet`, `who2020diet`)*
7. **Sodium is a population-level problem in Singapore.** ~9 in 10 residents exceed recommended sodium; much is hidden in sauces, processed and hawker food → applied levers are ordering/seasoning behaviour, not just the salt shaker. WHO target salt <5 g/day. *(`mohNNS2022`, `who2020diet`)*
8. **Risk is estimated, and the tool must fit the population.** Singapore recalibrated the Framingham/NCEP-ATP-III tables into SG-FRS-2023 using local multi-ethnic cohort data, because imported US equations mis-estimate risk in Asian populations. *(Note for the writer: SG-FRS-2023 estimates 10-year* **coronary/CAD** *risk — it does* **not** *predict ischaemic stroke or total ASCVD; signpost this rather than calling it a general "ASCVD score".) (`sgfrs2023`; teaching contrast)*
9. **Lipid targets are look-up, not recall (awareness level).** Specific LDL-c targets and drug algorithms live in the ACE Lipid guidance and are applied by clinicians per risk tier — first-years should know *that the guidance exists and is risk-tiered*, not memorise thresholds. *(`aceLipid2023`)*
10. **Lifestyle first, then refer.** Diet/activity/alcohol/smoking are first-line for raised BP and lipids; dietitian referral and guideline-directed pharmacotherapy are escalation steps, not replacements. *(`aceLipid2023`, `ada2024care` referral principle)*

## 6. Singapore context

- **Epidemiology to open with:** CVD ≈ 31% of all deaths in 2022 (leading cause, confirmed via NHCS/national data); hypertension in ~37% of adults aged 18–74 with ~53% undiagnosed (NPHS 2022) — frames diet as primary prevention at scale. *(`nphs2022`)*
- **Sodium:** ~9 in 10 residents exceed recommended sodium intake (NNS 2022); ties directly to the DASH-Sodium teaching point. WHO target salt <5 g/day. *(`mohNNS2022`, `who2020diet`)*
- **HPB instruments to anchor applied advice:**
  - **My Healthy Plate** (½ fruit & vegetables, ¼ wholegrains, ¼ protein) — the local, ready-to-prescribe operationalisation of a DASH/Mediterranean-aligned pattern. *(`hpbHealthyPlate`)*
  - **Nutri-Grade** beverage labelling (A–D) — a population lever against sugar-sweetened drinks, a cardiometabolic risk factor. *(`hpbNutriGrade`)*
  - **HPB "Lower in Sodium" / reduced-salt hawker programmes** and the ask-for-less-sauce/less-gravy behavioural prompts.
- **Hawker-food worked examples (make it concrete):**
  - *Sodium:* laksa, bak kut teh, instant/wonton noodle soup, char kway teow, and added sauces (chilli, dark soy, kicap) are major hidden sodium sources → coaching: "leave half the soup base," "sauce on the side," choose steamed over fried.
  - *Fat quality:* swap deep-fried (you tiao, fried bee hoon) for steamed/grilled; nasi lemak/curries are high in saturated (coconut/ghee) fat → moderate frequency, don't demonise.
  - *Pattern build:* yong tau foo (soup, more veg, fewer fried items, plain not laksa gravy), thunder tea rice, and fish-soup-with-brown-rice as DASH/Med-aligned hawker choices; add fruit; choose wholegrain where available.
- **Risk-tool localisation (the teaching contrast):** Singapore uses **SG-FRS-2023**, recalibrated from the Framingham/NCEP-ATP-III tables using the local **Multi-Ethnic Cohort (MEC1)** and National Registry of Diseases Office data, because imported US tools (Framingham, ACC/AHA pooled-cohort equations) mis-estimate risk across Chinese/Malay/Indian groups. (For the writer: SG-FRS-2023 estimates 10-year *coronary* risk and does *not* itself predict stroke or total ASCVD — present it as the local coronary-risk tool, not an all-ASCVD calculator.) Lipid management itself is governed by the **MOH ACE Lipid guidance (Dec 2023)** — risk-tiered, lifestyle-first — and is **signposted, not drilled**. *(`sgfrs2023`, `aceLipid2023`)*

## 7. Pillar intersections

- **nutrition × alcohol (dyslipidaemia / HTN):** alcohol raises blood pressure dose-dependently and elevates triglycerides; "moderate alcohol is cardioprotective" is a weakening, confounded claim (increasingly undercut by Mendelian-randomisation evidence) — teach as *not a reason to start drinking*, and a lever to reduce in raised BP/lipids. Links to the alcohol pillar chapter.
- **nutrition × physical activity:** aerobic activity independently lowers blood pressure and improves lipids; lifestyle bundles (diet + activity) are how DASH/Mediterranean translate to real-world risk reduction. Reinforce that diet advice is delivered *alongside* activity, not in isolation.

## 8. Clinical application & case ideas (case-player-ready)

**Core case (expanded from prompt):** *"Raised BP and LDL — explain DASH and a heart-healthy pattern and when to refer."*

> **Stem:** Mr Tan, 52, Chinese, office worker, eats most meals at the hawker centre. Clinic BP 148/92 (repeat 146/90). Non-fasting lipids show raised LDL-c. No prior CVD, non-smoker, no diabetes. BMI 27.3 (Asian cut-off ≥27.5 = obese; he is in the high-risk overweight band). Asks: "Do I need medication, doctor?"

**Branch A — First move:**
- ✅ *Confirm BP over repeated readings, take a diet/activity/alcohol history, and start lifestyle-first counselling.* → best path.
- ⚠️ *Immediately prescribe a statin + antihypertensive.* → Feedback: at first-year level the correct framing is lifestyle-first and **risk estimation before treatment**; drug thresholds are guideline-driven (ACE Lipid guidance) and a later-year/clinician decision — not memorised here.
- ❌ *Reassure and review in a year.* → Feedback: misses a modifiable, high-prevalence risk window.

**Branch B — Which dietary advice?**
- ✅ *Recommend a DASH-aligned pattern mapped onto My Healthy Plate, plus sodium reduction.* → Feedback: DASH is the BP-specific evidence (~11/5.5 mmHg in those with hypertension, Appel 1997 — a feeding-study BP effect, expect smaller free-living gains); pairing with sodium reduction is additive (DASH-Sodium).
- ⚠️ *Tell him to "cut all fat."* → Feedback: it's fat *quality and replacement* (saturated→unsaturated), not blanket fat avoidance; "low-fat" swapped for refined starch isn't protective.
- ⚠️ *Tell him only to stop adding table salt.* → Feedback: most Singaporean sodium is hidden in sauces/soup-bases/processed and hawker food — the lever is ordering and seasoning behaviour.

**Branch C — Hawker translation (free-text or select):**
- Coach concrete swaps: fish soup + brown rice, yong tau foo (soup, more veg, fewer fried items), sauce-on-the-side, leave half the soup base, add fruit. Reward pattern-level, culturally specific advice over generic "eat healthy."

**Branch D — When to refer / escalate:**
- ✅ *Refer to a dietitian for structured pattern change + arrange formal cardiovascular risk assessment (SG-FRS-2023) and re-check BP/lipids; manage per ACE Lipid guidance by risk tier.* → Feedback: correct escalation. Note SG-FRS-2023 is the *local* coronary-risk tool (recalibrated for the multi-ethnic population) — contrast with naively applying a US score.
- **Teaching pop-up (must-include):** "You do **not** need to memorise LDL-c targets. Know that targets are **risk-tiered and looked up in the ACE Lipid guidance** — applying them is specialist/clinician detail layered on later." *(`aceLipid2023`)*

**Mini-case variant (contrast tool):** Same patient, but compare the risk category a US pooled-cohort equation might give vs SG-FRS-2023 → discussion prompt on *why population-specific recalibration exists* (US tools mis-estimate Asian risk). Keep conceptual; no calculation drilling.

*Branching-readiness note:* the four branches form a clean decision tree (work-up → diet content → cultural translation → escalation), each with one best option and labelled distractors carrying teaching feedback — directly portable into the case player.

## 9. Common misconceptions & pitfalls

1. **"Salt is the only thing that matters for blood pressure."** Sodium is one lever; the *whole DASH pattern* (potassium, fibre, low-fat dairy, fruit/veg) lowered BP in Appel 1997 *even before* sodium was changed. Best results combine both.
2. **"Low-fat = heart-healthy."** What protects is *replacing saturated with unsaturated* fat. Low-fat products high in refined sugar/starch are not protective (AHA 2021).
3. **"PREDIMED was retracted, so the Mediterranean diet is debunked."** It was retracted and *re-published* in 2018; the composite benefit held (stroke-dominated). Teach as science self-correcting, not as failure.
4. **"DASH/PREDIMED prove diet alone prevents heart attacks."** PREDIMED's signal was a ~30% *relative* reduction in a composite endpoint, stroke-dominated, with **no significant effect on MI alone**; DASH was a short feeding study measuring **BP**, not events. Respect the endpoints and the relative-vs-absolute distinction.
5. **"I must memorise the LDL target."** ❌ At first-year level: no. Targets are risk-tiered and looked up (ACE Lipid guidance) — awareness, not recall.
6. **"Use the American risk calculator."** US Framingham/pooled-cohort equations mis-estimate Asian risk; Singapore uses the recalibrated SG-FRS-2023 (a coronary-risk tool).
7. **"A bit of red wine is good for the heart, so encourage it."** Confounded, weakening evidence; alcohol raises BP and triglycerides — not a reason to start or increase drinking.
8. **"Heart-healthy eating means Western/Mediterranean food only."** The *principles* map onto local hawker choices (fish soup, yong tau foo, brown rice, fruit) — culturally translate, don't import.

## 11. Evidence gaps / open questions

- **Pattern trials are mostly Western and short.** DASH was an 8-week US controlled-feeding study (surrogate endpoint, BP); PREDIMED was Spanish. Long-term, Asian-population, hawker-food-based RCT evidence is sparse — be honest about extrapolation.
- **Free-living adherence vs feeding-study efficacy.** Real-world BP/lipid effects are smaller than tightly-controlled feeding trials; teach realistic effect sizes without nihilism.
- **SG-FRS-2023 endpoint scope.** The tool predicts 10-year *coronary* (CAD) risk, **not** ischaemic stroke or total ASCVD — a real limitation to state plainly when teaching it as the "local risk tool." Validation work (recalibrated pooled-cohort vs SG-FRS-2023 vs SCORE2-Asia-Pacific) is ongoing; present as "best current local tool," not final word.
- **Sodium "J-curve" controversy.** Some observational data suggest harm at very low sodium; mainstream guidance still favours reduction from current high intakes — flag as contested at the extremes, not at typical Singapore levels.
- **Ultra-processed food causality.** AHA 2021 flags UPFs, but mechanism beyond nutrient profile (additives, matrix) is unresolved.
- **Alcohol "cardioprotection."** Genetic/Mendelian-randomisation evidence increasingly undercuts the J-curve; teaching should track this shift.
- **Citation housekeeping (residual):** `ada2024care` edition must be confirmed against the current ADA Standards of Care (2025/26) before final cite — the MNT "no single ideal macro split / refer to dietitian" principle is stable across editions, but the year/key should be pinned. `medUmbrella` is a placeholder key for the Mediterranean-diet umbrella review and needs a specific citation (author/journal/year) pinned before publication.
