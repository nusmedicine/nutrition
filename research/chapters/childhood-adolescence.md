# Research Dossier — Chapter 10 "Childhood & Adolescence"
*Health in Medicine (Singapore-first nutrition textbook, Part II "Across the life stages")*
*Altitude: recognise & refer, NOT manage*

---

## 1. Scope

This chapter tracks the nutrition story from school-age (~5–6 y, seaming with the *Infancy & Early Childhood* chapter at ~4–5 y) through the end of adolescence, organised on the internal↔external driver template: what changes *inside* the child (linear growth then the time-limited pubertal spurt, sex-hormone-driven body-composition divergence, the peak-bone-mass window, and rising iron demand crowned by menarche) and *outside* (the school and hawker food environment, sugary-drink and bubble-tea culture, food marketing, family meals, physical activity and youth sport, and the developmental handover of food autonomy to the adolescent and their peers). The clinician-in-training's job here is to **anticipate stage-defining needs and recognise when a normal shift has tipped into a clinical problem** — growth faltering, iron deficiency, an under-fuelled athlete, an emerging eating disorder, or the obesity→cardiometabolic trajectory — and to **refer, not manage**. Every management thread (osteoporosis, ED assessment/treatment, obesity and paediatric T2D, DRI biology, teen pregnancy) is explicitly handed off to its owning chapter.

---

## 2. Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, the early medical student should be able to:

1. **Outline** the normal growth arc from steady school-age growth to the transient adolescent spurt, and explain why peak nutritional demand tracks pubertal *stage* rather than chronological age (and differs by sex).
2. **Recognise** the three headline internal drivers of nutrient demand in this stage — the energy/protein cost of the spurt, the peak-bone-mass window (calcium/vitamin-D/weight-bearing activity), and rising iron needs — and identify the menstruating adolescent girl as the group most vulnerable to iron deficiency.
3. **Describe** the external food and activity environment (SSBs/free sugars, food marketing, family meals, physical-activity guidelines, growing food autonomy) and how it shapes intake as the adolescent gains autonomy.
4. **Identify** red flags that a life-stage need has become a clinical problem — growth-curve deviation, disordered-eating warning signs across the whole weight spectrum, the under-fuelled athlete (amenorrhoea/stress fracture), and the obesity→T2D trajectory — and know **when and to whom to refer**.
5. **Apply** do-no-harm principles: that dieting and weight-talk are prospective risk factors for both eating disorders *and* weight gain, so weight is addressed via healthy habits and family meals, never via casual "go on a diet" advice.
6. **Relate** each driver to Singapore's local anchors (HPB RDAs, HMSP, Nutri-Grade, growth monitoring) and know the local data gaps.

## 3. Didactic spine

### A. INTERNAL drivers — what changes inside

**A1. The growth spurt reframes energy and protein needs.**
After the infant phase, linear growth is slow and steady through school-age, then the adolescent spurt re-accelerates it to a peak height velocity (PHV). Mean age at PHV is ~12.1 y (SD 1.4) in girls and ~13.7 y (SD 1.4) in boys — about two years later in boys (Granados 2015, for *timing only*). The classic magnitudes (PHV ~9 cm/y girls, ~10.3 cm/y boys; total pubertal gain ~25 cm girls / ~28 cm boys; ~13 cm adult-height sex difference) come from **Rogol 2000**, not Granados. Teaching frame: read a single height on the growth-*velocity* curve, not as a fixed number. The spurt is sequenced with pubertal (Tanner) stage — in girls PHV comes **early** (around breast stage 2–3, *before* menarche; ~70% of girls have passed PHV by menarche, so only ~5–7.5 cm of height typically remains after menarche), whereas in boys PHV comes **late** (around genital stage 4). So a girl in early breast development is at or near peak demand; a boy just starting genital development still has his biggest spurt ahead.

Sex hormones drive a **body-composition divergence**: testosterone expands lean/muscle mass and skeletal size in boys, oestrogen promotes fat accrual/redistribution in girls — a normal, hormone-programmed change, not over- or under-nutrition (Rogol 2000). The greater fat-free mass in boys is the main reason their energy needs rise higher. Give **direction, not a single hard percentage**. Critically, rising body fat in a pubertal girl is expected physiology and should not be pathologised.

Energy requirements rise steeply and split by sex (FAO/WHO/UNU 2004, moderate activity): boys ~1,575 kcal at 6–7 y → ~3,400 kcal at 17–18 y; girls ~1,425 → ~2,500 kcal. **Per-kg, energy needs FALL** across childhood (~305→210 kcal/kg boys) because body size grows faster than per-kg needs — a common point of confusion. Protein per-kg *also falls* gradually (≈1.0 g/kg/day at 5–14 y toward 0.8–0.9 g/kg/day by 16–18 y; FAO/WHO/UNU 1985 safe levels), while total daily grams rise simply because the teenager is bigger. This tempers the "teens need protein powder" narrative: ordinary diets that meet energy needs usually meet protein needs. The direct **energy "cost of growth"** (~2 kcal/g of tissue laid down) is a small line-item (~1–2% of total requirement, roughly doubling during the spurt) — most of the extra intake fuels a bigger body and more movement, not tissue-building per se.

The appetite surge is a **normal, hormonally coordinated signal** that tracks growth velocity: circulating ghrelin falls to its nadir around PHV and correlates inversely with height/weight velocity (Cheng 2018). Teaching hook: read appetite *against* growth velocity — a mid-pubertal teen eating markedly more is usually feeding a spurt. Intake that does **not** track a plausible growth trajectory (restriction with falling velocity, or rapid fat gain) is the flag. **Catch-up growth** (height velocity above normal after relief of a growth-inhibiting insult) demands extra energy/protein, but catch-up potential narrows with age and is much reduced past early childhood (Leroy 2020) — so growth faltering or downward centile-crossing in an older child is a **refer** signal, not an assumption of easy recovery.

**A2. The peak-bone-mass window.**
Adolescence is the decisive window for lifelong skeletal capital: ~26% of adult total-body bone mineral is accrued in the ~2 years around peak bone-mineral-content velocity (**Bailey 1999**), and ~39% is laid down in the ~4 years bracketing peak accretion (peak ~12.5 y girls, ~14.1 y boys), with ~95% of adult bone mass in place by ~4 years after the peak and peak bone mass reached by the end of the second/start of the third decade (**Baxter-Jones 2011**; **NOF/Weaver 2016**). *(Note the corrected framing: the "39% in 4 years" figure is from Weaver/Baxter-Jones — do NOT phrase it as "a quarter in 2 years bracketing PHV" cited to Weaver; the ~26%/2-year figure is Bailey's, tied to bone-mineral-content velocity.)* Only **20–40% of adult peak bone mass is lifestyle-modifiable**; the rest is heritable (Weaver 2016) — worthwhile to optimise, but not a lever that overrides genetics.

Grade-A evidence supports **physical activity (weight-bearing/high-impact) and calcium** for bone accrual; **dairy and vitamin D carry grade-B** (Weaver 2016). Exercise is most osteogenic in **early puberty** (all 8 early-pubertal trials positive, 1.1–5.5% gains; Hind & Burrows 2007), and active children out-accrue inactive peers (9% boys / 17% girls greater total-body BMC one year after peak; Bailey 1999). Two honest limits: **calcium supplementation** gives only a small, mostly upper-limb effect in *replete healthy* children that largely fades on stopping and is unlikely to change fracture risk at a public-health level (Winzenberg 2006; larger site-specific gains in the peri-peak 20–35 y group also fade past ~2 y, Liu 2022); **vitamin-D supplementation** does *not* help bone density in *replete* children — benefit appears only in the deficient (Winzenberg 2011). The message is **food-first calcium + outdoor weight-bearing activity + find-and-treat deficiency**, not blanket supplementation.

Mechanism (internal driver): rising estradiol (both sexes) accelerates accrual *and* eventually closes the epiphyses, so the window is hormone-gated and time-limited (Ackerman & Misra 2011). Hypoestrogenic states — functional hypothalamic amenorrhoea from low energy availability, or anorexia — blunt peak bone mass with lasting deficits (38% of amenorrhoeic athletes with spine Z<−1 vs 11%; Ackerman & Misra 2011). **Rigour check:** "miss this window and it can never be recovered" is an overstatement (bone continues to accrue modestly after final height) but directionally sound; peak bone mass is the dominant modelled determinant of later osteoporosis timing — a 10% higher peak BMD delays osteoporosis onset ~13 years (Hernandez 2003). **Do NOT attach a "10% peak bone mass = 50% fewer fractures" figure to Hernandez** — the model outputs *years of delay*, not fracture reduction.

**A3. Iron and the menstruating adolescent.**
Iron requirements rise across childhood to cover growth (expanding red-cell mass and tissue iron), then jump in girls at menarche as menstrual loss stacks on top of the spurt — absorbed-iron needs climb from ~0.7–0.9 mg/day pre-adolescence to ~2.2 mg/day (or more) in heavily menstruating young women (**Beard 2000** — the correct source for these absorbed-iron figures, not the LPI webpage). Menstruating girls are the highest-risk group, and risk multiplies when heavy bleeding coincides with a meat-restricted diet: 40% iron-deficient in a Swedish schoolgirl cohort, with odds rising for heavy bleeding alone (OR 3.0), meat-restriction alone (OR 3.5), and both together (OR 13.5) (Söderman 2025).

Functional consequences matter beyond haemoglobin: iron deficiency impairs cognition, and correcting it improves **intelligence (SMD 0.46), attention/concentration (SMD 0.44), and memory (SMD 0.44)** — but with **no effect on school achievement (SMD ~0.06, null)** (Gutema 2023; use the *corrected* figures). WHO frames iron-deficiency anaemia as a major public-health problem in menstruating girls/women (30%/539 million women 15–49 anaemic in 2019; fatigue, reduced physical work capacity, impaired school performance — **cite the WHO Anaemia fact sheet**, not the ELENA children-5to12 page). Iron deficiency **without anaemia** already lowers aerobic capacity and is common in adolescent athletes, especially females (~41% male / ~53% female mild ID; Nicotra 2023) — bridging to the sport lever.

Dietary bioavailability: **heme iron ~25% absorbed; non-heme ≤17%** (StatPearls "Dietary Iron", NBK540969 — the correct source for these specific percentages and the "meat factor"); vitamin C and the meat factor enhance non-heme absorption 2–3×, while phytates, polyphenols (tea/coffee) and calcium (dairy) inhibit it; **vegetarians need ~1.8× more iron** (NIH ODS, for the qualitative/vegetarian factor). Practical heuristics: pair non-heme sources with a vitamin-C food; separate tea/coffee and dairy from iron-rich meals. Who to screen/refer: the **2026 AAP clinical report** recommends universal CBC + serum ferritin screening for all adolescents ≥1 year post-menarche but no later than 14 y, then risk-based thereafter; iron deficiency = ferritin ≤20 ng/mL (young/school-age) and **≤30 ng/mL in adolescents/menstruating individuals** — screening on ferritin, not haemoglobin alone, catches ID *before* anaemia. (Ferritin is an acute-phase reactant — falsely normal in infection/inflammation.)

### B. EXTERNAL levers — what changes outside

**B1. SSBs, free sugars & dental.** WHO recommends **free sugars <10% of energy (strong)** and <5% (conditional), anchored on **dental caries** not weight (~50 g / <5% ≈ 25 g for a typical adult diet; WHO 2015 guideline — use the ISBN'd guideline, not the news-release, for the 50 g figure and evidence grading). SSBs are causally linked to greater weight gain in children in two double-blind/masked RCTs (de Ruyter 2012: ~1.02 kg less over 18 months; Ebbeling 2012: −1.9 kg at 1 y but **NS at the 2-year primary endpoint**), with a modest per-serving association (+0.06 BMI/serving/day; Malik 2013). The effect is largely **displaced energy, not sugar per se** (isoenergetic swaps → no weight change; Te Morenga 2013). Frame SSBs as a life-stage red flag to recognise, not "sugar toxicity."

**B2. Food marketing.** Acute ad exposure increases children's (not adults') food intake (Boyland 2016); broader marketing is associated with higher intake (SMD 0.25), choice, preference and purchase requests (Boyland 2022) — prompting **WHO's 2023 call for mandatory, comprehensive HFSS-marketing restrictions** for children up to 18 (WHO 2023; formally a conditional recommendation on very-low-certainty evidence). Population/policy lever — clinicians *recognise* the environment, they don't treat it.

**B3. Family meals & parental modelling.** ≥3 shared family meals/week associate with better diet quality, healthier weight and less disordered eating (Hammons & Fiese 2011: 12%/24%/20%/35% odds); adolescent family-meal frequency predicts lower overweight odds a decade later, surviving some temporal ordering (Berge 2015). Almost entirely observational and confounded (reverse causation plausible) — **encourage as a low-risk, plausibly beneficial practice**, not a proven treatment.

**B4. Physical activity & youth sport.** WHO 2020 and Singapore's Integrated 24-Hour Activity Guidelines: average **≥60 min/day MVPA** across the week plus muscle/bone-strengthening ≥3 days/week (Bull 2020; SG 24-h Guidelines 2021). Singapore youth fall short (Overall PA graded C−; Tay 2023). Young athletes must be **fuelled, not over-restricted**: chronic low energy availability drives **REDs / the female athlete triad** — bone, menstrual and performance harms (Mountjoy 2023 IOC; Weiss Kelly & Hecht 2016 AAP: one triad component should prompt evaluation for the others; Thomas 2016: adequate energy availability is the foundation). **Recognise and refer** the under-fuelled adolescent with a stress fracture or amenorrhoea; detailed programming belongs to the Adulthood chapter.

**B5. Screen time & sedentary behaviour.** Higher screen time associates with obesity (OR 1.47 highest-vs-lowest, +13%/hour; Zhang 2016, cross-sectional), and community RCTs *reducing* screen time reduced weight gain — but much of the effect runs through **other levers**: eating while viewing, HFSS-marketing exposure, and reduced sleep (Robinson 2017). Present as interlinked, not a standalone cause.

**B6. Food autonomy & peers.** Adolescence is the pivot from parent-controlled to self-directed, peer-influenced eating (Story, Neumark-Sztainer & French 2002, ecological/social-cognitive model). As teens gain autonomy they eat more meals away from home, skip meals, and are more swayed by peers and advertising — so the family-meal lever weakens and the SSB/marketing/school-environment levers strengthen. This developmental shift is the organising logic linking all external levers.

### C. Where a life-stage need tips into a CLINICAL problem

**C1. Disordered-eating onset & do-no-harm.** Adolescence is the **peak onset window**: US lifetime prevalence AN 0.3%, BN 0.9%, BED 1.6%, with **median onset 12.3–12.6 y** (Swanson 2011). **Dieting is a prospective risk factor**, not a harmless habit: severe dieters were 18× and moderate dieters 5× more likely to develop a new ED (Patton 1999) — though a fair interpretation is "dieting flags/sits on the causal pathway," not sole cause. Dieting also **backfires on weight**: adolescent dieters gained *more* weight and binged more over 5 years (Neumark-Sztainer 2007) — uniting both tails under one do-no-harm banner. The clinician framework (AAP, Golden 2016): focus on **healthy habits not weight/dieting; encourage family meals; discourage weight talk/teasing** — the same messages prevent *both* obesity and EDs. Weight teasing predicts disordered eating (Haines 2006 — corrected authorship). **Red flags / refer** (Hornberger 2021 AAP): growth/weight-curve deviation, restricting/purging/binge/food-avoidance, amenorrhoea, stress fractures, hypokalaemia, bradycardia (HR <50); screen at every annual visit; EDs occur **across the weight spectrum**. **ARFID** (DSM-5) — sensory aversion, low interest, fear of aversive consequences, *without* weight/shape concern; younger, more often male, high anxiety/ASD comorbidity — is where "picky eating" tips clinical (Sanchez-Cerezo 2023); it seams with the Infancy chapter. **Orthorexia** ("clean eating" taken to a pathological extreme) is a real pattern relevant to social media but **not a formal DSM-5/ICD-11 diagnosis**; prevalence figures are unreliable (Horovitz & Argyrides 2023 — corrected authorship). Recognise the pattern; refer if functionally impairing.

**C2. Childhood obesity & emerging paediatric T2D (the other tail).** Childhood obesity **tracks into adulthood** — persistence is at least "moderate" and rises with degree of overweight and age (Singh 2008; note the review gave *no* single pooled figure — do not attribute "55–79%" to Singh). Paediatric **type-2 diabetes is emerging** downstream of adolescent obesity in Singapore, tied to rising childhood obesity (NUH patient-education resource — indicative, not a registry figure). **Recognise the trajectory and cardiometabolic risk; refer.** Management belongs to the obesity and diabetes chapters.

### When to refer — quick synthesis
Growth faltering / downward centile-crossing • suspected iron deficiency (fatigue, poor concentration, pica, heavy periods, meat-restricted diet — screen ferritin) • the under-fuelled athlete (amenorrhoea, stress/recurrent fractures, declining performance) • any ED red flag *across the weight spectrum* (including rapid weight loss in a formerly heavier teen) • the obesity→cardiometabolic trajectory.

## 5. Singapore context

**School food environment — HMSP.** The Healthy Meals in Schools Programme (launched ~2011, now near-universal across mainstream primary/secondary/JC/CI) is the single biggest lever on the school food environment a Singapore teen navigates: canteen vendors must cut fat/sugar/sodium, serve wholegrains (≥20% unpolished rice, wholegrain noodles), daily fruit and vegetables, keep plain water always available, and discourage SSBs (hpb_hmsp_page). These are vendor *requirements*, not measured student outcomes. *(The HMSP-guidelines PDF URL is dead — link the HPB programme page or the archived v2.0 PDF; do not use the HealthHub pre-schools mirror, which is a different document.)*

**The TAF cautionary tale → Holistic Health Framework.** Singapore's earlier school anti-obesity programme, TAF ("Trim And Fit," ~1992–2007), reduced measured childhood obesity but is the canonical local lesson in *why we screen weight without shaming*: heavier children were singled out (the acronym reads "FAT" backwards) and teased, and a peer-reviewed follow-up found **10.3% of anorexia-nervosa patients had participated in TAF before onset** (Kuek 2015). TAF was replaced in 2007 by the whole-school **Holistic Health Framework**. Frame this as a policy-and-ethics narrative — the *causal* "TAF caused eating disorders" claim is not established (cross-sectional, ecological). Cite Foo 2013 for the trend and framework shift and Kuek 2015 for the ED link; use the Wikipedia entry only for timeline colour. *(The MOE HHF marketing page does not narrate any of this — do not cite it for the TAF story.)*

**Obesity trends.** Overweight/severely-overweight (BMI-for-age ≥90th pct) among children 6–18 y rose from **11% (2013) to 13% (2017)** (MOH 2020) and stood at **~13% in 2024** (MOH 2025). Note the definition (≥90th pct "overweight+severely overweight," *not* the international ≥95th-pct "obesity") and do not chain differently-defined figures into a single trend line or label them "obesity." Recognise the trajectory; management is the obesity/diabetes chapters. Paediatric T2D is described locally as "emerging" (NUH resource — indicative only).

**Nutri-Grade & the bubble-tea environment.** Nutri-Grade — a world-first mandatory front-of-pack A–D beverage label with a **grade-D advertising ban** — directly targets the drinks teens buy, extended to **freshly-prepared beverages including bubble tea from 30 Dec 2023** under the War on Diabetes (hpbNutriGrade; mohNutriGradeFresh2023). Its demonstrated effect is honestly **modest** (~1.5 g less sugar/serving, no overall diet-quality change in a pre-rollout RCT; Shin 2023). Local diet data: ~59% of children 6–12 exceed added-sugar guidance (Brownlee 2019 — for the *intake* point, not as a Nutri-Grade policy anchor), and sugary-drink habits are shaped bidirectionally within families (Lwin 2025, measuring intentions not frequency), so family-centred intervention makes sense.

**Adolescent iron / calcium / vitamin-D status.** HPB anchors: iron RDA 7 mg school-age → **18–19 mg for adolescent girls vs 6–12 mg for boys** (a near-tripling at menarche, the clearest local teaching figure for the vulnerable group), calcium 1,000 mg (10–18 y), and vitamin D set unusually low at **2.5 µg/100 IU (7–18 y)** on the tropical-sun assumption (hpb_healthhub_rda). That assumption sits in tension with regional evidence that vitamin-D deficiency is common despite the sun (indoor lifestyles, sun avoidance, phototype; Nimitphong & Holick 2013), and with local intake data showing calcium ~66–83% of RDA and only 6.25% of children meeting vitamin-D recommendations (Choy 2021). **Genuine local data gap:** direct Singapore *adolescent* iron/vitamin-D/calcium *status* figures are sparse — the NNS 2010 calcium figure (~794 mg) is adult, and NNS 2022 covers ages 18–69. Flag the gap or lean on regional/international data with an explicit caveat.

**Physical activity.** Only **~23.7% of 13–17-year-olds** meet ≥60 min/day MVPA (2015 Student Health Survey, in Tay 2023; Overall PA & Sedentary Behaviour both graded C−). The new **Grow Well SG** (2025, MOH/MOE/MSF) is the whole-of-government child-health strategy — cite it for the initiative, not for the activity statistic.

**Disordered eating in SG youth.** 7.4% of schoolgirls screen "at risk," with **Malay girls over-represented** (Ho 2006). Presentations are **rising and diagnosed younger** (Wong 2022: 85% AN, mean dx 14.6 y, FBT lifting 1-year remission 30%→79%; Lee 2024: AN cases <15/yr in the 1990s → peak 109 in 2022). Crucially, EDs occur **across the weight spectrum**: ~31% of restrictive-ED presentations were atypical AN and 32% premorbidly overweight (Chew 2023) — a formerly-heavier teen who loses weight fast can be seriously ill, so praising weight loss can mask an emerging ED.

## 6. Common misconceptions

1. **"Teens need protein powder to fuel the growth spurt."** Protein *per kg* actually falls through adolescence toward the adult ~0.8 g/kg/day; total grams rise only because the teen is bigger. Diets meeting energy needs usually meet protein needs (FAO/WHO/UNU 1985).
2. **"A pubertal girl gaining body fat is over-eating / heading for obesity."** Oestrogen-driven fat accrual is normal, hormone-programmed physiology — read appetite and body change against growth velocity, not as pathology (Rogol 2000; Cheng 2018).
3. **"Growth energy explains a teenager's huge appetite."** The direct energy cost of laying down tissue is only ~1–2% of total requirement; most extra intake fuels a larger body and more movement (FAO/WHO/UNU 2004).
4. **"Menarche means she's basically finished growing / iron needs are now like an adult's."** Menarche marks the *tail* of height gain, but menstrual loss stacked on the spurt makes the menstruating adolescent girl the *highest*-iron-need group (HPB RDA jumps 7→18 mg; Beard 2000; Söderman 2025).
5. **"Calcium/vitamin-D supplements will build strong bones in any child."** In *replete* children, calcium supplements give only a small, fading, mostly non-hip/spine effect, and vitamin-D supplements add nothing — benefit is confined to the *deficient* (Winzenberg 2006, 2011). Food-first + treat deficiency, not blanket supplementation.
6. **"Miss the bone window and it's lost forever" / "10% more peak bone mass halves fracture risk."** The window is hard (not literally impossible) to make up, and the model output is a **~13-year delay in osteoporosis onset**, not a 50% fracture reduction (Hernandez 2003).
7. **"Telling an overweight teen to diet is harmless, sensible advice."** Dieting prospectively predicts *both* new eating disorders (18×/5×; Patton 1999) and *more* weight gain (Neumark-Sztainer 2007). Focus on healthy habits, family meals, and no weight-talk (Golden 2016).
8. **"You can rule out an eating disorder if the teen's weight/BMI is normal or high."** EDs span the whole weight spectrum; ~31% of SG restrictive-ED presentations were atypical AN, a third premorbidly overweight (Chew 2023).

## 8. Proposed interactive / quiz / case

### Bespoke island — "The Bone-Bank Window" (interactive accrual visualiser)
An interactive that prose cannot convey: a **stacked "bone bank" that fills over time** against a pubertal timeline. The learner drags a slider for **age (8→25)** and toggles three levers — *weight-bearing activity*, *calcium adequacy*, and *vitamin-D/deficiency status* — and watches (a) the accrual curve steepen through the ~4-year circumpubertal window, (b) the curve **plateau at ~18–20 y** (total body) with the spine plateauing later, and (c) the **modifiable band (20–40%)** visibly widen or narrow within genetically-set upper/lower bounds. A second toggle, "low energy availability / amenorrhoea," *lowers the ceiling and shades a permanent deficit*, teaching the hormone-gated window and the recognise-and-refer flag. Honest-limit callouts fire when the learner over-supplements a *replete* avatar (no extra bone) vs a *deficient* one (real gain). This makes the "window closing," the modifiable-vs-heritable partition, and the do-no-harm supplement message tangible in a way a paragraph can't. *(Grounding: Bailey 1999; Baxter-Jones 2011; Weaver 2016; Winzenberg 2006/2011; Ackerman & Misra 2011.)*

*Alternative island:* a **stage→need matcher** — drag Tanner-stage/velocity cards to their peak-demand nutrient (girl early breast stage → peak iron/energy; boy genital stage 2 → "biggest spurt still ahead"), reinforcing "stage not age."

### MCQ quiz topics (5–8 items)
1. Reading a single height point on a **velocity** curve (spurt is transient).
2. Why **per-kg** energy/protein needs *fall* while total needs rise.
3. Identifying the **menstruating adolescent girl** as highest iron-deficiency risk, and screening on **ferritin** not haemoglobin.
4. WHO free-sugars threshold and its **dental-caries** (not weight) anchor.
5. The **do-no-harm** fork: which response to a heavier teen is safe vs harmful.
6. Recognising an **under-fuelled athlete** (amenorrhoea + stress fracture → refer, don't restrict further).
7. Distinguishing **ARFID** (no weight/shape concern) from AN.
8. Interpreting the SG vitamin-D RDA tension (low RDA + tropical-sun assumption vs real deficiency).

### MCQ-only branching case (recognise-and-refer, with a do-no-harm fork)
**"Rachel, 14."** A 14-year-old girl is brought by her mother, worried Rachel "has put on weight" and "should go on a diet." Rachel plays competitive netball, trains 5×/week, and her mother mentions her periods "stopped a few months ago." She looks fit; BMI is in the normal range.

- **Node 1 (the do-no-harm fork):** *What is your first move?*
  - (A) Reassure and give Rachel a calorie-restricted meal plan to "tone up." → **harmful branch**: teaches that dieting advice in this context is a prospective risk for both ED and weight gain (Patton 1999; Neumark-Sztainer 2007), and that a normal BMI does not equal safety (Chew 2023). Loop back with feedback.
  - (B) Take a focused history of training load, energy intake, and the amenorrhoea; ask about stress/injury. → **correct branch**.
- **Node 2:** History reveals she has been "eating clean," cut out rice and most snacks to "stay lean for netball," and had a foot stress fracture last season. *What does this pattern suggest?* → recognise **low energy availability / female-athlete-triad** territory (amenorrhoea + stress fracture + restriction + high training load).
- **Node 3 (when-to-refer):** *What is the appropriate action for a first-year clinician?* → **Refer** (do NOT prescribe a diet, do NOT attempt triad/ED work-up or bone-density interpretation yourself); frame the message as *fuel, don't restrict*; flag the threat to the peak-bone-mass window. Distractor options (start a calcium supplement and review in 6 months; tell her to "just eat more and it'll sort itself out") reinforce the boundary that management belongs to specialists.

Each terminal node carries a one-line "why this matters" tied to a verified source; the harmful branch is recoverable (feedback + retry), never a dead end.
