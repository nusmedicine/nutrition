# Energy Balance and Metabolism — research dossier

## 1. Scope (one paragraph)
This chapter builds the quantitative and conceptual backbone that the rest of the nutrition curriculum stands on: how the body spends energy, what drives those costs, and how chronic mismatches between intake and expenditure move body weight over time. First-year students learn that total energy expenditure (TEE) is the sum of basal metabolic rate (BMR), the thermic effect of food (TEF), and physical activity (PA); that BMR is dominated by lean (fat-free) mass with smaller contributions from age and sex; and that energy requirements can be *estimated* — never measured precisely without indirect calorimetry — using the Mifflin–St Jeor equation scaled by an activity factor. Students learn to read energy balance as a dynamic system (the body adapts, so simple "3500 kcal = 1 lb" arithmetic over-predicts), and to connect energy *density* of foods to how easily surpluses accumulate. The chapter is deliberately role-and-estimation focused, not pathway-focused: students should understand *what each compartment is and what moves it*, not the biochemistry of ATP yield (signposted to the Macronutrients/biochemistry chapter). Singapore framing anchors the applied points — HPB/SportSG activity guidelines, the hawker-centre food environment, and the National Nutrition Survey 2022 — so estimation skills land in a recognisable local context.

## 2. Learning objectives (refined)
Original drafts → refined, measurable, first-year-level objectives:

1. **Define energy balance** as the relationship between energy intake and total energy expenditure, and explain *qualitatively* how sustained imbalance changes body energy stores (originally "Describe energy balance and determinants of energy requirements").
2. **Decompose TEE into its three components** (BMR + TEF + PA), state the approximate share each contributes in a typical adult, and identify the main drivers of each (originally part of objectives 1 and 4).
3. **Identify the determinants of BMR** — fat-free (lean) mass as the dominant driver, with secondary effects of age and sex — and explain *why* (lean mass is metabolically active tissue) (refined from "metabolism basics").
4. **Estimate an individual's energy needs** by computing BMR with the Mifflin–St Jeor equation and multiplying by an appropriate physical-activity factor, *and* state the equation's ~±10% individual-level uncertainty (originally "Estimate energy needs (BMR via Mifflin-St Jeor x activity)").
5. **Relate an energy surplus or deficit to body-weight change** over time, while recognising the body's adaptive response (so the change is *less than* naïve linear arithmetic predicts) (refined from "Relate energy surplus/deficit to body weight").
6. **Explain energy density** and how high-energy-density foods make surpluses easier to accumulate, linking this to local food choices (new — supports the "energy density" core topic).

## 3. Scope boundaries
**In:**
- TEE = BMR + TEF + PA, and the approximate proportional contribution of each.
- BMR drivers: fat-free mass (dominant), age, sex; the concept that organs/muscle are metabolically active.
- TEF concept (~10% of intake on a mixed diet, within a 5–15% range; protein highest, fat lowest) — at the level of "what it is and roughly how big," not mechanism.
- Mifflin–St Jeor equation and activity (PAL) factors as an *estimation* tool, with explicit uncertainty.
- Energy balance as a dynamic system; surplus/deficit → weight change with adaptation caveat.
- Energy density of foods and its practical relevance.
- Singapore anchoring: HPB/SportSG physical-activity guidelines, hawker-food examples, NNS 2022 context.

## 4. Key concepts to teach (didactic spine — ordered)
1. **Energy is conserved; the body is an open energy system.** Intake (food/drink) vs. expenditure (TEE); the *balance* sets the direction of change in body energy stores. (First-law framing, no thermodynamics depth.) → general physiology; `who2020diet` for intake context.
2. **TEE = BMR + TEF + PA.** Introduce the three compartments and their typical shares: BMR ≈ 60–70%, PA ≈ 15–30% (the most variable), TEF ≈ ~10%. → `mifflin1990`, `westerterp2004tef`, `fao2004energy`.
3. **BMR is the largest, most stable compartment, and lean mass is its main driver.** Fat-free mass explains most BMR variance (≈63% of between-subject variance in Johnstone 2005); age lowers it modestly, and sex differences are *largely explained by* body-composition differences (men carry more lean mass). → `johnstone2005bmr`, `mifflin1990`.
4. **TEF — the cost of processing food.** ~10% of intake on a mixed diet (5–15% range); protein has the highest thermic effect (~20–30%), carbohydrate intermediate (~5–10%), fat lowest (~0–3%). Teach as a *small, roughly fixed* term, not a weight-loss lever. → `westerterp2004tef`.
5. **PA is the swing factor.** The compartment people can actually change day-to-day; ranges from a sedentary multiplier to a very active one. → `fao2004energy`, HPB/SportSG activity guidance.
6. **Estimating energy needs: Mifflin–St Jeor × activity factor.** Walk through the equation by sex, then scale by a PAL/activity factor (≈1.2 sedentary → ≈1.7+ very active). Stress: this is an *estimate*, accurate to within ~±10% in most people (and within 10% of measured RMR in the majority of subjects in validation studies), less reliable in older adults and under-represented ethnic groups. → `mifflin1990`, `frankenfield2005`, `fao2004energy`.
7. **Energy balance is dynamic, not static.** A surplus or deficit shifts weight, but the body adapts (expenditure changes with body size and metabolic adaptation), so naïve "3500 kcal per pound" linear arithmetic *over-predicts*. Use the 100-kcal/day example as an order-of-magnitude illustration, then add the caveat. → `hall2013_3500`.
8. **Energy density connects food choice to balance.** Energy-dense foods (high fat/refined carbohydrate, low water/fibre) deliver more kcal per bite, making surpluses easier to accumulate; water- and fibre-rich foods are less energy-dense. → `who2020diet`, `hpbHealthyPlate`.
9. **Caveat on lifespan metabolism (teach the nuance).** Fat-free-mass-adjusted expenditure is actually *stable* through mid-adulthood (~20–60 y) and declines later — challenging the "metabolism inevitably crashes at 30" folk belief. (Observational, doubly-labelled-water data; teach the finding, not a mechanism.) → `pontzer2021`.

## 6. Singapore context
- **Singapore Physical Activity Guidelines (SportSG / HPB, 2022):** adults 18–64 should accumulate **150–300 minutes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity per week** plus muscle/bone strengthening **≥2 days/week**, with explicit messaging to *reduce and break up sedentary time*. Use this to ground the PA compartment and the choice of an activity factor in Mdm Tan's estimate — and to make the "PA is the swing factor you can change" point locally credible. (`hpbPAG2022`)
- **My Healthy Plate (QQH):** ¼ wholegrains, ¼ protein, ½ fruit & vegetables — a low-energy-density plate template; contrast a half-plate of vegetables (low energy density) with the same plate dominated by fried carbohydrate. (`hpbHealthyPlate`)
- **National Nutrition Survey 2022:** only ~4% of staples are wholegrain and ~9 in 10 residents exceed sodium recommendations — useful to frame the *food environment* in which energy surpluses accumulate (refined staples are energy-dense and easy to over-consume). (`mohNNS2022`)
- **Nutri-Grade beverages (2022/23):** sugar-sweetened drinks are concentrated liquid energy with low satiety — a clean, locally salient example of energy density and "drinking your surplus." Grade D (>10 g sugar/100 ml) bubble tea / kopi-c-gao-siu-dai contrasts. (`hpbNutriGrade`)
- **Hawker-food worked examples (for estimation/energy-density teaching):** approximate energy values — *treat as placeholders and confirm against HPB's* Energy & Nutrient Composition of Food *database before publication; date each figure honestly:*
  - Chicken rice ~600 kcal; char kway teow ~740 kcal; laksa ~600–700 kcal; nasi lemak (with fried chicken) ~600–800 kcal; teh tarik / kopi (with condensed milk) ~120–170 kcal each.
  - Teaching use: a single Grade-D drink + a fried hawker dish vs. a half-vegetable plate illustrates how *energy density and liquid calories* — not gluttony — drive a quiet daily surplus.

## 7. Pillar intersections
- **Nutrition × physical activity (fuelling and expenditure).** This is the spine of the chapter: PA is the controllable expenditure compartment, and the SportSG/HPB 150–300 min/wk target operationalises it. The estimation worked example (BMR × activity factor) is exactly where the two pillars meet — students see that the same person's energy *need* moves with their activity level, and that adding activity widens the room to eat nutritiously without surplus. (Cross-reference the Physical Activity pillar chapter for prescription detail.)

## 8. Clinical application & case ideas (case-player-ready)

### Case A — "Estimating Mdm Tan's energy needs" (already built; extend into a branching estimator)
**Stem:** Mdm Tan, 58, Singaporean Chinese, recently retired, 62 kg, 158 cm, mostly sedentary (light housework, occasional walks). She asks the student-clinician, "How much should I be eating?"

- **Node 1 — Compute BMR (Mifflin–St Jeor, female):** 10(62) + 6.25(158) − 5(58) − 161 = 620 + 987.5 − 290 − 161 = **~1,157 kcal/day**.
  - *Branch — student forgets the female −161 offset / uses the male +5:* feedback flags the sex term and why it exists (body-composition difference, not a separate "female metabolism").
- **Node 2 — Choose an activity factor:** sedentary ≈ 1.2 → TEE ≈ **~1,388 kcal/day**.
  - *Branch — student picks 1.55 ("she walks sometimes"):* prompt to reconcile with the 150–300 min/wk threshold; occasional walks ≠ moderately active. Teaches that activity factors are honest self-classification, not aspiration.
- **Node 3 — State the uncertainty:** student must add the ~±10% caveat (≈1,250–1,530 kcal) and note that older adults and Asian populations are under-represented in the equation's original validation → estimate, then adjust by monitoring weight trend over weeks. (`frankenfield2005`)
- **Node 4 — Apply to a plan:** translate into a My Healthy Plate pattern and one practical swap (e.g., kopi-o instead of kopi-c, or adding a half-plate of vegetables). Reinforces energy density over calorie-counting.

### Case B — "The 100 kcal/day surplus over a year" (build the dynamic-balance intuition)
**Stem:** Mr Lim, 35, adds one teh tarik (~120 kcal) to his daily routine and changes nothing else.

- **Node 1 — Naïve linear prediction:** 100 kcal/day × 365 ≈ 36,500 kcal ÷ ~7,700 kcal/kg fat ≈ **~4.7 kg/year**. Student computes it.
- **Node 2 — The caveat (the teaching point):** ask "will he really gain ~4.7 kg every year indefinitely?" → **No.** As body mass rises, BMR and the cost of moving rise too, so expenditure climbs to meet intake and weight *plateaus* at a new (higher) steady state — typically a *fraction* of the naïve prediction in the first year, then levelling off. The 3500-kcal/lb (≈7,700 kcal/kg) rule is a *static* approximation that over-predicts because it ignores this dynamic adaptation. (`hall2013_3500`)
- **Node 3 — Direction of the lesson:** small, *sustained* changes still matter (the new plateau is higher) — but the clinically honest message is "a small daily surplus nudges your steady-state weight up; reversing it also adapts," not "every biscuit is 5 kg."
  - *Branch — student over-promises rapid loss from a 100-kcal deficit:* feedback applies the same adaptation logic symmetrically.
- **Node 4 — Local action:** identify the surplus source (liquid Grade-D calories) and a low-friction swap; tie back to energy density.

## 9. Common misconceptions & pitfalls
- **"Metabolism crashes at 30."** FFM-adjusted TEE is roughly *stable* from ~20 to ~60 y; the decline is later and modest (observational doubly-labelled-water data). Mid-life weight gain is more about reduced activity and intake creep than a metabolic collapse. (`pontzer2021`)
- **"3500 kcal = exactly 1 lb, so cutting 500 kcal/day = 1 lb/week forever."** Linear and static, and over-predicts over time — the body adapts; real loss decelerates and plateaus. (`hall2013_3500`)
- **"Eating more protein torches calories via TEF, so you can eat more."** TEF is real and protein's is higher, but it's a *small* (~10% total) term — not a meaningful weight-loss lever. Don't oversell it. (`westerterp2004tef`)
- **"BMR equations give the true number."** They estimate to ~±10% and are weaker in older adults and under-represented ethnic groups (relevant to Singapore's multi-ethnic population). Treat the number as a starting estimate, then track the weight trend. (`frankenfield2005`)
- **"Women have inherently slower metabolisms."** Sex differences in BMR are largely explained by lean-mass differences, not a separate female penalty. (`johnstone2005bmr`)
- **"A calorie of soda and a calorie of vegetables behave the same for weight."** True for the energy ledger in a closed bomb calorimeter, but *not* for satiety and intake regulation — energy density and liquid calories drive over-consumption. (Energy-density framing; `who2020diet`, `hpbNutriGrade`.)
- **Activity-factor inflation.** Students/patients routinely pick too high a factor; anchor to the concrete 150–300 min/wk threshold. (`hpbPAG2022`)

## 10. Proposed interactive elements
- **Quiz topics:**
  1. Order the macronutrients by thermic effect (protein > carbohydrate > fat).
  2. MCQ: which compartment is *largest* vs. *most variable*? (BMR largest; PA most variable.)
  3. Compute Mifflin–St Jeor for a given vignette and select the correct activity factor.
  4. True/false: "Sex difference in BMR persists after adjusting for lean mass." (False, largely.)
  5. Critique a statement using the 3500-kcal rule (identify the static-arithmetic flaw).
- **Diagram idea(s):**
  - A stacked-bar "energy budget" showing TEE split into BMR / TEF / PA for a sedentary vs. active person — same BMR, PA bar grows, illustrating the swing factor.
  - An "energy-density ruler" placing hawker items (vegetables → chicken rice → char kway teow → Grade-D bubble tea) along kcal-per-100 g, with water/fibre annotations.
- **Case (interactive estimator):** the Mdm Tan branching estimator from §8A, with live computation, the ±10% uncertainty band rendered visually, and an activity-factor selector tied to the SportSG/HPB thresholds.

## 11. Evidence gaps / open questions
- **Local energy-value accuracy:** hawker-dish kcal figures in §6 are approximate placeholders; verify each against HPB's *Energy & Nutrient Composition of Food* database before publishing, and date them honestly.
- **Equation validity in Asian populations:** Mifflin–St Jeor was derived largely in Western cohorts (and validated mainly in non-elderly adults); its accuracy in Singaporean Chinese/Malay/Indian adults and in older adults is under-characterised — flag as a limitation rather than asserting precision.
- **Singapore activity-attainment data:** confirm the latest National Population Health Survey figure for the proportion of adults meeting the 150–300 min/wk target before stating any number (not yet sourced to a verified current statistic).
- **Hall & Chow 2013 exact locator:** title/journal/year verified (Int J Obes 2013; nature.com/articles/ijo2013112); confirm the exact volume/issue/page before adding the BibTeX entry to the shared library (commonly cited as 37(12):1614).
- **TEF inter-individual variability:** the ~10% figure is a population average within a 5–15% range (Westerterp); individual and meal-composition variability is large and a niche research area — keep at first-year depth and signpost.
- **Pontzer 2021 interpretation:** robust and widely cited, but observational (doubly-labelled water); teach the finding with its design caveat and avoid over-claiming mechanism.
