## 1. Scope (one paragraph)

This chapter teaches the healthy older adult (roughly **65+**) — ageing **in health**, anticipating the normal way nutritional needs shift with age — for a first-year medical student, the FINAL chapter of Part II "Across the life stages" (after *Adulthood* and *Menopause & Midlife*). Its altitude is **recognise the nutritional levers, advise simply, and refer** — NOT manage frailty or malnutrition clinically. Built on the Part II **internal↔external driver template**, it runs: (1) the counter-intuitive **internal mismatch** — total energy needs fall (lower lean mass/activity, "anorexia of ageing" cutting intake ~16–20%) while **protein and micronutrient needs per calorie RISE**, so the plate must become more *nutrient-dense*, not just smaller; (2) **anabolic resistance & sarcopenia** at recognition level — ageing muscle answers a protein dose more weakly, so daily protein rises to ~1.0–1.2 g/kg/day and per-meal protein matters, paired with resistance exercise; (3) the age-specific **micronutrient watch-list** — falling B12 absorption (food-cobalamin malabsorption), deficiency-prone vitamin D, rising calcium requirement against a wide local intake gap, and blunted thirst/hydration; (4) the **appetite / dentition / swallowing** decline (chemosensory loss, poor dentition, dysphagia, polypharmacy); then (5) the **external** drivers — social isolation, eating alone, bereavement, and access/finances/mobility to shop and cook — countered by communal eating and community meal programmes. Finally it teaches **where healthy ageing tips into undernutrition** (recognise unintentional weight loss ≥5% and refer) and the crucial **do-no-harm** message: do not reflexively put a thin, frail older adult on a low-fat/low-salt/weight-loss "diet." The **decision-C boundary** is strict — the pathological deficiency state (screening tools, the MDT, oral nutritional supplements, enteral/parenteral routes, refeeding) belongs to the *Undernutrition & Malnutrition* chapter.

## 2. Learning objectives

By the end, a student can…

- [ ] **LO1 — Explain the internal mismatch of ageing:** that energy requirements fall while protein and micronutrient requirements per calorie rise, so the correct response is a *nutrient-dense* plate ("energy down, density up"), not simply "eat less."
- [ ] **LO2 — Recognise anabolic resistance and sarcopenia at recognition level:** why healthy older adults need more protein (~1.0–1.2 g/kg/day), why per-meal protein and pairing with resistance exercise matter, and what sarcopenia is (low muscle strength + low quantity/quality) — deferring diagnosis/management to later years.
- [ ] **LO3 — Name the age-specific micronutrient and hydration watch-list:** who to watch for low B12 (atrophic gastritis, long-term metformin/PPI, poor intake, vegetarians), why vitamin D is deficiency-prone even in sunny Singapore, the rising calcium requirement, and why blunted thirst makes proactive hydration a lever — cross-referring the underlying biology to Part I.
- [ ] **LO4 — Identify the appetite / dentition / swallowing / medication drivers** of reduced intake (anorexia of ageing, chemosensory loss, poor dentition, dysphagia red flags, polypharmacy) and the *external* drivers (social isolation, eating alone, bereavement, mobility/finances/access), and advise simple food-first mitigations (fortify familiar foods, share meals).
- [ ] **LO5 — Recognise when ageing-in-health tips into undernutrition** (unintentional weight loss ≥5% over 6–12 months, sustained reduced intake, dysphagia signs) and REFER — while applying the do-no-harm rule that dietary restriction should generally be avoided in frail older adults.
- [ ] **LO6 — Situate the advice in the Singapore context:** HPB Dietary Guidelines for Older Adults, the local protein/calcium intake gaps, fortifying familiar local dishes, and active-ageing/community-meal referral endpoints, in a super-ageing population.

## 3. Key concepts to teach (didactic spine)

The spine runs **INTERNAL drivers → EXTERNAL drivers → the tip into UNDERNUTRITION + do-no-harm**.

### A. INTERNAL — the counter-intuitive mismatch (energy DOWN, protein/density UP)

1. **Appetite and energy intake fall with age ("anorexia of ageing") — but micronutrient needs do not fall in step.** Healthy older adults eat ~16–20% less energy than younger adults (hunger ~25% lower fasting, ~39% lower postprandial; fullness ~37% greater). *(`giezenaar2016appetite` — for the intake/appetite magnitude ONLY.)* Meanwhile micronutrient dietary recommendations for older persons **do not differ** from younger adults, and energy needs fall mainly through declining fat-free mass — so the same nutrients must be met from fewer calories. *(`espenGeriatrics2019`)* **Teaching frame:** "energy down, nutrient density up" is the whole chapter in four words.

2. **PROTEIN needs go UP, not down.** Healthy older adults need **≥1.0–1.2 g/kg/day** (vs the 0.8 g/kg adult RDA), ≥1.2 g/kg for the active, and 1.2–1.5 g/kg in acute/chronic illness — with ONE red-flag caveat: severe non-dialysis kidney disease (eGFR <30) may need protein *limited*. *(`bauer2013protage`)* ESPEN's expert group independently endorses the same 1.0–1.2 g/kg target and adds the headline pairing: **protein + exercise together** is optimal for muscle. *(`deutz2014espen`)* Because targets are per kg on a *lower* energy budget, protein must come from more nutrient-dense choices — the density theme again.

3. **ANABOLIC RESISTANCE is the mechanism behind the higher target.** Ageing muscle mounts a blunted protein-synthesis response to a given dose (reduced amino-acid/leucine sensitivity, dampened mTOR signalling), so more protein *per meal* is needed to trigger synthesis. *(`breen2011anabolic` — mechanism only)* Pooled dose-response data quantify it: maximal myofibrillar synthesis needed **~0.40 g/kg/meal in older vs ~0.24 g/kg in younger men** — for a 70 kg person, ~28 g protein per meal. *(`moore2015anabolic`)*

4. **PER-MEAL DISTRIBUTION is a pragmatic lever.** Spreading protein evenly (~25–30 g/meal) gave ~25% higher 24-h muscle protein synthesis than skewing it to dinner. *(`mamerow2014distribution`)* **Caveat to state in text:** this trial was in healthy *middle-aged* adults (~37 y) and some older-adult distribution trials are null — so teach "hit the per-meal threshold" as mechanistically grounded (anabolic resistance) and pragmatic, not as settled elderly-outcome evidence.

5. **SARCOPENIA at recognition level = low muscle STRENGTH + low muscle quantity/quality; low PERFORMANCE marks severe disease.** EWGSOP2 reframed sarcopenia as a muscle disease and put low strength first (Find–Assess–Confirm–Severity pathway; SARC-F case-finding). *(`cruzjentoft2019ewgsop2`)* For a Singapore/Asian reader, AWGS 2019 gives Asian-appropriate cut-offs (grip <28 kg men / <18 kg women) and a "possible sarcopenia" category usable in the community from a *single* impairment — enabling early protein+exercise advice without DXA. *(`chen2020awgs2019`)* Keep to concept + community screening idea; diagnosis/management → later clinical years.

### B. INTERNAL — the micronutrient & hydration watch-list

6. **VITAMIN B12: absorption falls via food-cobalamin malabsorption (FCM).** Atrophic gastritis / low gastric acid stops B12 being split from food protein — the dominant cause of B12 deficiency in older adults (not dietary lack); ~15% of >60 y are deficient, mainly via FCM. *(`andres2005foodCobalamin`)* B12 deficiency reaches 38% with atrophic gastritis and 21% in PPI users (dose-dependent) vs 15% controls — and because FCM spares *crystalline* B12, fortified foods / oral B12 are absorbed normally. *(`porter2021b12Gastritis`)* Watch B12 in the at-risk (atrophic gastritis, long-term **metformin/PPI**, poor intake, vegetarians, unexplained anaemia / neuro-cognitive change) — not everyone. *(`mouchaileh2023b12Older`; metformin link → `metforminB12`)*

7. **VITAMIN D: older adults are deficiency-prone.** Classic teaching: cutaneous synthesis capacity falls >2-fold from young to old. *(`maclaughlin1985skinVitD`)* **State honestly that this is now contested** — a healthy older-vs-younger study found no difference in skin 7-DHC or D3 response to UVR. *(`borecka2024skin7dhc`)* The robust, teachable point is behavioural/exposure risk (less time outdoors, institutionalisation, sun avoidance, reduced renal 1α-hydroxylation). Dosing / fracture evidence → *Bone Health*.

8. **CALCIUM requirement rises while intestinal absorption efficiency falls.** Flag the age-specific requirement rise + local intake gap, food-first; the Ca+vit-D-fracture *management* evidence (incl. USPSTF no-net-benefit + stone-risk signal) is owned by *Bone Health*. *(local intake gap → `ye2023daha`; food-first principle → `hpbSeniors`)* **Do not print a specific mg RDA sourced to the HPB seniors release** — take the numeric RDA from Singapore RDA tables / Part I (see §11).

9. **HYDRATION: blunted thirst + reduced renal concentrating ability.** Older adults respond poorly to rising plasma osmolality (higher osmotic set-point), renal maximum concentrating ability falls ~20% by 60–79 y, and total body water shrinks to ~50–60% — so they dehydrate before feeling thirsty. *(`li2023hydrationOlder`)* **Do-no-harm nuance:** commonly-used bedside signs of dehydration are *unreliable* in older people (serum osmolality is the reference) — teach recognition of RISK and proactive offering of fluids, not diagnosis-by-signs or "wait until thirsty." *(`hooper2016drieDehydration`)*

### C. INTERNAL — appetite, dentition, swallowing, medications

10. **The anorexia-of-ageing mechanism (recognition level).** Delayed gastric emptying and reduced fundal accommodation → earlier antral stretch/fullness; higher/more potent CCK; lower, "resistant" ghrelin; low-grade inflammation (IL-1/IL-6/TNF-α) suppressing orexigenic drive. *(`wysokinski2015anorexia`)* Prevalence ~15–30% community, higher in hospital/nursing-home settings; appetite loss is an EARLY sign that can precede weight loss — the red flag to recognise and refer. *(`desoutobarreto2022icfsr`, `malafarina2013anorexia`)*

11. **Chemosensory loss.** Olfaction declines more than taste (>60% of 65–80 y, >75% of >80 y impaired), blunting food reward and intake. *(`wysokinski2015anorexia`)* This sets up the *flavour-amplification* mitigation (§below).

12. **Dentition.** Tooth loss/edentulism shifts older adults to soft, carbohydrate-dense foods and away from meat/fruit/veg, lowering diet quality and protein/micronutrient intake. *(`chan2023oralHealth`)* Recognition prompt: "can this patient chew a piece of meat or an apple?"

13. **Dysphagia (recognise-and-refer).** Oropharyngeal dysphagia is common in older adults (~30–60% across care settings). *(`wu2020tmd`)* Texture-modified diets and thickened fluids keep swallowing safe but are consistently linked to LOWER energy/nutrient intake — a teaching paradox; ESPEN recommends texture-modified **enriched** foods as compensation. *(`espenGeriatricsPractical2022`)* Red flags: coughing/choking on food or fluids, wet/gurgly voice, recurrent chest infections, prolonged mealtimes, pocketing → refer to speech therapy / dietitian.

14. **Polypharmacy.** Multiple drugs contribute to anorexia, nausea, dysgeusia and dry mouth. Polypharmacy (**defined as ≥5 medications**) is significantly associated with malnutrition risk in older people. *(`kok2022polypharmacy`)* The classic **"MEALS ON WHEELS"** mnemonic of reversible causes of weight loss (M = Medication effects) is a useful teaching device. *(`morleySilver1995meals`)* Action: flag polypharmacy and refer for medication review. *(NB per §11: the "~60% report dry mouth" and "3+ medications" specifics are NOT supported by these sources — drop or re-source.)*

### D. Mitigations for reduced intake (advise simply)

15. **Fortify familiar foods first ("food first").** Adding milk powder, cream, egg, cheese, oil or tofu/fish to normal dishes raises energy/protein without increasing volume — ideal when appetite/early satiety limit portions. DIY fortification raises intake reliably (~+400 kcal/day, ~+19 g protein/day). *(`geny2023fortification`, `mills2018fortified`, `bapenFoodFirst`)* **Caveat:** evidence is strong for INTAKE, weak for hard outcomes (weight/muscle) — a sensible first step, not a proven malnutrition fix; established malnutrition (ONS/enteral) → *Undernutrition & Malnutrition*.

16. **Amplify flavour to compensate for chemosensory loss.** Intensifying flavour/aroma (herbs, spices, umami) restored palatability and, in a landmark small trial, improved intake and grip strength. *(`schiffman1993flavor`)* Argues *against* over-restrictive low-salt "therapeutic" diets in frail elders that strip flavour and intake.

### E. EXTERNAL — social & environmental drivers

17. **Eating in company raises intake ("social facilitation").** People eat more with familiar others than alone (pooled SMD 0.76); no effect with strangers. *(`ruddock2019facilitation`)* In older populations, intake rises up to ~60% eating with others. *(`middleton2022sharedMeal`)* **Caveat:** the facilitation meta-analysis is dominated by younger adults — the older-adult extension is inferential but mechanistically reasonable.

18. **Eating alone is a practical RED FLAG.** Solo eaters have lower vegetable intake and less-varied eating. *(`bjornwall2024eatingAlone`)* In Korean older adults, more frequent eating-alone tracked graded falls in fruit/vegetable and protein intake, higher food insecurity and depressive symptoms. *(`kwon2023eatingAlone` — per §11, drop "fewer meals/day" and "more ready-made meals"; soften "independently.")* "Eats alone most of the time" is a simple, askable screening question.

19. **Social isolation predicts inadequate micronutrient intake — distinct from loneliness.** Objective isolation (not subjective loneliness) raised odds of inadequate magnesium/potassium/B6/folate/vitamin C intake by ~15–26% per point. *(`steptoe2024isolation`)* The lever is *structural* (companionship at meals, meal programmes). **Present the isolation-vs-loneliness split as context-dependent** — Singapore frailty data show the opposite (loneliness matters, isolation not; see §5).

20. **The social driver acts THROUGH appetite** — loss of commensality blunts meal cues and cooking motivation (conceptual bridge to §10). *(`sanchez2023anorexia` — editorial, framing only)*

21. **Bereavement/widowhood is a discrete risk window.** Recently widowed elders had more weight loss, more solitary/commercial and fewer home-cooked meals, and less eating enjoyment. *(`shahar2001widowhood`)* An anticipatory "healthy-ageing" action: proactively check weight and intake in the widowhood window.

22. **Food insecurity is about ACCESS, not just money.** Older-adult food insecurity spans mobility, transport/shopping access, finances and bodily function (e.g. hand strength to cook), co-occurring with high IADL limitation. *(`tucher2021foodInsecurity`)* Ask: "Can you get to the shops? Carry groceries? Stand to cook?"

23. **Community meal services are an evidence-backed referral endpoint.** Home-delivered and congregate meals reduced the percent malnourished (−15.5 and −9.0 percentage points) and raised RDA attainment; they reach the isolated/low-SES group. *(`cpstf2021meals`)* The student's job is to recognise the need and connect the patient to such a service (local endpoints in §6).

### F. The tip into UNDERNUTRITION + do-no-harm

24. **Unintentional weight loss ≥5% over 6–12 months is THE red flag** that ageing-in-health has tipped into a clinical problem; corroborate with looser clothing/belt or a relative's report when no baseline exists. *(`gaddey2021weightLoss`)* WHO ICOPE offers a simple frontline screen (weight loss >3 kg/3 months OR appetite loss → refer) — a memorable, non-tool-heavy recognise-and-refer prompt. *(`whoIcope`)*

25. **Frailty and nutrition overlap at recognition level.** Fried's phenotype's first criterion — unintentional weight loss — is itself a nutrition signal (also: weakness, slow gait, exhaustion, low activity; ≥3 = frail, 1–2 = pre-frail). *(`fried2001frailty`)* Primary-care role = screen/recognise and REFER (dietitian for malnutrition; physio for frailty/sarcopenia), with food-first + exercise as the combined response. *(`roberts2021community`)*

26. **DO-NO-HARM headline: do not reflexively restrict a thin, frail older adult.** ESPEN: dietary restrictions should **generally be avoided**; weight-reducing diets only in *obese* older persons with weight-related problems and combined with exercise; and all older persons should be routinely screened for malnutrition. *(`espenGeriatrics2019`)* A low-fat/low-salt/low-sugar or weight-loss diet in a frail elder limits intake and precipitates malnutrition — support intake instead (counselling, food-first fortification). Screening TOOLS themselves (MUST/MNA/GLIM) and management → *Undernutrition & Malnutrition*.

27. **Why early recognition matters (local outcome evidence).** In Singapore, ~28% of community older adults are at malnutrition risk and 46% pre-frail — a large "pre-" reservoir where food-first can prevent the slide. *(`wei2017slasFrailty`)* When frailty and poor nutrition co-occur, mortality is far higher (~3.0 vs 0.5 per 100 person-years). *(`wei2018slasOutcomes`)*

  - ***Bone Health & Osteoporosis***: osteoporosis dx/DXA/T-scores, the calcium+vitamin-D fracture-prevention evidence (incl. USPSTF), and supplement dosing. This chapter flags only the age-specific calcium/vit-D requirement shift + local intake gap ("food-first, refer").

  - ***Adulthood: working life & activity***: the "build a muscle reserve" and exercise/sports-nutrition angle; here the reserve matters precisely because the anabolic machinery is harder to stimulate.

## 6. Singapore context

Singapore is exceptionally well served for this chapter — foreground the local anchors, per the SG citation principle (socio-cultural / clinical-practice, not basic biology).

- **A super-ageing population sets the urgency.** Citizens aged 65+ rose from **13.1% (2015) to 20.7% (2025)**, projected **~23.9% (1 in 4) by 2030**, with the working-age share falling 64.5%→59.8% *(`sgPopulationLongevity` — for the percentages)*; Singapore is projected to reach "**super-aged**" status in **2026** *(`dukeNusSuperAged2026` — for the qualitative super-aged/2026 milestone)*. **Cite these two separately** (see §11).
- **HPB Dietary Guidelines for Older Adults** are the primary local scaffold: **nutrient-dense quality over quantity, softer/easier-to-swallow foods, calcium-rich/fortified foods, and more fluids** — mapping cleanly onto the internal-driver template. *(`hpbSeniors`)* HealthHub operationalises the protein message (~1.2 g/kg/day for 50+, ~50% more than a younger adult; ~25 g protein/meal across 3 meals) with local carriers — 3 eggs, a palm-sized 90 g fish/meat portion, 2 blocks firm tofu, ¾ cup pulses. *(`healthhubSeniorsProtein`)* A hook statistic: **~1 in 2 older adults in Singapore do not meet their protein requirement** for their age. *(`mohNNS2022` — NNS 2022, noting its 18–69 frame; see the data-gap note below.)* The **"Recipe for Healthy Ageing"** toolkit and **STAR** one-dish recipes model fortifying familiar local dishes (mee sua, porridge, yong tau foo) while keeping flavour. *(`hpbRecipeAgeing2016`)*
- **Fortify the familiar local dish** (the culturally-anchored "food-first" lever): add egg/fish/tofu/ikan bilis or milk/soy powder to porridge; richer soups; minced/soft-cooked meats and calcium-set tofu for chewing difficulty; a palm-sized protein at every meal. Calcium-relevant local foods: calcium-set tofu/tau kwa, fortified soy milk, ikan bilis/canned sardines with bones, milk/yoghurt.
- **The "sunny-country paradox" persists in older adults.** Vitamin-D deficiency is common despite year-round sun — 52% low 25(OH)D in *healthy community* ≥65 y (SHIELD; `cheong2020shield`) and 85.6% low / 44% deficient in rehab inpatients (`neo2016vitDsg`), higher in Malay/Indian ethnicity — driven by indoor living/sun avoidance, not latitude. *(General SG deficiency context also `sgVitDeficiency`.)*
- **Calcium is the widest local micronutrient gap** (~50–55% below 70% of RDA in the DaHA cohort; `ye2023daha`) — food-first, refer; leave dosing to *Bone Health*.
- **Reassuring nuance:** B12 deficiency is uncommon in *well* community-dwelling older Singaporeans (~2% low in SHIELD; `cheong2020shield`) — watch B12 in the at-risk (atrophic gastritis, long-term metformin/PPI — common in this diabetes-prevalent population, poor intake, vegetarians), not everyone.
- **The external pillar is strongest here.** One-person households aged 65+ more than **doubled to 87,200 by 2024**, and older adults living alone are ~2× as likely to report depressive symptoms *(`mohSeniorsAlone2026` — for living-alone/depression/policy levers only)*. Strikingly, in the DaHA cohort **24.1% "ate alone most of the time" though only 6.7% lived alone** (`ye2023daha`) — in Singapore's multigenerational households, co-residence does NOT guarantee a shared meal (adult children work; the elder eats alone). Locally, **loneliness** (not objective isolation) nearly tripled frailty odds (OR 2.90) while social participation protected (`geSocialFrailtySg2022`) — the opposite construct from Steptoe's micronutrient finding, so present the split as context-dependent. **Referral endpoints students should name are LOCAL, not US Meals-on-Wheels:** Active Ageing Centres (befriending/communal dining), Silver Generation Office, community mental-health referral under **Age Well SG** (~S$800m FY2024–28; expanding to 220 AACs by 2025, ~8 in 10 seniors with access), and communal-meal initiatives (e.g. Goodlife! Makan). *(`mohAgeWellSg2023`)*
- **The older-adult data gap (flag for the author):** NNS 2022's sampling frame is **18–69**, so it does not cover the 70+ "older-old" most at nutritional risk. The best peer-reviewed *local* older-adult nutrition-status data are the DaHA cohort (`ye2023daha`) and SHIELD (`cheong2020shield`) — cite these where NNS 2022 leaves the 70+ unmeasured.

## 7. Common misconceptions & pitfalls

- **"Older people should just eat less of everything."** Energy needs fall but protein and micronutrient needs per calorie RISE — the answer is a *nutrient-dense* plate, not a smaller version of the same plate. *(`giezenaar2016appetite`, `espenGeriatrics2019`)*
- **"Protein needs go down with age."** They go UP (~1.0–1.2 g/kg/day) because ageing muscle is anabolically resistant. *(`bauer2013protage`, `moore2015anabolic`)*
- **"As long as the daily protein total is met, timing doesn't matter."** Per-meal threshold matters — banking all protein at dinner is less effective for muscle synthesis than spreading ~25–30 g across meals. *(`mamerow2014distribution`)*
- **"A low-fat / low-salt / weight-loss diet suits every frail senior."** No — dietary restriction should generally be AVOIDED in older/frail adults; it strips intake and flavour and precipitates malnutrition. Weight-reducing diets only for *obese* elders with weight-related problems + exercise. *(`espenGeriatrics2019`, `schiffman1993flavor`)*
- **"Weight loss in an older adult is just normal ageing."** Unintentional weight loss ≥5% over 6–12 months is a RED FLAG (mortality/morbidity, malignancy, depression, dental/swallowing, medication) to evaluate and refer — not to shrug off. *(`gaddey2021weightLoss`, `fried2001frailty`)*
- **"We have plenty of sun in Singapore, so vitamin-D deficiency isn't an issue in the elderly."** ~52% of healthy community ≥65 y have low 25(OH)D. *(`cheong2020shield`, `neo2016vitDsg`)*
- **"All older adults should be screened/supplemented for B12."** Watch the at-risk (atrophic gastritis, long-term metformin/PPI, poor intake, vegetarians, unexplained anaemia/neuro-cognitive change), not everyone — B12 deficiency is uncommon in well local elders. *(`porter2021b12Gastritis`, `cheong2020shield`)*
- **"Wait until an older person feels thirsty before offering fluids / the usual signs will tell you they're dehydrated."** Thirst is blunted and bedside signs are unreliable — offer fluids proactively; serum osmolality is the reference. *(`li2023hydrationOlder`, `hooper2016drieDehydration`)*
- **"If they live with family, they're eating well."** In Singapore, 24.1% eat alone most of the time though only 6.7% live alone — co-residence ≠ a shared meal. *(`ye2023daha`)*
- **"A texture-modified diet solves the swallowing problem."** It keeps swallowing safe but is linked to LOWER intake — enrich texture-modified foods and monitor; refer for dysphagia work-up. *(`wu2020tmd`, `espenGeriatricsPractical2022`)*
- **"Fortification means expensive supplement drinks."** Food-first fortification of familiar dishes (milk powder, egg, oil, tofu) raises energy/protein without extra volume, before any ONS. *(`geny2023fortification`, `mills2018fortified`)*
- **"Sarcopenia just means being thin/frail."** It is low muscle STRENGTH plus low muscle quantity/quality — a strong, thin-looking person can be sarcopenic; a heavier person can be too. *(`cruzjentoft2019ewgsop2`, `chen2020awgs2019`)*

## 8. Proposed interactive elements

### Bespoke island — `SamePlateDifferentNeeds` (Svelte island, book house-style)
**The one thing prose can't teach: that the SAME older adult needs a *smaller-energy but denser* plate — energy DOWN while protein/nutrient-density goes UP — and that you can rebuild the plate to hit that.** Two linked panels on one screen:
- **Panel 1 — "The mismatch dial."** A dual-axis figure across the adult lifespan: **energy requirement** trending *down* (falling fat-free mass/activity, anorexia-of-ageing intake drop) while **protein requirement (g/kg/day)** steps *up* from 0.8 → ~1.0–1.2, and a **micronutrient-density index** rising (same nutrients, fewer calories). A draggable age marker (40 → 85) animates the diverging lines. One-line captions anchor each curve to its source (`giezenaar2016appetite`, `bauer2013protage`, `espenGeriatrics2019`).
- **Panel 2 — "Fortify the familiar" hawker/porridge builder.** Start from a real senior meal (plain rice porridge, ~small bowl). The student adds culturally-authentic components — egg, ikan bilis, silken/firm tofu, fish slices, a spoon of oil, milk/soy powder, kai-lan — and a live readout shows **kcal, protein (g), and per-meal protein vs the ~25–30 g threshold**, plus a calcium tick. A "distribute across 3 meals" toggle contrasts even vs dinner-skewed protein (`mamerow2014distribution`). A do-no-harm guardrail fires if the student reflexively strips fat/salt from an already-underweight profile → prompt: "this elder is losing weight — density up, restriction off" (`espenGeriatrics2019`). ### MCQ-only case (recognise-and-refer altitude, per house rule — no live patient-chat)
**`mdm-lim-widowed-alone`.** *Vignette:* **Mdm Lim, 78, Singaporean Chinese**, widowed 4 months ago, lives in an HDB flat; her daughter (who works full-time) notices her *cheongsam is looser*, she "isn't bothered to cook for one," and often skips lunch. She takes long-term **metformin** and a **PPI**. No dysphagia signs.
- **Node 1 — recognition.** Which is the strongest single red flag? Correct: **unintentional weight loss (looser clothing) in the widowhood window** → warrants evaluation/referral. Distractors: "normal ageing"; "just grief, will pass." *(`gaddey2021weightLoss`, `shahar2001widowhood`)*
- **Node 2 — the do-no-harm fork.** Best first nutrition advice? Correct: **food-first density + share meals** (fortify familiar dishes, hit per-meal protein, connect to an Active Ageing Centre communal meal). Distractor (do-no-harm trap): "start a low-fat, low-salt 'healthy' diet to be safe" → **coach line:** *restriction in a thin, losing-weight elder precipitates malnutrition — density UP, restriction OFF.* *(`espenGeriatrics2019`, `geny2023fortification`, `mohAgeWellSg2023`)*
- **Node 3 — the B12 watch.** Given metformin + PPI + poor intake, which is the appropriate step? Correct: **consider B12 testing and refer** (at-risk profile), not blanket supplementation of the whole panel. *(`porter2021b12Gastritis`, `mouchaileh2023b12Older`, `metforminB12`)*
- **Node 4 — routing.** All roads → **REFER** (GP/polyclinic + dietitian; connect to community meal/befriending) and explicitly *not* manage malnutrition here (that's the *Undernutrition & Malnutrition* chapter). Log in `../cases/case-bank.md`.
