# Evidence Literacy: Appraising Diets, Fads and Supplements — research dossier

## 1. Scope (one paragraph)
This chapter equips first-year students to *appraise* nutrition claims rather than merely react to them. Surveys consistently show graduates feel under-prepared in clinical and evidence-based nutrition (`crowley2019`), and critical appraisal is among the weakest entry-level skills — so the chapter pitches **genuine, guided appraisal practice**, not a checklist of "red flags." Students learn why the evidence hierarchy is applied *differently* in nutrition (whole-diet patterns vs single nutrients; the limits of RCTs and the role of cohorts), how to read a claim from headline back to its primary study, how to distinguish a *mechanism-plausible* idea from a *clinically-proven* one (using chrononutrition / time-restricted eating as the worked calibration case), how conflicts of interest and weak designs distort the literature, and when supplements genuinely are and are not indicated. The applied spine is Singapore-first (HPB My Healthy Plate, HealthHub messaging, HSA supplement regulation) before international guidelines (ADA, AHA, ACLM). The goal is calibrated humility: most fad claims are *over*-stated, a few "fads" contain a real-but-modest signal, and the student's job is to tell which is which using transparent reasoning.

## 2. Learning objectives (refined)
1. **Apply** a simple, repeatable appraisal routine to a nutrition claim — trace headline → study → design → outcome → applicability — and reach a defensible verdict (not just flag a red flag).
2. **Distinguish** evidence-based dietary guidance (patterns, guidelines) from fad diets and unproven/unnecessary supplements, and **state the grade of evidence** behind each.
3. **Explain** why dietary-*pattern* evidence is graded differently from single mechanistic or single-nutrient studies, including why blinded RCTs are often infeasible in nutrition and why cohort evidence still matters.
4. **Differentiate** "mechanism-plausible" from "clinically-proven," using time-restricted eating (TRE) as the worked case where biological rationale outran the trial evidence.
5. **Identify** when a supplement *is* indicated (documented deficiency, life-stage need, specific risk groups) versus *not* indicated (replacing a balanced diet, "detox," generic prevention in well-nourished people), and recognise that some supplements cause harm.
6. **Recognise** common conflicts of interest and weak study designs (surrogate endpoints, tiny/short trials, animal-only data, industry-funded single studies).

## 3. Scope boundaries
**In:** the nutrition evidence hierarchy at an introductory level; pattern-vs-nutrient reasoning; a hands-on appraisal routine with practice; common fad-diet claims (keto, detox, "metabolism boosters") and supplement claims; mechanism-plausible vs clinically-proven (chrononutrition/TRE); spotting conflicts of interest and weak designs; indications and non-indications for supplements; landmark trials taught *with* caveats; Singapore instruments (HPB/HSA/MOH).
**Out, covered elsewhere:** formal biostatistics, p-values, confidence intervals and meta-analytic methods (general research-methods/EBM course); detailed micronutrient biochemistry and deficiency syndromes (nutrition-science chapter); pharmacology of specific supplements and drug–nutrient interactions; clinical management of obesity/T2DM and remission programmes (`lean2018direct`, `dpp2002` — signposted only); motivational interviewing and behaviour change as a skill set (`frost2018mi`, `mastellos2014ttm` — counselling chapter); specialist GRADE methodology and guideline development (signposted in §11 as a live methodological debate, not resolved here).

## 4. Key concepts to teach (didactic spine)
1. **A claim is not evidence.** Every nutrition headline rests on *some* underlying study — your job is to find it and judge it, not to accept or dismiss it on vibes. Introduce the appraisal routine as a habit. (`crowley2019` motivates the need.)
2. **The evidence hierarchy — and why nutrition bends it.** Case report → cross-sectional → cohort → RCT → systematic review/meta-analysis. In nutrition, blinding is often impossible, there is no true "zero-intake placebo," and effects come from *whole patterns*, so well-conducted prospective cohorts carry more weight here than they would for a drug. (`tobias2019ebnutrition` — note this is a *specialist* methodological argument; teach the intuition, signpost the full debate.)
3. **Patterns over nutrients.** Guidelines now grade *dietary patterns* (Mediterranean, DASH) rather than isolated nutrients, because foods interact and single-nutrient swaps mislead. (`aha2021diet`; `estruch2018predimed`; `appel1997dash`.)
4. **Mechanism-plausible ≠ clinically-proven.** A compelling biological story (circadian biology → meal timing) raises a *hypothesis*; only outcome trials confirm benefit. TRE is the teaching anchor. (`sleepRestriction` for the mechanism; `lowe2020treat` and `lin2023tre` for the modest/null outcomes.)
5. **Landmark trials, taught with their caveats.** Big results are real *and* bounded:
   - **PREDIMED**: the headline "~30%" is a *relative* risk reduction on a *composite* CVD endpoint that was *stroke-dominated*, from a trial *re-analysed and re-published in 2018* after randomisation irregularities were found in part of the cohort (`estruch2018predimed`). State the absolute reduction is much smaller, and that the result held on re-analysis but with these caveats attached.
   - **DASH**: lowered BP ~5.5/3.0 mmHg (more in those with hypertension) in a *tightly controlled feeding study* — participants were *fed* the diet, and BP fell *without* weight loss or sodium change (`appel1997dash`). Generalising to free-living self-prepared diets is an extrapolation.
   - Where T2DM-prevention/remission trials are mentioned in passing, carry their caveats too: DPP's 58% reduction came from an *intensive 16-session lifestyle program*, not a single clinic visit (`dpp2002`); DiRECT's ~46% remission applied only to *early* (≤6y) *non-insulin-treated* T2DM in adults with *BMI 27–45* (`lean2018direct`). Both are signposted, not taught in depth.
   - The transferable lesson: effect size (relative vs absolute), endpoint type (surrogate vs hard; composite vs single), study conditions, and who-was-studied always qualify the headline.
6. **Weak designs and conflicts of interest.** Surrogate endpoints, n≈20 / 4-week trials, animal/in-vitro only, single industry-funded studies, "proprietary blends." Funding source is a prompt to scrutinise, not an automatic disqualifier.
7. **Supplements: indicated vs not — and sometimes harmful.** Indicated for documented deficiency and specific life-stages/risk groups; *not* indicated to replace a balanced diet, to "detox," or for blanket prevention in the well-nourished. β-carotene in smokers and the null VITAL primary endpoints show supplements are not free of downside. (`caret1996`; `vital2019`.)
8. **The appraisal routine, practised.** Students run the routine on real claims end-to-end and *commit to a verdict with reasoning* — the deliberate "genuine practice" beyond red-flag spotting.

## 6. Singapore context
- **HPB / HealthHub voice as the appraisal anchor.** HealthHub states health supplements "cannot and should not replace a balanced diet," advises against self-medicating with supplements, and tells consumers to consult a professional first — a ready-made, locally authoritative example of evidence-based messaging students can contrast against marketing (`hpbHealthSupplements`). HealthHub's "balanced food choices over extreme diets" messaging is a direct fad-diet counter. *(Verify exact HealthHub page titles/URLs at publication — these pages are periodically reorganised.)*
- **"Detox" debunked locally.** HealthHub/HSA consumer guidance: detox products typically act by promoting water loss or stimulating bowel movements, lack scientific evidence of "toxin removal," and can cause headaches, fatigue, dehydration, GI cramps and (with prolonged laxative use) bowel dependency. A home-grown worked example for Concept 7 and Case A.
- **HSA as the regulatory backstop.** Health supplements in Singapore are *notified*, not pre-market-approved for efficacy; HSA periodically finds illegal/adulterated products containing undeclared potent or banned ingredients (e.g., sibutramine — withdrawn globally in 2010 for cardiovascular risk; products with dangerously high heavy-metal content causing hospitalisation). Teaches that "available for sale" ≠ "proven safe/effective," and that adulteration is a real Singapore harm. *(Confirm the specific enforcement-case figures — heavy-metal multiple, number of online listings removed — against a current HSA media release before quoting exact numbers.)*
- **My Healthy Plate (QQH)** as the evidence-based default the fads deviate from (¼ wholegrains, ¼ protein, ½ fruit & veg) (`hpbHealthyPlate`).
- **Local epidemiology hook:** NNS 2022 shows only ~4% meet wholegrain staple recommendations and ~9/10 exceed sodium (`mohNNS2022`) — the realistic baseline diet that supplements/fads claim to "fix," reframing the question as "improve the pattern" not "buy a product."
- **Hawker-food examples:** appraise a viral claim that "apple cider vinegar before chicken rice blunts the sugar spike," or that a "detox kombucha" undoes a laksa — students trace the claim to its (thin) evidence and to HealthHub guidance. Contrast with a genuinely evidence-based swap (brown-rice option, teh-O kosong, Nutri-Grade A/B drink choice) (`hpbNutriGrade`).

## 7. Pillar intersections
- **nutrition × circadian biology (meal timing / TRE as a calibration case).** TRE is the spine's worked example of how a chrononutrition mechanism (`sleepRestriction`: disrupted sleep/circadian signalling lowers insulin sensitivity and raises appetite) generated a strong, *plausible* rationale — yet the human outcome trials show effects that are modest-to-null and largely explained by calorie reduction (`lowe2020treat`, `lin2023tre`). This is the cleanest case in the book for teaching "mechanism-plausible vs clinically-proven" and for showing that being skeptical of hype is *not* the same as dismissing a mechanism. Cross-reference the circadian/sleep chapter so students see the same evidence appraised from both directions.

## 8. Clinical application & case ideas (case-player-ready)

**Case A — "I want to detox and try a keto-supplement stack" (the patient-facing appraisal case).**
Setup: A 24-year-old polytechnic graduate (well-nourished, no chronic disease) says she bought a 7-day "detox tea" and a "keto fat-burner" supplement online after a TikTok video, and wants your blessing.
- *Branch A1 — student dismisses it bluntly ("that's nonsense"):* patient feels judged, disengages. Coach: appraisal ≠ contempt; ask what outcome she wants.
- *Branch A2 — student asks what she's hoping for and where the claim came from:* surfaces the real goal (lose 3 kg before a wedding) → opens an evidence-based path. **Preferred.**
- *Branch A3 — appraise the detox claim:* student walks her through HealthHub's position (no toxin-removal evidence; acts via water/bowel loss; possible dehydration/dependency) and HSA's adulteration findings → *verdict: not indicated, possible harm.*
- *Branch A4 — appraise the keto/"fat-burner":* student distinguishes the *diet* (real but modest early weight loss that attenuates over months; adherence hard; LDL-C may rise; certainty mostly low — `keto2023umbrella`) from the *supplement* (no efficacy evidence; "proprietary blend"; notified-not-efficacy-vetted by HSA). *Verdict: the diet has a real but bounded signal; the product does not.*
- *Resolution:* redirect to My Healthy Plate + a modest sustainable deficit; flag "see a doctor before any supplement," and that supplements don't replace diet. Teaches *genuine appraisal to a verdict*, not red-flag spotting alone.

**Case B — "Is time-restricted eating worth it?" (the calibration case).**
Setup: A 40-year-old office worker with a strong family history of T2DM read that "fasting fixes your metabolism" and wants to do strict 16:8.
- *Branch B1 — student calls TRE a fad and dismisses it:* over-correction. Coach: the mechanism is real and plausible; don't throw it out.
- *Branch B2 — student over-promises ("fasting will reset your metabolism"):* hype. Coach: present the mechanism *and* the trial evidence honestly.
- *Branch B3 — calibrated answer:* explain the circadian rationale (`sleepRestriction`), then that head-to-head RCTs show TRE's weight effect is **not significantly better than calorie control** and is largely explained by eating less (`lowe2020treat`: 1.17% vs 0.75%, NS; `lin2023tre`: ~4.6 vs 5.4 kg vs daily calorie restriction); it can be a *useful structure for some people* if it helps them reduce intake and is sustainable. **Preferred verdict: reasonable optional tool, not a magic lever; success = adherence + overall pattern.**
- *Twist:* patient asks "but a study showed 30% lower heart risk on the Mediterranean diet — why not just trust studies?" → mini-exercise unpacking *relative* vs *absolute* reduction, the composite/stroke-dominated endpoint, and the 2018 re-analysis in PREDIMED (`estruch2018predimed`). Teaches reading a landmark trial *with* caveats.

*Both cases plug directly into the case-player with branching verdicts, a "name the evidence level" prompt, and a forced final recommendation with stated reasoning. Each branch has an explicit preferred path, a failure mode (over-credulous and over-cynical), and a coach line — enough structure to build the branching case directly.*

## 9. Common misconceptions & pitfalls
- **"RCT or it's worthless."** Often false in nutrition: blinding and zero-intake placebos are frequently impossible, so well-run cohorts and pattern studies legitimately carry weight (`tobias2019ebnutrition`). Over-applying drug-trial standards discards good dietary evidence.
- **"Observational study proved X causes Y."** Cohorts show association; confounding (healthy-user bias), measurement error in diet recall, and substitution effects limit causal claims.
- **"Mechanism proven in cells/mice = works in patients."** The TRE story is the antidote.
- **"Relative risk reduction = my personal benefit."** A ~30% relative reduction on a composite endpoint can be a small absolute change; endpoint type and baseline risk matter (`estruch2018predimed`).
- **"Natural / sold in a pharmacy = safe and proven."** HSA notifies (does not efficacy-approve) supplements; β-carotene *harmed* smokers (`caret1996`); VITAL primary endpoints were null (`vital2019`).
- **"Detox clears toxins."** No supporting evidence; observed effect is water/bowel loss (HealthHub).
- **Over-correction pitfall (the one students fall into after this chapter):** becoming reflexively cynical and dismissing *everything* new. The skill is *calibration* — appraise to a proportionate verdict, including "modest but real."
- **"Funded by industry = automatically fake" / "no funding = automatically true."** Funding is a scrutiny prompt, not a verdict.

## 10. Proposed interactive elements
- **Quiz topics:** (a) place 5 claims on the evidence hierarchy; (b) for a given headline, identify the weakest design feature (surrogate endpoint / n=18 / 3 weeks / mouse-only / single industry study); (c) supplement indicated vs not (6 vignettes: pregnancy folate ✓, vegan B12 ✓, documented iron-deficiency ✓ vs "detox" ✗, generic multivit in well-fed adult ✗, β-carotene for a smoker ✗-harm); (d) relative vs absolute risk from a PREDIMED-style stat.
- **Diagram idea:** an **"appraisal funnel"** — Headline → Find the study → Design (where on the hierarchy?) → Outcome (surrogate vs hard? relative vs absolute?) → Applicability (who was studied? like my patient?) → **Verdict** (proven / plausible-unproven / disproven / harmful). Used as a reusable on-screen scaffold in both cases.
- **Case:** the two branching scenarios in §8 (detox/keto-supplement; TRE calibration), each ending in a forced student verdict + one-line justification graded for *calibration*, not just direction.

## 11. Evidence gaps / open questions
- **TRE long-term hard outcomes** (CVD events, mortality, durable glycaemia) remain untested; current evidence is short-term weight/cardiometabolic surrogates (`lin2023tre`).
- **Ketogenic long-term safety**, especially LDL-C trajectory and CV events, is unresolved; signal varies by population/genetics, and most umbrella-review outcomes were graded low/very-low certainty (`keto2023umbrella`).
- **How best to apply GRADE to nutrition** is actively debated (whether observational evidence should start at "low" certainty is contested) — flag as a live methodological question, signpost as future learning, do not resolve at first-year depth.
- **Local data:** no published Singapore RCT of TRE on hawker-centre eating patterns; supplement-use prevalence and motivations in SG youth are under-characterised (opportunity to cite HPB surveys when available).
