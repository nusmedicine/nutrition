## 1. Scope

This chapter covers maternal nutrition across pregnancy and lactation at first-year literacy: the physiological drivers that reshape a woman's nutritional requirements (plasma-volume expansion and haemodilution, physiological insulin resistance, doubled calcium absorption, the energy economics of gestation and milk synthesis), the micronutrients whose need rises or whose excess harms (folate, iron, iodine, vitamin D/calcium, choline, DHA, and teratogenic preformed vitamin A), gestational weight gain and physical activity as the modifiable levers, gestational diabetes at recognition-and-referral depth (why Singapore screens universally), food-safety and alcohol/caffeine counselling, the mother's side of lactation (her energy/fluid needs and the buffered-vs-diet-dependent milk-nutrient partition), and the near-universal, ethnically-patterned Singapore confinement practices a local clinician must navigate respectfully. It deepens — does not repeat — the life-cycle overview, and hands all infant-feeding mechanics to the infancy chapter.

## 2. Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, the first-year student should be able to:

1. **Recognise** the major physiological adaptations of pregnancy (plasma-volume expansion → haemodilution; ~50–60% fall in insulin sensitivity; doubled intestinal calcium absorption) and explain how each reshapes a nutritional requirement.
2. **Quantify and refute** the "eating for two" myth: state that energy needs rise only modestly and mainly in later trimesters (~+340 kcal T2, ~+452 kcal T3, negligible T1), and that lactation (~+450–500 kcal/day) is the stage where the phrase nearly earns its keep.
3. **Anticipate** the pregnancy-specific micronutrient needs (periconceptional folic acid and its dose split; rising iron and iodine; the counter-intuitively unchanged calcium allowance; emerging choline and DHA) and the one nutrient whose *excess* is teratogenic (preformed vitamin A).
4. **Apply** the IOM/NAM 2009 gestational-weight-gain framework by pre-pregnancy BMI, explain the U-shaped risk of too-little and too-much gain, and flag that WHO-based BMI cut-offs may fit Asian women imperfectly.
5. **Explain at recognition level** why gestational diabetes arises (physiological insulin resistance overwhelming finite beta-cell reserve) and **justify** Singapore's universal OGTT screening from the local evidence that risk-factor screening missed ~half of cases.
6. **Counsel** on food safety (listeria, toxoplasma/salmonella, high-mercury fish — "choose low-mercury fish, don't avoid fish"), on alcohol (no known safe amount — abstinence) and caffeine (<200 mg/day as a pragmatic guardrail, not a proven-safe line).
7. **Distinguish**, in lactation, the milk nutrients buffered from maternal stores (iron, calcium, folate, zinc, copper) from those that fall with maternal depletion (B12, iodine, vitamin D, vitamin A, DHA), and identify the vegan-B12 red flag as a referral trigger.
8. **Navigate** Singapore confinement practices through respectful negotiation, and **refer** appropriately (dietitian, obstetric/diabetes team) rather than manage.

## 4. Key concepts to teach (didactic spine)

Ordered for teaching; each on the internal↔external template.

1. **Energy: the "eating for two" myth, quantified.** *Internal driver:* pregnancy adds only a modest energy cost, back-loaded into later trimesters (IOM DRI: ~nil T1, +340 kcal T2, +452 kcal T3) — and measured total energy expenditure often rises even *less* (Savard median +144 early→mid, +290 early→late). *External lever:* counsel "an extra small meal or snack in T2/T3, not a doubling; quality over quantity." *(Savard 2020; IOM DRI 2005.)*

2. **Energy, part two: lactation is where the phrase nearly earns its keep.** Milk production costs ~670 kcal/day; ~170 kcal is drawn from pregnancy fat stores, so the *net* added dietary requirement is ~450–500 kcal/day. *Teaching pivot:* pregnancy fat gain is the biological down-payment for lactation. *(FAO/WHO/UNU 2004; National Academies DRI 2005.)*

3. **Plasma-volume expansion and physiological haemodilution.** *Internal driver:* plasma volume rises ~42% (wk 28–34) to ~48% (wk 35–38), outpacing the ~25% rise in red-cell mass, so Hb falls by dilution — physiological, not iron-deficiency, anaemia. *External lever:* use pregnancy-specific Hb cut-offs and check ferritin/MCV rather than reflexively treating every low Hb. *(Aguree & Gernand 2019.)*

4. **Physiological insulin resistance — the substrate for GDM.** *Internal driver:* insulin sensitivity falls ~50–60% by the third trimester in *both* normal pregnancy and GDM, driven multifactorially by placental/maternal hormones (hPL, progesterone, oestrogen, cortisol, placental growth hormone) plus TNF-α — *no single hormone explains it*. *External lever:* this normal adaptation diverts glucose to the fetus; GDM is the failure of beta-cell compensation, which is why it predicts future T2DM. *(Kampmann 2019.)*

5. **Gestational weight gain — stratified target, U-shaped risk.** *Internal driver:* healthy gain depends on pre-pregnancy BMI (IOM/NAM 2009: underweight 12.5–18, normal 11.5–16, overweight 7–11.5, obese 5–9 kg). *External lever:* GWG is counselled, not merely watched, because both directions carry risk — too little raises SGA (OR 1.53) and preterm (OR 1.70); too much raises LGA (OR 1.85), macrosomia (OR 1.95), caesarean (OR 1.30); only ~30% of women land in range. *(Rasmussen & Yaktine/IOM 2009; Goldstein 2017.)*

6. **Physical activity — the external counterpoint to the internal physiology.** ~150 min/week of moderate aerobic activity for those without contraindications lowers GDM, pre-eclampsia and caesarean risk; previously active women may generally continue. *(ACOG Committee Opinion 804, 2020.)*

7. **Folate — dose split and timing.** *Internal driver:* the neural tube closes by ~28 days, often before pregnancy is confirmed. *External lever:* 400 µg/day periconceptional for the general population; 4–5 mg/day for prior-NTD/high-risk women. *(MRC 1991 [4 mg, recurrence]; Czeizel 1992 [0.8 mg, first-occurrence].)*

8. **Iron — rising need, common deficiency.** *Internal driver:* expanded red-cell mass plus fetal stores raise requirements. *External lever:* daily oral iron reduces maternal anaemia (RR 0.30) and low birth weight; WHO recommends 30–60 mg elemental iron + 400 µg folic acid antenatally. Locally, ~74% of Singaporean women are iron-deficient by the early third trimester. *(Peña-Rosas 2015; Loy 2019.)*

9. **Iodine — neurodevelopment.** *Internal driver:* requirement rises to ~250 µg/day for fetal thyroid/brain development; the early fetus depends entirely on maternal supply. *External lever:* choose iodised salt (in Singapore, iodisation is voluntary and status cannot be assumed). *(Bath 2013; Codling 2017; WHO iodine-in-pregnancy criteria.)*

10. **Calcium — the counter-intuitive "requirement doesn't rise."** *Internal driver:* intestinal calcium absorption roughly *doubles* from early pregnancy (calcitriol-driven), so the RDA is unchanged (1000 mg/day). *External lever:* ensure adequate baseline intake; no extra allowance needed. *(Kovacs 2016; IOM DRI 2011.)*

11. **Vitamin D — probable benefit, weakened evidence.** Supplementation *probably* reduces pre-eclampsia (RR 0.48), GDM (RR 0.51) and low birth weight (RR 0.55), but the 2024 Cochrane update downgraded certainty on trustworthiness screening — present cautiously. Deficiency is common in Singapore despite the sunshine. *(Palacios 2019/2024; cross-ref sgVitDeficiency.)*

12. **Choline and DHA — the under-taught pair.** Choline AI 450 mg/day (550 lactation); >90% of pregnant women fall short; higher third-trimester intake may improve infant information-processing speed (small trial). DHA/omega-3 reduces preterm birth (RR 0.89 <37 wk; RR 0.58 <34 wk). *(Caudill 2018; Middleton 2018.)*

13. **The fish paradox — "choose low-mercury fish, don't avoid fish."** Over-restriction backfires: low maternal seafood intake is linked to worse child verbal IQ (OR 1.48). Eat 2–3 servings/week of low-mercury fish; avoid only shark, swordfish, marlin, king mackerel (and bigeye tuna). *(Hibbeln 2007; FDA/EPA 2021.)*

14. **Vitamin A — the "excess harms" nutrient.** High-dose *preformed* retinol (supplements, liver) is teratogenic (~1 in 57 malformed above 10,000 IU/day; prevalence ratio 4.8, corrected 95% CI 2.2–10.5); β-carotene from plants is safe. *(Rothman 1995.)*

15. **GDM recognition and the screening pathway.** *Internal driver:* insulin resistance overwhelming beta-cell reserve (concept 4). *External lever:* universal 75 g OGTT at 24–28 wk (IADPSG thresholds 5.1/10.0/8.5 mmol/L), justified locally because risk-factor screening would have **missed about half** of cases (GUSTO). The IADPSG cut-offs are a *chosen* operational line on a *continuous* glucose–outcome relationship (HAPO). Outcomes follow the fetal-hyperinsulinaemia chain (macrosomia → neonatal hypoglycaemia). Postpartum: ~7-fold future T2DM risk, OGTT at 4–12 weeks, lifelong periodic screening. *(Chong/GUSTO 2014; ACE 2018; HAPO 2008; Plows 2018; Bellamy 2009.)* **(Do NOT print a precise sensitivity % or per-ethnicity GDM rates — the source supports only "~half missed" and "~1 in 5"; see §11.)**

16. **Lactation — the buffered-vs-diet-dependent milk-nutrient partition.** *Internal driver:* the mother's body prioritises milk for some nutrients at her own expense. **Buffered** (stable in milk regardless of maternal status): iron, calcium, folate, zinc, copper. **Diet-dependent** (fall if she is low): B12, iodine, vitamin D, vitamin A, thiamin, riboflavin, B6, choline, selenium, DHA. *External lever:* target maternal supplementation at the diet-dependent group; the vegan-B12 case is the danger example; milk is naturally low in vitamin D, so the *infant* is supplemented (mechanics → infancy chapter). *(DGAC 2020; Kühne 1991; Meek/AAP 2022.)*

17. **Alcohol and caffeine in lactation.** Milk alcohol tracks blood alcohol; *time* clears it (~2–2.5 h/standard drink) — "pump and dump" does not. Caffeine ≤200–300 mg/day (2–3 cups) is compatible. *(LactMed; AAP 2022; CDC.)*

18. **Galactagogues — evidence vs hype.** Only low-certainty evidence that any galactagogue raises milk volume; frequent effective milk removal is the real driver of supply. Counsel confinement "milk soups" supportively, don't debunk dismissively. *(Foong 2020.)*

19. **Singapore confinement — know it, respect it, negotiate.** Near-universal (Chinese 96.4%, Malay 92.4%, Indian 85.6% adherence), a structured dietary pattern with mental-health correlations, resting on a shared hot/cold model. The clinician's job is respectful negotiation: guide only the genuinely risky (alcohol in cooking → breast milk; high sodium/fat; wholesale avoidance of "cooling" fruit/veg). *(Fok 2016; Teo 2018; HealthHub/KKH.)*

## 6. Singapore context

Singapore supplies unusually rich, genuinely local evidence — and it clusters in the *socio-cultural* and *clinical-practice* domains where the SG citation principle licenses local research (never basic metabolism "for flavour").

- **GDM burden and screening policy.** GUSTO (Chong 2014, `gusto_gdm`) found ~18.9% GDM ("roughly 1 in 5", 1999 WHO criteria), with an **ethnic gradient** (higher in Indian and Chinese than in Malay women) and — load-bearingly — that **risk-factor-based screening would have missed about half of cases**. That is the empirical justification for **universal** OGTT screening in Singapore (ACE guidance, `aceGDM2018`). *(First author is **CHONG**, not "Chu" — confirmed. ⚠ Do NOT print per-ethnicity GDM percentages or a precise sensitivity figure — they are not confirmable at abstract level and resemble the cohort-composition trap the parent overview dossier deliberately removed; see §11.)*

- **Iron deficiency, even in a high-income setting.** Loy 2019 (`loy2019ironSG`) found **73.8%** of Singaporean women iron-deficient at 26–28 weeks — a powerful hook that iron deficiency is not merely an LMIC problem, and rationale for the routine antenatal iron/folic-acid supplement.

- **Iodine adequacy cannot be assumed.** Salt iodisation is **voluntary** in Singapore (Codling 2017, `codling2017iodine`), with no national iodine-status survey — so iodised salt is a sensible kitchen default. *(Verify-trap: do NOT cite PMID 26596695 as Singapore iodine — it is Turkey; see §11.)*

- **Gestational weight gain fit.** Ee 2014 (`ee2014gwgSG`) found optimal GWG for underweight and obese Singaporean women fell *outside* the IOM-2009 ranges; He 2019 (`he2019gwgSG`) found only 40.7% gained within IOM ranges. Together they justify teaching IOM 2009 as the framework while flagging its WHO (non-Asian) BMI basis — a clinical-practice caveat, not fabricated Asian tables.

- **Culturally-familiar GDM diet.** de Seymour 2016 (`gustoDietGDM`) found a "seafood-and-noodle" pattern protective (OR 0.74), licensing "feed the person you have" — lower-GI swaps within local hawker foods (brown rice, wholegrain bee hoon, Nutri-Grade A/B drink swaps) over unfamiliar diet regimens.

- **Confinement — the postpartum month.** Fok 2016 (`fok2016confinement`) quantifies near-universal, ethnically-patterned adherence (Chinese *zuo yue zi*, Malay *berpantang*, Indian practices); Teo 2018 (`teo2018confinement`) shows it is a structured dietary pattern with mental-health correlations. Practical counselling: alcohol (rice/tonic wine) cooked into confinement dishes enters breast milk (HealthHub/KKH advise feeding/expressing before, or ~2–3 h after, alcohol-containing food); watch high sodium/fat; avoid wholesale exclusion of "cooling" fruit/veg (vitamin C, fibre, folate).

- **Breastfeeding — the initiation-vs-duration gap.** Near-universal initiation (~99%, NBS 2011) collapses to very low *exclusive* breastfeeding at 6 months — teach the gap, not the low-precision "~1%" headline. Ties to `smjFeeding2023`, `whoBreastfeeding` (BFHI).

- **Everyday local context:** kopi/teh, Milo, bubble tea for the caffeine message; sashimi, raw oysters, half-boiled eggs, raw-egg-in-porridge, cold deli meats and large predatory fish (swordfish, marlin, tuna sashimi, shark) for food safety; substantial Indian/Hindu and Buddhist vegetarian populations for the B12/iron message; halal needs reassurance (halal-certified supplements; avoid gelatine/alcohol-based tinctures), not deficiency-correction.

## 7. Pillar intersections

**Nutrition × physical activity.** The chapter's spine is internal physiology vs external lever, and activity is the cleanest external lever: ~150 min/week moderate aerobic activity (ACOG 804) lowers GDM, pre-eclampsia and caesarean risk and helps keep gestational weight gain in range — directly countering the internal insulin-resistance driver. Frame activity and the modest energy increment together: pregnancy is not a mandate to eat more and move less.

**Nutrition × social/cultural.** Singapore confinement is the flagship intersection — a near-universal, structured, mostly-benign practice (Fok 2016; Teo 2018) resting on the hot/cold model. The competency is *respectful negotiation*: support the harmless, guide only the genuinely risky (alcohol in cooking, sodium/fat load, "cooling"-food avoidance). The same cultural lens applies to culturally-familiar GDM counselling (de Seymour's protective seafood-noodle pattern), vegetarian pregnancy (Indian/Hindu, Buddhist communities), and halal reassurance.

**Nutrition × the War on Diabetes.** GDM is the lifecourse hinge. Physiological insulin resistance (Kampmann) overwhelming beta-cell reserve produces GDM, which carries a ~7-fold future T2DM risk (Bellamy, `bellamy2009`) — making pregnancy an early, actionable window on a woman's metabolic trajectory and tying Ch.9 directly to `mohWarOnDiabetes`. The payoff continues into lactation: breastfeeding improves maternal insulin sensitivity and lowers future maternal T2DM risk — a concrete reason to support a *GDM mother* to breastfeed.

## 8. Clinical application & case ideas

**Flagship case — "Mrs Devi", 26 weeks (case-player-ready, MCQ-branching).** A multi-ethnic, recognition-level GDM scenario that also carries the physiology, micronutrient and food-safety threads. MCQ-only (no live AI patient-chat — that belongs to the Integrated cases chapter). Suggested branching nodes:

1. **Universal-screening justification.** Mrs Devi "feels well" and has few classical risk factors. *Should the OGTT be offered?* Correct branch offers it and articulates the universal-screening logic (local risk-factor screening would have missed ~half of cases). Weak branch reassures-and-skips → corrected. **Do-no-harm angle:** the *tempting* action (reassure a well-feeling woman, avoid an inconvenient fasting test) is the harmful one; the case teaches that withholding the test is the error.
2. **Haemodilution vs true anaemia.** Her Hb has fallen 12.5 → 10.8 g/dL. *Physiological haemodilution, iron-deficiency anaemia needing investigation, or reassure-and-ignore?* Best path recognises the dilutional driver *and* still checks ferritin/MCV. **Do-no-harm angle:** neither over-treating a normal dip nor dismissing a real deficiency.
3. **Folate timing/dose.** *Was she on folic acid before conception, and — given raised BMI — was the dose right?* Surfaces the 400 µg vs 4–5 mg split and the pre-conception timing point.
4. **Fetal-hyperinsulinaemia chain.** *Why is her baby at risk of hypoglycaemia in the first hours after birth?* Tests mechanism (fetal insulin stays high after the maternal glucose supply is cut), not management.
5. **Culturally-familiar dietary counselling.** The "win" branch keeps her usual meals but swaps white rice → brown rice/wholegrain bee hoon and the sweet drink → water/Nutri-Grade A/B. The over-reach branch tries to prescribe insulin/glucose targets → corrected as the obstetric/diabetes team's role (by referral / endocrinology). **Do-no-harm angle:** don't over-medicalise first-line dietary management.
6. **Postpartum follow-up.** *GDM resolves after delivery — what next?* Correct: 75 g OGTT at 4–12 weeks + lifelong periodic screening (~7-fold T2DM risk); weak "discharge, no follow-up" → corrected. Explicit War-on-Diabetes tie-in.

**Supporting vignettes (per-chapter MCQ items):**
- *"Eating for two"* — a normal-BMI woman at 10 weeks asks how many extra calories now (correct: essentially none yet).
- *GWG by BMI* — pre-pregnancy BMI 27 → which IOM range (7–11.5 kg)? Name one risk of too-little (SGA/preterm) and one of too-much (macrosomia/caesarean).
- *Physical activity* — a previously-jogging low-risk woman: continue up to ~150 min/week.

## 9. Common misconceptions & pitfalls

1. **"Eat for two."** Needs rise only ~+340 kcal (T2) / +452 kcal (T3), ~nil T1 — a snack, not a doubling; measured expenditure often rises even less. Lactation (~+500 kcal) is the stage where it nearly applies.
2. **"A falling mid-pregnancy Hb means she's anaemic and needs iron."** Not necessarily — plasma volume (~48%) outpaces red-cell mass (~25%), so Hb falls by dilution. Distinguish from true iron-deficiency (ferritin/MCV, pregnancy-specific cut-offs).
3. **"hPL causes the insulin resistance of pregnancy."** Oversimplified — the 50–60% fall is multifactorial (hPL, progesterone, oestrogen, cortisol, placental GH, TNF-α); no single hormone explains it.
4. **"Insulin resistance in pregnancy is abnormal."** It is a normal adaptation sparing glucose for the fetus; the pathology is failure to compensate (GDM).
5. **"The main GWG risk is gaining too much."** Both directions carry risk (U-shaped); only ~30% land in range.
6. **"One weight-gain target fits all."** GWG is stratified by pre-pregnancy BMI — and even those WHO-BMI-based ranges may not fit Asian women optimally.
7. **"Pregnant women should rest and avoid exercise."** ~150 min/week moderate activity is recommended and lowers GDM/pre-eclampsia/caesarean risk.
8. **"Start folic acid when you find out you're pregnant."** Too late — the neural tube closes by ~28 days; start before conception.
9. **"Everyone needs the same folic-acid dose."** 400 µg general vs 4–5 mg for prior-NTD/high-risk (obesity, diabetes, anticonvulsants).
10. **"Pregnancy means much more calcium."** The requirement barely rises because absorption doubles; the RDA is unchanged.
11. **"More vitamin A is good for the baby."** Preformed retinol/liver/retinoid acne drugs are teratogenic; only β-carotene from plants is safe.
12. **"Avoid all fish because of mercury."** Over-restriction backfires (worse child neurodevelopment); avoid only high-mercury species, eat low-mercury fish 2–3×/week.
13. **"Iron deficiency in pregnancy is a poor-country problem."** ~74% of Singaporean women are iron-deficient by the third trimester.
14. **"Iodine is covered by any salt."** SG iodisation is voluntary; status cannot be assumed.
15. **"GDM screening is only for high-risk women."** Singapore screens universally because risk-based selection missed ~half of local cases.
16. **"GDM ends when the baby is born."** ~7-fold lifetime T2DM risk persists; postpartum OGTT at 4–12 weeks + lifelong screening.
17. **"The 18.9% is Singapore's current rate under today's criteria."** It is the GUSTO figure on 1999 WHO criteria, not IADPSG — teach "~1 in 5", flag it as cohort/era/criteria-dependent.
18. **"The IADPSG cut-offs mark a natural threshold."** HAPO showed a continuous relationship; the cut-offs are a chosen operational line (~1.75× odds), not a biological cliff.
19. **"Macrosomia and neonatal hypoglycaemia are unrelated."** They are two ends of one chain (maternal glucose in → fetal insulin out → excess growth, then hypoglycaemia once the glucose supply is cut).
20. **"A little wine in confinement chicken is fine while breastfeeding."** Alcohol does pass into milk; time feeds around it or use alcohol-free versions.
21. **"A little alcohol in pregnancy is okay."** No known safe amount or trimester — abstinence.
22. **"Caffeine under 200 mg/day is proven safe."** Observational, threshold-free dose-response — a pragmatic guardrail, not a safe line; less is better.
23. **"Pump and dump clears alcohol from milk."** Only time clears it; pumping relieves fullness, not alcohol.
24. **"If the mother eats well, her milk has everything — including vitamin D."** Milk is naturally low in vitamin D regardless of diet; the *infant* is supplemented.
25. **"A vegan mother's milk is fine as long as she feels well."** She can look healthy while producing B12-critically-low milk causing severe infant neuro-disease — vegan/vegetarian mothers must supplement B12.
26. **"Milk-boosting soups/herbs reliably increase supply."** Galactagogue evidence is low-certainty; frequent effective milk removal is the real driver; some herbs (fenugreek) carry safety caveats.
27. **"Breastfeeding only benefits the baby."** It also lowers future maternal T2DM/cardiovascular and breast/ovarian-cancer risk — especially valuable after GDM.
28. **"Halal or vegetarian diets cause specific pregnancy deficiencies."** Halal itself creates none (a supplement-sourcing issue); vegetarian risk is B12/iron, addressable by planning.
29. **"Morning sickness just has to be endured / it's always benign."** First-line measures (small frequent meals, ginger, vitamin B6) help; but hyperemesis with weight loss, ketosis and dehydration needs referral — and thiamine before any glucose/dextrose, to prevent Wernicke's encephalopathy.
30. **"Just take the iron tablet — tolerability doesn't matter."** GI intolerance is the main driver of non-adherence; timing (with vitamin C, away from tea/calcium) and alternate-day dosing are the practical fixes, and iron worsens the near-universal constipation.
31. **"Folic acid is for once you're pregnant."** Too late for the neural tube — and the wider point is the *pre-conception window*: folate already running, plus weight/glycaemia optimised and teratogens stopped, before conception.

## 11. Evidence gaps / open questions + CITATION HYGIENE

**Evidence gaps / open questions.**
- **GUSTO 18.9% is on 1999 WHO criteria**, not IADPSG — the single most important caveat. Teach "~1 in 5" and flag it as cohort/era/criteria-dependent; modern IADPSG thresholds detect a different (generally larger) case set. **⚠ CORRECTION (adversarial critic):** the earlier "these ethnic numbers are resolved/verifiable" claim was WRONG. The per-ethnicity GDM rates (Chinese 21.0%, Malay 12.1%, Indian 22.3%) and the precise "51.6% (Chinese 33.3%)" sensitivity are **not confirmable at abstract level** and **directly contradict the parent overview dossier** (`08-life-cycle-undernutrition.md` §11), which removed the ethnic numbers as unverifiable / cohort-composition-like. **Do NOT print them.** Use "~1 in 5 overall, with an ethnic gradient" and "risk-factor screening would have missed ~half of cases." Restore a number only against a specific full-text table/page.
- **No SG-specific iodine-in-pregnancy status study exists** — frame iodine adequacy generally; iodised salt is a sensible default.
- **No standard "Asian GWG table"** — teach the IOM-2009 framework plus the honest fit-caveat; do not fabricate a local range.
- **Vitamin D evidence weakened** on the 2024 Cochrane re-appraisal — present benefits cautiously.
- **Small choline trial (n=26)** — teach choline as "emerging/under-recognised", not settled.
- **Caffeine evidence is observational** (reverse-causation possible: nausea reduces intake); present <200 mg/day as a guardrail, not proven safety.
- **Post-2011 Singapore National Breastfeeding Survey** — confirm whether a newer survey exists before quoting the "~1% EBF at 6 months" figure; teach the initiation-vs-duration *gap* instead.
- **Local infant vitamin-D practice** — confirm whether HPB routinely recommends universal infant supplementation given local sun exposure before asserting a Singapore recommendation (infant *dosing* is the infancy chapter's to own regardless).
