# Brain Health & Dementia — Author Dossier

## 1. Scope

This is the closing disease chapter of Part IV, pitched at the **worried-well patient question**: *"What should I eat to reduce my risk of, or slow my progression to, dementia?"* The chapter answers that question **honestly and in graded tiers**, then pivots to the **later-disease** nutrition problem (undernutrition, feeding difficulty, and the do-no-harm feeding decision).

The spine is deliberately deflationary, because the honest evidence is deflationary:

1. **The biggest lever is vascular.** *What is good for the heart is good for the brain.* Managing blood pressure, glucose, lipids, weight, smoking and physical inactivity is the most causally-supported, most actionable message — and it is **identical to cardiovascular prevention** already taught in the CVD/Hypertension and Type 2 Diabetes chapters.
2. **Dietary patterns (MIND / Mediterranean) are a "low-regret" choice, not a proven cure.** Observational cohorts are impressive; the one purpose-built prevention RCT (the MIND trial) was essentially **null**. Recommend the pattern because it is safe, cheap, and good for the vessels — not because it is proven to prevent dementia.
3. **Supplements are hype (do-no-harm).** No supplement has robust RCT evidence for prevention; several carry harm signals. This is the chapter's clearest "don't waste your money / don't do harm" teaching set.
4. **In advanced dementia, the nutrition problem inverts.** Weight loss and feeding difficulty are an expected, disease-driven terminal milestone — not a fixable deficit. **Tube feeding does not help**; careful hand/comfort feeding is preferred; the generalist recognises the trajectory and refers for goals-of-care.

**Altitude:** recognise the modifiable levers, counsel honestly, and refer. **No memorised drug names or dosing.** Pathophysiology is taught at *recognition depth only*, built explicitly on the CVD/Hypertension and Type 2 Diabetes chapters by name.

## 2. Learning objectives

By the end of the chapter the first-year student should be able to:

- **LO1.** Distinguish, at recognition depth, the **two pathways** to dementia — neurodegenerative (amyloid-β plaques + tau tangles) and vascular (cerebral small-vessel disease) — and explain why **mixed pathology is the rule, not the exception**.
- **LO2.** Explain the **vascular thesis** ("what's good for the heart is good for the brain"): the same cardiometabolic risk factors from the CVD/Hypertension and Type 2 Diabetes chapters drive the vascular contribution to dementia, and this is the modifiable pathway diet acts through.
- **LO3.** State and correctly qualify the **Lancet Commission "~45%" figure** as a *population-attributable* estimate across 14 factors — a theoretical population ceiling assuming full causality and full elimination, **not** an individual guarantee or an RCT-proven effect.
- **LO4.** Grade the **dietary-pattern evidence honestly**: strong observational signal (MIND cohort HR ~0.47) vs the **null MIND prevention RCT**; and explain *why* the numbers diverge (confounding, reverse causation, short trials, active/improved controls).
- **LO5.** Counsel a worried-well patient with an **evidence-informed, non-over-promising** message: manage vascular risk; adopt a brain-healthy *pattern* as low-regret; skip unproven supplements; stay physically, socially and cognitively active; correct hearing/vision.
- **LO6.** Recognise **undernutrition and weight loss** as an expected feature of advancing dementia, and articulate the **do-no-harm feeding position**: tube feeding does not improve survival, comfort, aspiration or pressure ulcers in advanced dementia; **careful hand/comfort feeding is preferred**; refer for goals-of-care.
- **LO7.** Identify **when to refer** (cognitive symptoms, rapid decline, established dementia, complex feeding decisions) rather than manage on diet advice alone.

## 4. Key concepts / didactic spine

**Beat 1 — Two pathways, one brain (recognition depth).**
Dementia in older adults is usually a *blend*: (a) **neurodegeneration** — extracellular amyloid-β plaques and intracellular hyperphosphorylated tau tangles, synaptic/neuronal loss, cortical atrophy (the amyloid cascade is the dominant *but contested* model — flag honestly); and (b) **vascular** — cerebral small-vessel disease (cSVD). Build cSVD directly on the **Hypertension chapter's "end-organ damage" concept**: chronic high pressure → arteriolosclerosis (concentric fibrotic wall thickening) → impaired autoregulation → white-matter hyperintensities, lacunar infarcts (≤15 mm), and microbleeds. **Diabetes is a vascular-pathway modifier** (small-vessel + branch-atheromatous injury, accelerated atrophy) — build on the **Type 2 Diabetes chapter**; do *not* invent a distinct "diabetic dementia."

**Beat 2 — Mixed pathology is the rule.** In oldest-old autopsy cohorts, the large majority have ≥1 co-occurring neuropathology and pure single-pathology dementia is uncommon. **Teaching cornerstone (Nun Study):** among people with equivalent Alzheimer pathology, those *with* infarcts were far more likely to be clinically demented — **vascular lesions lower the threshold** at which neurodegeneration produces clinical dementia. Teach the *safe, defensible* version: vascular and AD injury **sum** to worsen cognition (the "additive vs synergistic" debate is unsettled — see §11), so **reducing the vascular load lowers total burden regardless of which model is right.**

**Beat 3 — The vascular thesis: "what's good for the heart is good for the brain."** The shared cardiometabolic risk factors (BP, glucose, lipids, weight, smoking, inactivity) are the single most actionable, evidence-based, first-year-appropriate lever — *identical to standard cardiovascular prevention.* Nutrition sits **inside** this vascular story: the diet lever works largely by improving cardiometabolic health.

**Beat 4 — The "~45%" number, taught honestly.** Up to ~45% of dementia is *associated with* 14 potentially modifiable factors across the life course (Lancet Commission 2024). This is a **population-attributable fraction** — a theoretical ceiling assuming full causality and complete population-level elimination. It is **not** a promise that an individual who fixes their risk factors cuts personal risk by 45%, and it is **not** RCT proof. Teach the paradox: individual relative risks are modest (mostly 1.1–1.7) and per-factor PAFs are small (1–7%); the "big number" comes from **summing many common exposures** across a lifetime, not from one dominant cause. Note **midlife** is the key window (many factors act in midlife; late-life measures are contaminated by reverse causation).

**Beat 5 — Dietary patterns: cohort optimism vs trial reality.** The cleanest worked example of "impressive HR does not survive the RCT." Observational MIND cohort: HR ~0.47 for incident AD (top vs bottom tertile). The **MIND prevention RCT was null** over 3 years (no added benefit over an *improved* control diet). The honest read: a healthy pattern (Mediterranean/MIND/DASH/generally high-quality) is **reasonable to recommend — safe, cheap, cardioprotective — but not proven to prevent dementia.**

**Beat 6 — Multidomain lifestyle: one positive cognition trial, several nulls, no dementia-incidence proof.** FINGER (positive on a *cognition composite*); US POINTER (both arms improved, small structured-vs-self-guided difference, **no no-treatment control**); several nulls elsewhere. No RCT has yet shown reduced **dementia incidence**. Benefit plausibly runs largely through the vascular-risk arm.

**Beat 7 — Vascular-risk RCT evidence (the honest best case).** SPRINT MIND: intensive BP control significantly reduced **MCI** and the **MCI-or-dementia composite** (secondary endpoints) but **not probable dementia alone** (primary endpoint; trial stopped early for cardiovascular benefit → underpowered for dementia). This is the strongest RCT signal that treating a vascular risk factor protects cognition — stated with its honest caveat.

**Beat 8 — Supplements: evidence-vs-hype, do-no-harm.** No supplement has robust prevention evidence. B-vitamins (null for cognition; a subgroup imaging signal only), omega-3 pills (null RCTs; *eat fish as food, not capsules*), vitamin E (null in prevention/MCI + **harm** at high dose), ginkgo (definitively null + bleeding/interaction caution), coconut oil/MCT (no good evidence + saturated-fat CV downside), "medical foods" (a regulatory category, not proof). Red-flag pattern: surrogate/imaging endpoints, post-hoc APOE4 subgroups, small single-centre pilots, industry sponsorship, "medical food" loophole.

**Beat 9 — The inversion: undernutrition & the graded feeding beat.** Weight loss and eating problems are an **expected terminal milestone** of advanced dementia (~86% develop an eating problem; high 6-month mortality thereafter). There are **no RCTs** of tube-vs-oral feeding; consistent observational evidence shows **no survival/comfort/aspiration/ulcer benefit** and signals of **harm** (pressure ulcers, pneumonia). Every major society (AGS, ESPEN, Choosing Wisely) converges: **careful hand/comfort feeding is the default; refer for goals-of-care.** Correcting *genuine* B12/folate deficiency, and supporting oral intake/feeding assistance in *mild-moderate* disease, remain standard care and are separate.

**Beat 10 — Counselling the worried well without false hope.** Validate the fear → lead with vascular/metabolic risk → offer the *pattern* not magic foods (flag the null MIND RCT) → actively de-prescribe/discourage supplements → bundle exercise/social/cognitive activity/sleep/hearing-vision → watch for weight loss and caregiver strain later → refer red flags.

## 6. Singapore context

- **Burden and trajectory.** Prevalence among Singapore residents ≥60 was **8.8% (2023)**, a non-significant decline from **10% (2013)**; ~85% of that decline is attributable to rising education/employment across cohorts — a locally-actionable illustration that population-level dementia risk is partly modifiable (cognitive-reserve mechanism). About **73,900** older Singaporeans are affected; MOH grey-literature projects ~152,000 by 2030 (cite as policy projection, not peer-reviewed). Prevalence still rises steeply with age (~1 in 2 at 85+). `subramaniam2025wisedecade`, `subramaniam2015wise`
- **The recognise-and-refer hook.** Undiagnosed dementia fell from **70.6% → 51.5%** over the decade — but roughly **half of community dementia is still undiagnosed.** Most dementia is not being picked up: a direct argument for the generalist's recognise-and-refer role. `subramaniam2025wisedecade`
- **Ethnic disparity ties directly to the vascular thesis.** Indian ethnicity carries ~1.7× higher dementia odds vs Chinese, mirroring the higher cardiometabolic/stroke burden in South Asian populations. Vascular risk is highly prevalent locally (hypertension ~35.5%, diabetes ~9.5% of adults) — so the "brain health = vascular health" message is locally actionable and dovetails with national diabetes/hypertension control efforts. `subramaniam2025wisedecade`
- **Cost and caregivers.** Peer-reviewed societal cost (2013) ≈ **S$532M/yr** (~S$10,245/person), with a large informal (family) share; caregiver burden (mean Zarit ~17) is associated with greater healthcare utilisation. Anchor durable teaching numbers to these peer-reviewed figures (the "S$2.8bn" media figure is unverified grey literature — see §11). `abdin2016wisecost`, `lau2021caregiver`
- **Local diet signals (socio-cultural, per citation principle).** The diet-cognition association replicates in a **multi-ethnic Asian cohort using a general diet-quality index** (AHEI-2010 → MMSE), supporting counselling around *overall diet quality* rather than a branded Western pattern. A distinctly Singaporean signal — **curry/curcumin** intake associated with lower incident MCI/dementia — is a good "hypothesis-generating, not a prescription" teaching example. **Malnutrition risk** predicts incident cognitive decline in older Singaporeans, reinforcing the under-nutrition angle that fits a nutrition text. `huangx2023singaporemec`, `lu2025curry`, `lu2021slasnutrition`
- **The local "brain tonic" market (do-no-harm).** Singapore has a large OTC brain/memory supplement market (ginkgo, fish oil, phosphatidylserine, plus TCM tonics — ginseng, bird's nest). Health supplements are **not pre-market approved** and by law must not carry disease-treatment claims: "HSA-registered ≠ proven effective for dementia." The null ginkgo RCT is the anchor for the "brain tonic doesn't prevent dementia" message. `dekosky2008gem`
- **Locally-adapted prevention.** The **SINGER** pilot shows FINGER-style multidomain lifestyle intervention can be culturally adapted (local produce: kai lan, guava, papaya) — a "translate global evidence to the local plate" example; **feasibility only, not efficacy** (the full SINGER RCT is the test). `chew2021singerpilot`

## 7. Pillar intersections

- **CVD & Hypertension (Part IV) — the load-bearing link.** cSVD is the Hypertension chapter's "end-organ damage" extended to the brain; the vascular contribution to dementia *is* hypertensive/cardiometabolic vascular injury. SPRINT MIND's cognitive signal comes from BP control. Build Beats 1, 3, 7 explicitly on this chapter by name.
- **Type 2 Diabetes (Part IV).** Diabetes as a vascular-pathway modifier (small-vessel + branch-atheromatous injury, accelerated atrophy). Reuse the micro/macrovascular-complications framing; do not create a separate "diabetic dementia."
- **Undernutrition & Malnutrition (Part IV).** The advanced-dementia inversion: weight loss, feeding difficulty, malnutrition risk. Reuse malnutrition-screening and oral-intake-support concepts; the graded feeding beat and comfort feeding live at this intersection.
- **Healthy Ageing (Part II).** Cognitive/brain reserve, the life-course window (midlife matters), sarcopenia/frailty overlap with undernutrition, and hearing/vision correction as high-value modifiable levers.
- **Capstone (Ch. 27, follows).** This chapter's evidence-graded counselling and recognise-and-refer scaffolding feeds the integrated cases.

## 8. Case ideas (case-player-ready)

> Per house style, in-chapter cases are **MCQ/choice-only**. The live AI patient-chat belongs only to the Integrated Cases chapter.

**Case A — "What should I eat to not get dementia?" (the worried-well, core case).**
A 52-year-old whose mother has dementia asks what diet will prevent it. She has BP 148/92 and a fasting glucose in the prediabetes range. She has read that the MIND diet "cuts Alzheimer's by 50%" and wants a supplement recommendation.
- *MCQ 1:* Best first counselling priority → **manage vascular/metabolic risk (BP, glucose, weight, activity, smoking)** [correct]; vs "start a MIND diet and it will halve your risk"; vs "start fish oil + ginkgo"; vs "no evidence anything helps, do nothing."
- *MCQ 2:* How to honestly frame the "50%" cohort figure → **an observational association, not proven cause; the best prevention RCT (MIND) was null** [correct]; vs "a guaranteed 50% personal reduction"; vs "proven in randomised trials"; vs "only true for Mediterranean populations."
- *MCQ 3:* Supplement request → **discourage; no supplement is proven and some carry harm** [correct]; vs prescribe high-dose vitamin E; vs prescribe ginkgo; vs prescribe coconut oil.
- *Feedback teaching:* the pattern is *low-regret* (cardioprotective), so it is reasonable to recommend — just not as a proven dementia cure.

**Case B — "The 45% number" (evidence-literacy case).**
A patient cites a headline that "45% of dementia is preventable" and wants to know if fixing their cholesterol will cut their risk by that much.
- *MCQ:* Correct interpretation → **a population-attributable estimate assuming full causality and full elimination across 14 factors; not an individual guarantee, not RCT-proven** [correct]; vs "yes, ~45% personal reduction from lipids alone"; vs "only vascular factors count"; vs "the number is made up / meaningless."

**Case C — Do-no-harm feeding case (required).**
An 84-year-old with advanced dementia (bed-bound, minimal verbal output) is eating less and losing weight. The family, distressed, asks the team to "put in a feeding tube so she doesn't starve." The ward reflex is to consult for PEG.
- *MCQ 1:* Best evidence-based recommendation → **careful hand/comfort feeding; explain tube feeding does not improve survival, comfort, aspiration or pressure ulcers, and may cause harm; refer for goals-of-care** [correct]; vs "insert PEG to prolong survival"; vs "insert NG tube to prevent aspiration pneumonia"; vs "insert PEG to heal the sacral pressure ulcer."
- *MCQ 2:* How to frame weight loss to the family → **an expected feature of advanced dementia, not a reversible nutritional deficit** [correct]; vs "a sign of neglectful feeding"; vs "curable with high-calorie supplements"; vs "means she is imminently dying today."
- *Do-no-harm learning:* the harm is the *reflex* ("not eating → insert a tube"); the skill is recognising the trajectory, communicating honestly, and referring. (Correcting genuine B12/folate deficiency and supporting oral intake earlier in disease are separate and appropriate.)

**Case D — Supplement de-prescribing (Singapore-flavoured).**
A 68-year-old brings a bag of brain/memory supplements bought at a pharmacy (ginkgo, fish oil, a TCM "brain tonic") and asks which to keep for prevention.
- *MCQ:* Best response → **none is proven to prevent dementia; ginkgo RCT was null; fish oil pills null (eat fish as food); note bleeding/interaction risk with ginkgo; redirect spending to diet quality + vascular control** [correct]; vs "keep the ginkgo, it's proven"; vs "add high-dose vitamin E"; vs "HSA registration means they work."

## 9. Common misconceptions

1. **"45% of dementia is preventable — so fix your risk factors and cut *your* risk by 45%."** No — it is a *population-attributable* ceiling (full causality + full elimination assumed), not an individual guarantee, and not RCT-proven.
2. **"The MIND diet is proven to prevent Alzheimer's (it cut risk 53%)."** The 53% is *observational*; the purpose-built **MIND prevention RCT was null.** Recommend the pattern as low-regret, not as proven prevention.
3. **"Brain supplements protect the brain."** No supplement has robust prevention evidence; ginkgo is definitively null; omega-3 pills are null (eat fish, not capsules); high-dose vitamin E may *increase mortality*.
4. **"Coconut oil / MCT is a natural Alzheimer's remedy."** No credible RCT evidence; coconut oil is ~90% saturated fat (cardiovascular do-no-harm concern).
5. **"'Medical food' means it's an approved, proven treatment."** It's a *regulatory/marketing category* with a lower bar than a drug — not proof of efficacy.
6. **"A person with advanced dementia who stops eating will starve unless we tube-feed."** Weight loss/feeding difficulty is an expected terminal feature; tube feeding does not improve survival, comfort, aspiration or pressure ulcers, and may cause harm. Careful hand/comfort feeding is preferred.
7. **"A feeding tube prevents aspiration pneumonia and heals bedsores."** Evidence shows the opposite/no benefit: PEG is associated with *more* new pressure ulcers and a pneumonia signal.
8. **"Diet dissolves plaques and tangles."** No credible mechanism or RCT supports a food/supplement clearing amyloid/tau; where diet helps the brain it almost certainly acts through *small-vessel* protection (BP/glucose/lipids).
9. **"Obesity/low activity in old age protects against dementia."** A reverse-causation trap: pre-dementia weight loss and activity decline in the prodrome distort late-life measures; midlife is the meaningful window.
10. **"There's a distinct 'diabetic dementia.'"** Diabetes is a *vascular-pathway modifier*, not a separate neurodegenerative disease.

## 11. Evidence gaps / "verify before printing" flags

1. **"45.3%" false precision (Livingston 2024).** Every authoritative secondary source cites "~45%"/"almost half"; the modelled "45.3%" could **not** be independently verified. **Use "~45%"** in print unless the exact figure can be sourced to a specific line/table in the primary PDF. (Recurring error class in this project.)
2. **Mechanism wording (Livingston 2024).** The Commission lists vascular damage as **one of several** mechanisms ("reducing vascular damage, dementia neuropathology, stress, and inflammation while increasing cognitive and brain reserve"). Do **not** write "*the* core mechanism." Applied inline in Beat 4 / row 1; keep the softened wording.
3. **"Dominant factors" wording (Livingston 2024).** By per-factor PAF, only high LDL (7%) among the six vascular/metabolic factors is a top contributor; the largest *individual* factors are non-vascular (hearing loss 7%, education/social isolation 5% each; HTN/diabetes/smoking/inactivity ~2%, obesity ~1%). **Do not call the six vascular factors "the dominant ones"** — say "many of the modifiable factors" and make the collective-cluster point instead.
4. **Burley 2026 — 7-factor, not 9-factor.** The Singapore combined PAF of **20.1%** is a **7-factor** figure (excludes hearing loss and alcohol misuse), though the paper's full framework spans 9 factors. **Corrected in row 8 / §6.** Use only as a "PAFs are population-specific" caveat, not as a truer "lower" number.
5. **PREDIMED-NAVARRA MCI clause misattribution (corrected).** The "MedDiet+EVOO had lowest MCI incidence" finding is **not** in Martínez-Lapiscina 2013 (n=522, MMSE/Clock only). If used, cite the **companion EVOO substudy** (`martinezlapiscina2013evoo`, n=285, OR 0.34). Rows 13/12b handle this split — do not attribute the MCI clause to the main NAVARRA paper.
6. **PREDIMED P-values corrected.** Valls-Pedret 2015: global cognition **P=.005** (not .008), frontal/executive **P=.003** (not .004); memory (nuts) P=.04 is correct. Also note PREDIMED's **2018 retraction/republication** over randomisation irregularities (cognition signal survived; keep the cautious "secondary outcome, weak comparator" framing). A 2015 erratum exists for this paper — confirm it does not alter these values before print.
7. **VITACOG endpoint label.** Brain atrophy was the study's **primary** outcome (cognition underpowered), not "secondary." Corrected in row 18. The caveat (imaging signal, not a proven cognitive/dementia benefit) stands.
8. **Additive vs synergistic (mixed dementia) — unsettled.** Hainsworth 2024 says the SVD–AD interaction is **additive**; Custodio 2017 (and older Nun-Study-derived framing) says **synergistic**. Teach the safe version (they *sum*; reducing vascular load helps regardless). Flag the disagreement, don't adjudicate it.
9. **Author-list errors already corrected — apply the corrected strings.** Three verified papers had fabricated/incorrect middle authors in the proposed metadata: **Subramaniam 2025** (`subramaniam2025wisedecade`), **Huang L 2023** (`huangl2023mindmeta`), and **Nucci 2024** (`nucci2024medmeta`). Use the corrected author lists in §12 — do **not** reuse the originally-proposed strings.
10. **Snowdon 1997 DOI + effect-size caution.** DOI is **10.1001/jama.1997.03540340047031** (was blank). The dramatic OR 20.7 has an extremely wide CI (1.5–288.0) in a small, all-female, highly-educated cohort — **cite the threshold-lowering effect qualitatively**, not the exact OR.
11. **Grey-literature figures — attribute, don't present as peer-reviewed.** MOH "152,000 by 2030," DFSG/CREST/AIC programme facts, DementiaHub caregiver-survey profile (~33 hrs/wk vs peer-reviewed ~55 hrs/wk — flag the discrepancy, don't average), HSA regulation, and the unverified "S$2.8bn" media cost figure. Anchor durable numbers to peer-reviewed sources (Abdin 2016; WiSE).
12. **Andres 2024 caregiver-distress nuance.** Reduced patient QoL with tube feeding is solidly significant; the caregiver anxiety/depression link was significant **unadjusted** but a **trend after adjustment** (P<.10). Keep the hedged wording ("associated with").
13. **"Biessels 2014 — 'macrovascular'."** The source emphasises *small*-vessel disease, microinfarcts and atrophy; "macrovascular" is the weakest-supported element. Soften or drop "macrovascular" (or add a large-vessel-specific citation) if the sentence leans on it.
14. **No RCT has shown reduced dementia *incidence* for diet or multidomain lifestyle.** Every "positive" trial (FINGER, POINTER, PREDIMED cognition) is a *cognition* endpoint, often secondary, sometimes without a no-treatment control. Do not let the prose drift into "proven to prevent dementia." World-Wide FINGERS / full SINGER RCT are ongoing — state as "not yet answered."
15. **Curry/curcumin secondary endpoint (Lu 2025).** The significant, dose-dependent result is for **incident MCI/dementia**; the plain "cognitive decline" trend was **non-significant** in the fully adjusted model. Cite specifically for the MCI/dementia dose-response; do not overstate to general decline.
