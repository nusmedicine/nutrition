## 1. Scope (one paragraph)

This chapter builds *midlife literacy and myth-busting* for a first-year medical student: it teaches what the menopausal transition actually changes about a woman's nutrition-relevant physiology, and — just as importantly — what it does **not** change, so the student can counsel from evidence rather than folklore. The didactic spine runs: (1) menopause is a defined reproductive transition (peri- then post-menopause) driven by falling oestrogen, with a Singapore mean age of natural menopause of ~49 years and a locally distinct symptom profile (somatic joint/muscle aches and sleep problems dominate; classic hot flushes are comparatively uncommon); (2) the flagship **weight-and-metabolism myth** — total midlife weight gain tracks chronological ageing and lifestyle, *not* menopause per se, and fat-free-mass-adjusted energy expenditure does not "crash" at menopause; what *is* menopause-specific is a shift in body **composition** (visceral/android fat redistribution, lean-mass loss); (3) the menopause-specific *acceleration* of bone loss around the final menstrual period (FMP), the oestrogen→RANKL mechanism at recognition level, and the honest magnitude of what diet (calcium/vitamin D permissive, protein modest) versus loading exercise can do; (4) the menopause-linked cardiometabolic shift (LDL-C/apoB rise at the FMP, visceral fat, metabolic-syndrome risk) and pattern-level prevention; and (5) the **phytoestrogen/soy evidence-vs-hype flagship** — soy foods are safe and reasonable (and, at Asian intakes, associated with lower breast-cancer risk, including in survivors), but isoflavone supplements are not a proven hot-flush cure. Throughout, the chapter supplies *nutrition levers + when-to-refer* and hands specialist material (bone diagnosis/drugs, CVD management, HRT, appraisal method, counselling technique) to the named chapters below. HRT/MHT prescribing is out of scope entirely.

## 2. Learning objectives

By the end, a student can…

- [ ] **LO1 — Recognise** the menopausal transition as a defined oestrogen-withdrawal event, state the Singapore mean age of natural menopause (~49 y) and the locally dominant symptom profile (somatic/sleep over vasomotor), and explain why the "menopause = hot flushes" Western script misreads many Asian patients.
- [ ] **LO2 — Appraise and debunk** the two flagship midlife myths: that menopause *causes* weight gain (total weight gain tracks ageing/lifestyle; the menopause-specific change is fat *redistribution* to visceral depots + lean-mass loss), and that "metabolism crashes at menopause" (FFM-adjusted energy expenditure is stable from ~20–60 y).
- [ ] **LO3 — Anticipate** the menopause-specific *acceleration* of bone loss around the FMP and the cardiometabolic shift (LDL-C/apoB rise, visceral adiposity, metabolic-syndrome risk), naming the correct prevention window (peri-/early post-menopause) — while deferring diagnosis and management to Ch.20 and Ch.18.
- [ ] **LO4 — Appraise** the phytoestrogen/soy evidence and counsel accurately: soy foods are safe (and, at Asian intakes, associated with lower breast-cancer risk including in survivors), whereas isoflavone *supplements* give at most a modest, inconsistent hot-flush effect and are not endorsed by menopause societies as a treatment.
- [ ] **LO5 — Counsel** a midlife woman on evidenced nutrition + physical-activity levers (protein + resistance/impact loading for muscle and bone; a Mediterranean/DASH-style pattern; food-first calcium/vitamin D; alcohol minimisation) using weight-inclusive, do-no-harm, consent-based messaging, and recognise when to refer.

## 4. Key concepts to teach (didactic spine)

1. **Menopause is a defined oestrogen-withdrawal transition; the Singapore anchor is ~49 y and the symptom script is local.** Mean age of natural menopause ~49.0 y in a nationwide multi-ethnic sample, with little difference across Chinese/Malay/Indian and higher parity delaying menopause. *(`loh2005sgMenopause`)* Locally, classic vasomotor symptoms are comparatively uncommon and somatic (joint/muscle) complaints dominate. *(`chim2002sgSymptoms`, `logan2023iwhp`)*
2. **FLAGSHIP MYTH #1 — "menopause makes you gain weight" is largely false.** In SWAN, body weight rose ~linearly through the transition with *no* acceleration timed to the FMP; accelerated fat gain and simultaneous lean-mass loss offset each other on the scale. Total weight gain tracks ageing/lifestyle (energy balance). *(`greendale2019swanBodyComp`)*
3. **What IS menopause-specific is body COMPOSITION.** The rate of fat gain roughly doubles starting ~2 y before the FMP and lean mass declines from ~2 y before to ~2 y after, then both flatten; the clinically important change is redistribution to visceral/android (abdominal) fat — the waistline, not the scale, is the tell. *(`greendale2019swanBodyComp`)*
4. **FLAGSHIP MYTH #2 — "metabolism crashes at menopause" is false.** Fat-free-mass-adjusted total daily energy expenditure is stable from age ~20 to ~60 (even in pregnancy) and only declines after ~60 (~0.7%/yr), with no menopause-specific breakpoint. Any real drop in absolute burn comes mainly from losing muscle — which is modifiable. *(`pontzer2021energy`)*
5. **The menopause-specific hook for the protein/muscle message.** Lean-mass loss concentrates around the FMP, marking the sarcopenia-risk onset window; the actual protein-dosing evidence and g/kg targets are taught in **Ch.9** — Ch.10 supplies only the timing hook. *(`greendale2019swanBodyComp`; cross-ref Ch.9)*
6. **Bone loss ACCELERATES in a narrow window around the FMP.** BMD loss begins ~1 y before the FMP and decelerates (not stops) ~2 y after; transmenopause loss ~7.4% lumbar spine / ~5.8% femoral neck (~2%/yr during fast loss, greater at spine than hip); 10-yr totals ~10.6% LS / ~9.1% FN. *(`greendale2012swanBone`)*
7. **Ethnicity modifies the rate — relevant to majority-Chinese Singapore.** Chinese and Japanese women lose femoral-neck bone faster through the transition (~2.1%/yr) than Caucasian women (~1.8%/yr); higher body weight is protective. Report subgroup rates as approximate. *(`greendale2012swanBone`)*
8. **Mechanism at recognition level: oestrogen restrains resorption; when it falls, RANKL rises → more/longer-lived osteoclasts.** In early postmenopausal women, marrow-cell surface RANKL rose ~2–3 fold and correlated with resorption markers; oestrogen reversed it. Keep to a sentence or two. *(`eghbali2003rankl`)* **Hygiene note:** this citation supports the *RANKL-rise* only; do not lean on it for an "OPG falls" clause — the paper found serum OPG unchanged (see §11).
9. **Calcium and vitamin D are PERMISSIVE, not curative.** USPSTF: routine daily ≤400 IU vitamin D + ≤1000 mg calcium gives *no net benefit* for primary fracture prevention in community-dwelling postmenopausal women, insufficient evidence for higher doses, and a small increase in kidney-stone risk. Build the "bone bank" from food + activity; reserve supplements for genuine shortfall/osteoporosis under guidance (→ Ch.20). *(`uspstfCalcium2018`)*
10. **Protein is a modest bone lever (debunks the "protein leaches calcium" myth).** Higher vs lower protein, *when calcium is adequate*, is associated with ~11% lower hip-fracture risk (HR 0.89) and better hip/spine BMD. Not menopause-specific; cross-ref Ch.9 for dosing. *(`groenendijk2019protein`)*
11. **The strongest modifiable lever against menopausal bone loss is mechanical loading.** In LIFTMOR, 8 months of supervised high-intensity resistance + impact training raised lumbar-spine BMD +2.9% (vs −1.2% control) and femoral-neck +0.3% (vs −1.9%). Frame as proof-of-concept that loading beats no loading; everyday message = brisk walking/jogging + strength work (→ physical-activity pillar). *(`watson2018liftmor`)*
12. **The menopause cardiometabolic shift is real but selective.** LDL-C, total cholesterol and apoB accelerate within the ~1-yr window around the FMP (menopause-linked), whereas triglycerides, glucose, insulin and BP drift up with age (not FMP-timed) — an anti-over-attribution point. *(`matthews2009swanLipids`)* Metabolic-syndrome incidence climbs steeply through the transition, highest in perimenopause. *(`janssen2008swanMetS`)* Professional consensus frames midlife as the critical early-prevention window. *(`elkhoudary2020ahaMenopause`)*
13. **Pattern, not single nutrients, is the evidenced diet lever (cross-ref, not owned here).** Mediterranean pattern cut major CV events ~a third in primary prevention (with the PREDIMED retraction/re-analysis caveat → Ch.12); DASH lowers BP; AHA guidance is pattern-level. Management belongs to Ch.18. *(`estruch2018predimed`, `appel1997dash`, `aha2021diet`)*
14. **FLAGSHIP MYTH #3 — phytoestrogens/soy.** Isoflavone *supplements* give at most a modest, inconsistent hot-flush effect (a signal for high-genistein extracts / equol producers); the honest verdict is soy foods are safe and healthy but not a proven flush remedy, and menopause societies do not recommend soy/S-equol as a VMS treatment. *(`lethaby2013cochranePhyto`, `taku2012isoflavones`, `chen2015phyto`, `namsNonhormone2023`)*
15. **MYTH REVERSAL — soy does not cause breast cancer.** At Asian intakes soy is associated with ~29% lower breast-cancer risk and is safe (and associated with better survival) in survivors, including ER+ disease and tamoxifen users; the old fear came from rodent data that do not translate. *(`wu2008soyMeta`, `shu2009shanghai`, `nechuta2012abcpp`, `wu2008sgSoy`)*
16. **Do-no-harm midlife counselling.** No special "menopause diet" (heart/bone health, not symptom cure); alcohol is a linear, no-safe-threshold breast-cancer risk factor and worsens sleep/bone; and disordered-eating risk is real in 40–60 y women — counsel weight-inclusively, with consent, and screen before pushing weight loss (skills → Ch.13/14). *(`denby2023bms`, `hamajima2002alcohol`, `mangweth2014ed`)*
17. **Iron needs FALL after menopause — and new iron-deficiency is a RED FLAG, not a life-stage need.** Once menstruation stops, iron requirements drop from ~18 mg/day to ~8 mg/day (same as an adult man), so routine iron supplements are unnecessary and — since the body has no route to excrete excess iron — potentially harmful. Crucially, *new* iron-deficiency anaemia in a post-menopausal woman is **not** explained by menopause: it warrants investigation for gastrointestinal blood loss (recognise & refer), never a reflexive supplement. *(`odsIron`; the GI-red-flag is standard clinical practice — a clean recognise-and-refer teaching point.)*
18. **Genitourinary syndrome of menopause (GSM) has essentially no dietary lever — the honest "nutrition is not the answer" case.** Vaginal dryness/urinary symptoms are common locally (~20% report vaginal dryness — `chim2002sgSymptoms`; top-three in `logan2023iwhp`) and, unlike bone/cardiometabolic change, **no diet or supplement treats them**. Teach recognition + refer for effective therapy (vaginal moisturisers, topical oestrogen); explicitly warn against "natural remedy" marketing aimed at this symptom.
19. **Sleep is a top local complaint — give it real levers, don't just name it.** Sleep problems rank among the most prevalent SG symptoms (`logan2023iwhp`). Evidenced, low-risk levers: **limit alcohol** (fragments sleep — see §7), **shift caffeine earlier**, **avoid large late meals**, and **manage weight/OSA risk**; persistent disruption with a vasomotor/mood component is a referral cue, not a diet failure. (General sleep-hygiene levers, cross-ref the sleep pillar; no symptom "cure" is claimed.)
20. **Name the commercial pressure students will actually meet.** Midlife women face a booming "menopause weight-loss" and "menopause supplement" market and rising demand for GLP-1 receptor agonists. There is **no menopause-specific diet pill**: obesity pharmacotherapy (incl. GLP-1s) is covered in the *Obesity & Metabolic Syndrome* chapter, and most "menopause supplements" are unproven. Redirect to protein + resistance training + a sound dietary pattern, and appraise specific product claims with the toolkit from *Evidence vs Hype*.

## 6. Singapore context

Singapore has unusually strong, recent local data for a Ch.10 built around Asian, multi-ethnic (Chinese/Malay/Indian) women — cite for practical/socio-cultural and clinical-practice framing, per the SG citation principle, not for basic biology.

- **Age & symptom script differ from the West.** Mean age of natural menopause ~49.0 y with no significant ethnic difference (`loh2005sgMenopause`) — slightly earlier than the ~51 y Western figure, so quote the local number. The symptom profile is *not* the hot-flush stereotype: in a community survey the most prevalent complaint was somatic (low backache with aching muscles/joints, 51.4%) while hot flushes were only 17.6% (`chim2002sgSymptoms`), and in the flagship IWHP cohort 62.6% had ≥1 moderate-to-severe symptom with joint/muscle discomfort and sleep problems on top and hot flushes only fifth (`logan2023iwhp`). **Ethnic gradient:** Indian women report the highest symptom burden, Chinese the least (`loh2005sgMenopause`, `logan2023iwhp`). A Singaporean woman may therefore present with aches and poor sleep rather than flushes.
- **Bone-loss risk applies with extra force locally.** In SWAN, Chinese women — Singapore's majority group — are among the *faster* losers of femoral-neck bone through the transition (`greendale2012swanBone`). Layered on top: despite year-round sun, ~1 in 3 mid-life Singaporean women are vitamin-D deficient, driven by indoor lifestyles/sun-avoidance rather than lack of sun, with Indian (aOR 5.58) and Malay (aOR 3.83) women at markedly higher risk than Chinese (`cheong2021vitDsg`) — a concrete equity/counselling point. Local dietary calcium hovers at/below the RDA (mean ~794 mg/day; 35.2% inadequate, women worse) (`hpbNNS2010`), making food-first calcium (calcium-set tofu/tau kwa, kai-lan and low-oxalate Asian greens, ikan bilis/canned sardines with bones, calcium-fortified soy milk) a realistic non-supplement lever. Screening/management defers to Ch.20; HPB bone-health and physical-activity messaging is the local guidance layer.
- **The soy paradox is native here.** In the Singapore Chinese Health Study, above-median isoflavone intake was associated with lower breast-cancer risk (RR 0.82; postmenopausal RR 0.74) at habitual intakes ~15–20× typical Western levels (`wu2008sgSoy`). Everyday local soy foods — tauhu, taukwa/taupok, tau huay/soybean drink, foojook/beancurd skin, tempeh, edamame — are worth naming so students recognise them, and the higher Asian equol-producer prevalence (~50–55%) may partly explain why soy trials in Asian populations lean positive. Counselling line: soy foods are safe and may be protective, but tofu is not HRT-level flush relief and isoflavone *supplements* are a different, less-tested product.
- **Cardiometabolic burden is real and not uniform across ethnic groups** — but treat this as a *signpost*, not a taught fact here. The specific SG ethnic epidemiology (e.g. Malay/Indian risk at lower BMI cut-points) and its management belong to the *Cardiovascular Disease & Hypertension* chapter; **source the ethnic-risk specifics there.** This dossier deliberately carries no citation for the claim — do not print numbers without one (SG citation principle).
- **Cultural silence & under-served care.** Menopause is often stigmatised or unspoken across communities, sexual-health symptoms especially undisclosed, and primary-care menopause training is limited — so counselling must *proactively and sensitively open the topic and normalise it* (see §11 for the citation-hygiene note on this SG qualitative source). Local practice signpost: KKH (via MCHRI) has issued Singapore's first guidelines on managing the menopause transition — a useful when-to-refer anchor.

## 7. Pillar intersections (nutrition × physical activity)

- **× physical activity for BONE.** This is the load-bearing intersection of the chapter. Diet is *permissive* — adequate calcium/vitamin D and modest protein let remodelling proceed but do not blunt the oestrogen-driven acceleration (`uspstfCalcium2018`, `groenendijk2019protein`) — while progressive resistance + impact loading is the strongest modifiable lever, able to hold or modestly *increase* BMD where supplements alone do not (LIFTMOR: LS +2.9% vs −1.2%; `watson2018liftmor`). Teach as a combined prescription (food-first minerals + loading), cross-ref the physical-activity pillar for exercise prescription and Ch.20 for management. Everyday framing: brisk walking/jogging + strength work, not "self-prescribe heavy lifting."
- **× physical activity for WEIGHT / body composition.** Because the menopause-specific change is fat *redistribution* + lean-mass loss (not a metabolic crash), the evidenced response is **protein + resistance exercise to preserve muscle** plus modest energy balance — *not* aggressive restriction, which accelerates lean and bone loss (`greendale2019swanBodyComp`, `pontzer2021energy`; protein dosing → Ch.9). The waistline, not the scale, tracks the cardiometabolically important change.
- **× sleep / vasomotor symptoms.** Sleep problems are a top-ranked local menopausal complaint (`logan2023iwhp`). Give it concrete, low-risk levers: **limit alcohol** (fragments sleep), **shift caffeine earlier in the day**, **avoid large late meals**, and **manage weight** — while flagging that a vasomotor/mood component may need referral rather than a dietary fix. Be honest about the commonly-repeated vasomotor "triggers" (caffeine, alcohol, spicy/hot food/drink): there is **little good trial evidence either way** — an *absence-of-evidence* point, not a proven trigger list. A brief personal trial-and-error is reasonable, but do **not** teach trigger-avoidance as established fact or use it to justify blanket dietary restriction. A Mediterranean-style pattern is the "safe default": its clear win is the cardiometabolic shift; any symptom benefit is only weakly/observationally suggested (see §5) and should not be over-sold.
- **× substances / alcohol.** Alcohol is the key do-no-harm intersection at midlife: a linear, no-safe-threshold breast-cancer risk factor (+7.1% RR per daily drink; `hamajima2002alcohol`) that *also* worsens sleep and is a recognised risk factor for low bone density/falls — so "a glass of wine for your hot flush" is poor advice. Frame as: minimise alcohol; never recommend it for symptom relief.

## 8. Clinical application & case ideas

**Flagship case (case-player-ready, MCQ branching): the phytoestrogen/soy counselling scenario.**
*Vignette:* Mdm Tan, a 51-year-old Singaporean Chinese woman, 12 months amenorrhoeic with moderate hot flushes and disturbed sleep, asks: *"If I switch to soy milk and eat more tofu, can it replace hormones for my flushes? And my sister says soy causes breast cancer — should I stop?"*

- **Node 1 — the efficacy question.**
  - **Preferred path (correct):** "Soy foods are a safe, healthy part of your diet and reasonable to try, but the best evidence shows only a *modest and inconsistent* effect on hot flushes — much weaker and slower than HRT — so I wouldn't rely on it as a cure. Let's also talk about proven options and, if you'd like, HRT with your doctor." *(Anchors `taku2012isoflavones` + `lethaby2013cochranePhyto` + `namsNonhormone2023`.)*
  - **Hype failure-mode (distractor):** "Yes — soy is a natural HRT; two servings a day will fix the flushes." → **Coach line:** *"Over-promising. Isoflavone extracts cut flush frequency only ~20% and don't work in everyone (partly equol-producer status); menopause societies don't recommend soy as a treatment. Offer it as a safe option, not a guaranteed remedy — and don't imply it equals HRT's ~75% reduction."*
  - **Dismissive failure-mode (distractor):** "Soy is useless and possibly dangerous — avoid it and just tough out the flushes." → **Coach line:** *"Two errors: soy foods are safe (and at Asian intakes associated with lower breast-cancer risk), and dismissing the patient shuts down the conversation. Correct the myth, validate the symptom, and offer evidenced options."*
- **Node 2 — the survivor/safety question (myth reversal).**
  - **Preferred path (correct):** "Dietary soy does not raise breast-cancer risk in humans — the fear came from rodent studies. At local intakes it's associated with *lower* risk, and even breast-cancer survivors, including ER+ and those on tamoxifen, do as well or better eating moderate soy foods. No need to stop." *(Anchors `wu2008soyMeta`, `shu2009shanghai`, `nechuta2012abcpp`, `wu2008sgSoy`.)*
  - **Note for the writer:** keep the food-vs-supplement distinction explicit — the reassurance is for *whole soy foods*, not high-dose isoflavone supplements.

**Other MCQ case ideas (MCQ-only, per house rule):**
1. *"Menopause made me gain weight."* — 50-year-old woman, +3 kg over 4 y, waist growing faster than hips. Correct: total weight gain reflects ageing/lifestyle; the *abdominal/visceral redistribution* is the menopause-linked change; the waistline, not the scale, is the tell. *(`greendale2019swanBodyComp`)*
2. *Energy expenditure across adulthood* — which statement fits Pontzer 2021? Correct: stable ~20–60 y after adjusting for fat-free mass, declining only after ~60. Distractors: "falls sharply at menopause"; "falls steadily from age 30." *(`pontzer2021energy`)*
3. *Rapid bone loss* — 51-year-old Chinese woman, 14 months post-FMP, read that "supplements prevent osteoporosis." Correct: her fast loss is oestrogen-driven (RANKL); routine Ca+vitD gives no primary-prevention benefit and slightly raises stone risk — food-first + loading, refer per Ch.20. *(`greendale2012swanBone`, `uspstfCalcium2018`, `watson2018liftmor`)*
4. *Vitamin D despite the sun* — indoor-working Indian Singaporean woman who avoids sun. Correct: deficiency is common locally from low cutaneous synthesis; food-first + test, manage per Ch.20. *(`cheong2021vitDsg`)*
5. *Anti-over-attribution* — 48-year-old woman with new borderline BP asks if menopause caused it. Correct: LDL-C/apoB are menopause-linked at the FMP, but BP/glucose track ageing; refer per Ch.18. *(`matthews2009swanLipids`)*
6. *Do-no-harm / alcohol* — patient told to "have a nightly glass of wine for the flushes." Correct: no safe threshold, +7% breast-cancer risk per daily drink, worsens sleep/bone; don't recommend alcohol for symptoms. *(`hamajima2002alcohol`)*
7. *Disordered eating red flag* — perimenopausal woman skipping meals, daily weigh-ins on a strict diet. Correct: recognise ED risk (real in this age group), respond weight-inclusively and with consent; skills → Ch.13/14. *(`mangweth2014ed`, `denby2023bms`)*
8. *Iron "top-up" red flag* — 55-year-old woman, 3 y post-menopause, fatigued, buys iron tablets "because women need iron." Correct: iron needs *fell* after menopause; new iron-deficiency here is a GI-bleeding red flag to investigate, not a supplement to start. Distractor: "start iron, recheck in 3 months." *(`odsIron`)*
9. *"Which symptom won't diet fix?"* — patient wants a "menopause diet" for hot flushes, vaginal dryness, bone and heart. Correct: diet meaningfully supports bone/heart risk, has only a modest/unproven effect on flushes, and does **nothing** for GSM (refer). Teaches matching the lever to the problem. *(`chim2002sgSymptoms`, `namsNonhormone2023`)*

## 9. Common misconceptions & pitfalls

- **"Menopause makes you gain weight."** Total weight gain tracks chronological ageing and energy balance; SWAN shows weight rises ~linearly with no menopause-timed jump. *(`greendale2019swanBodyComp`)*
- **"My metabolism crashed at menopause."** FFM-adjusted total energy expenditure is stable 20–60 y; any real drop in absolute burn comes mainly from losing muscle, which is modifiable. *(`pontzer2021energy`)*
- **"Nothing really changes / it's all in my head."** The opposite error — body composition genuinely shifts (visceral fat up, lean mass down) even when the scale barely moves. The waistline is the tell.
- **"Belly fat at midlife is just cosmetic."** Visceral fat is cardiometabolically active and the menopause-linked change most tied to CVD risk (defer management to Ch.18).
- **"Bone loss at menopause is slow and gradual like ageing."** It accelerates to ~2%/yr in the ~3-year transmenopause window, losing ~7% of spine BMD. *(`greendale2012swanBone`)*
- **"Calcium/vitamin D supplements will stop or reverse menopausal bone loss."** They are permissive, not curative; USPSTF found no net fracture-prevention benefit from routine low-dose supplementation plus a small stone-risk increase. *(`uspstfCalcium2018`)*
- **"We have plenty of sun in Singapore, so vitamin-D deficiency isn't real here."** ~1 in 3 mid-life women are deficient despite the climate. *(`cheong2021vitDsg`)*
- **"High-protein diets leach calcium from bone."** With adequate calcium, higher protein is associated with better BMD and ~11% lower hip-fracture risk. *(`groenendijk2019protein`)*
- **"Exercise can't rebuild bone once menopause hits."** Progressive resistance + impact loading increased spine BMD in women with low bone mass. *(`watson2018liftmor`)*
- **"The cholesterol rise at menopause is just ageing" / "everything bad at midlife is menopause."** LDL-C/apoB specifically accelerate at the FMP, but triglycerides/glucose/BP track ageing — avoid both under- and over-attribution. *(`matthews2009swanLipids`)*
- **"Soy/phytoestrogen supplements are a proven natural HRT."** Effect is modest and inconsistent; societies do not recommend them for VMS. *(`taku2012isoflavones`, `lethaby2013cochranePhyto`, `namsNonhormone2023`)*
- **"Soy causes breast cancer / feeds ER+ tumours — survivors should avoid it."** Dietary soy is safe and, at Asian intakes, associated with lower risk and better survival, including ER+ and tamoxifen users. *(`wu2008soyMeta`, `shu2009shanghai`, `nechuta2012abcpp`)*
- **"Soy supplements and soy foods are interchangeable."** The safety/benefit evidence is for whole foods; high-dose extracts are less-tested and not endorsed.
- **"A negative soy trial means soy works for nobody."** Response is heterogeneous partly via equol-producer status; *preliminary* evidence (a single small RCT) suggests giving S-equol directly to non-producers may help. *(`aso2012sequol`)*
- **"There's a special menopause diet that fixes symptoms" / "just avoid triggers."** No — diet supports heart/bone health; symptom-trigger evidence is weak and observational. *(`denby2023bms`, `namsNonhormone2023`)*
- **"Eat less to fix midlife weight."** Under-eating accelerates lean and bone loss and carries real ED risk in this age group; use weight-inclusive, consent-based counselling. *(`mangweth2014ed`)*
- **"A daily glass of wine is a harmless way to relax through menopause."** No safe threshold for breast cancer; worsens sleep and bone. *(`hamajima2002alcohol`)*
- **"Women need iron supplements — especially at menopause."** The opposite: iron needs *fall* once periods stop; routine supplements are unnecessary, and new deficiency is a red flag to investigate (GI blood loss), not to self-treat. *(`odsIron`)*
- **"There must be a diet/supplement for the dryness too."** GSM has no dietary lever — recognise and refer for effective (topical) treatment; be alert to "natural remedy" marketing aimed at this symptom. *(`chim2002sgSymptoms`)*
- **"A menopause supplement / GLP-1 is the answer to midlife weight."** No menopause-specific diet pill exists; anchor on protein + resistance training + pattern, and appraise product claims with the toolkit (*Evidence vs Hype*); obesity pharmacotherapy → *Obesity & Metabolic Syndrome*.
