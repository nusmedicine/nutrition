# The Gut Microbiome — research dossier

## 1. Scope (one paragraph)

This chapter introduces the community of microbes that live in the gut and answers, at first-year
recognition level, four linked questions: *who is in there, what do they do for (and to) us, what
feeds them, and what does that mean for a patient?* It opens with **composition and scale** — the
microbiome as bacteria (plus archaea, fungi, viruses), densest in the colon, roughly **1:1** with
our own cells (busting the old 10:1 figure), and carrying a metabolic "second genome" ~150× the
human gene set — then **how it assembles over a lifetime** (birth, breast-milk HMOs, weaning,
adult-like by about age three, decline in frail old age). The chapter's **owned centrepiece** is
**fibre → short-chain-fatty-acid (SCFA) fermentation**: the colon's anaerobes ferment the fibre and
resistant starch our own enzymes could not digest into **acetate, propionate and butyrate**
(butyrate is the colonocyte's preferred fuel), plus the gases that explain bloating — the mechanism
Ch.5 deliberately deferred here. It then covers **microbial vitamin synthesis** (with the honest
bioavailability caveat), the **"-biotics" family** (ISAPP definitions of pre-, pro-, syn- and
post-biotics, and why *fermented food ≠ probiotic*), and **diet as the dominant, fast-acting,
reversible lever** on the microbiome (fibre diversity — "eat the rainbow / ~30 plants a week"). The
applied close is a **recognise-don't-manage** tour: antibiotics and dysbiosis, the striking
**faecal-microbiota-transplant (FMT)** cure for recurrent *C. difficile*, why direct-to-consumer
"microbiome test kits" are not clinically actionable, and a **cautious, mechanism-first** look at the
gut–brain axis. Singapore-first hooks (the local wholegrain/fibre gap, culturally familiar fermented
foods with an honest live-culture vs cooked distinction, ubiquitous Yakult, and genuine SG cohort
data) keep the physiology food-first and lived. Throughout, the discipline is **honesty about hype**:
the microbiome is a real, diet-modifiable system, and also the single most over-marketed topic in
consumer nutrition — the chapter teaches students to hold both.

## 2. Learning objectives (recognition-level)

By the end of the chapter a first-year student can:

- **LO-1** *Describe* what the gut microbiome is (bacteria-dominant community, densest in the colon),
  *state* the corrected scale (~1:1 with human cells, ~0.2 kg, a metagenome ~150× the human gene
  set), and *name* the two dominant phyla using current nomenclature (Bacillota, Bacteroidota).
- **LO-2** *Explain* that healthy people differ enormously in *which* microbes they carry yet share a
  *functional* core ("functional redundancy"), and *treat* diversity as a loose, context-dependent
  health marker rather than a simple "more is better" target.
- **LO-3** *Outline* how the microbiome assembles across life (delivery mode and — more durably —
  feeding; HMOs feeding *Bifidobacterium*; weaning; adult-like by ~age 3; decline in frail old age),
  and *avoid* the overstatement that C-section "permanently damages" it.
- **LO-4 (owned centrepiece)** *Explain* colonic **fibre → SCFA fermentation**: that anaerobic
  bacteria ferment the fibre/resistant starch the small intestine could not digest into **acetate,
  propionate and butyrate** (≈60:20:20), *state* their main fates (**butyrate → colonocyte fuel**;
  propionate → liver; acetate → systemic circulation), and *explain* the gas by-products (H₂, CO₂,
  CH₄) — the mechanism behind bloating and the Ch.5 lactose hand-off.
- **LO-5** *Explain* why **fibre diversity feeds a diverse microbiome** (cross-feeding trophic chains;
  one microbe's product is another's food), and *connect* low-fibre "Western" eating to a starved,
  less-resilient community and a thinner mucus barrier.
- **LO-6** *State* that gut microbes synthesise some vitamins (K2, several B vitamins) but *explain*
  why this is **not a reliable dietary source** for humans (the B12 site/bioavailability paradox),
  cross-referencing Ch.4.
- **LO-7** *Define* probiotic, prebiotic, synbiotic, postbiotic and fermented food (ISAPP), *explain*
  why **fermented food ≠ probiotic**, and *summarise honestly* the strain- and indication-specific
  state of probiotic evidence.
- **LO-8** *Apply* the chapter at first-year altitude: *recognise* antibiotic-associated dysbiosis and
  *C. difficile*, *recognise* FMT as an ecosystem-restore therapy for recurrent *C. diff* only (and
  refer), *debunk* commercial microbiome test kits and "detox/cleanse" claims, and *hold* gut–brain
  claims with appropriate scientific caution.

## 3. Scope boundaries

**In (this chapter OWNS and teaches):**
- Microbiota composition, scale (the 1:1 correction, mass, metagenome), current phylum names,
  inter-individual variability + functional redundancy, enterotypes-as-continuum.
- Lifetime development (delivery/feeding, HMOs, weaning, stabilisation, ageing).
- **Fibre → SCFA colonic fermentation — the full mechanism** (substrates, the three SCFAs and their
  fates, gases and methanogens, cross-feeding, colonic pH and the mucus barrier, resistant starch).
  This is the definitive home.
- **Colonic gas production** (H₂/CH₄/CO₂) — Ch.5 explicitly handed this mechanism here.
- **Microbial vitamin synthesis** (the *synthesis* angle and its bioavailability caveat).
- The **"-biotics" family + fermented foods** (ISAPP definitions; fermented ≠ probiotic; live-culture
  honesty; probiotic evidence at recognition level).
- **Diet as a microbiome modulator** (fibre diversity, patterns as modulators, non-fibre modulators
  with honest evidence-grading, antibiotics).
- The **gut–brain axis microbial mechanism** (recognition level, cautious).
- Applied/clinical-lived layer: antibiotics/dysbiosis/AAD, *C. diff* + **FMT** (recognise-refer),
  microbiome-test-kit myth-bust, "detox/cleanse/reset" myth-bust (microbiome angle).

  **Ch.6 (Integrative Metabolism)** — now taught *before* this chapter, so SCFA metabolic fates **build

## 4. Key concepts to teach (didactic spine)

**Part 1 — Who is in there (composition, scale, development).**

1. **The gut microbiome is a community — bacteria dominate.** Trillions of microbes (bacteria, plus
   archaea, fungi/mycobiome, viruses/phages) live in the gut; bacteria carry the load-bearing story,
   the rest are minor by mass and largely "dark matter" at this level. → `matijasic2020beyond`
2. **It lives mostly in the colon.** Microbial density climbs along the tract — sparse in the acidic
   stomach and proximal small intestine, densest by orders of magnitude in the **colon** (~10¹¹–10¹²
   cells/g). This is *why* the colon (slow transit, near-neutral pH, little absorption — Ch.5 anatomy)
   is the fermentation chamber. Cross-ref Ch.5; do not re-teach the anatomy. → `donaldson2016gutbiogeography`
3. **Scale, honestly — bust the 10:1 myth.** Bacteria (~3.8×10¹³) and human cells (~3.0×10¹³) are
   roughly **1:1**, not 10:1 (the old ratio ignored red blood cells, ~84% of our cells). Total
   bacterial mass ≈ **0.2 kg (~200 g)**, not "1–2 kg." *Present ratio and mass as the takeaways; the
   exact counts are educator-only.* → `sender2016revised`
4. **The real "wow" is genes.** The collective microbial gene catalogue (metagenome) is on the order
   of **~150× the ~20,000 human genes** — a metabolic "second genome" that lets us extract nutrients
   and make compounds our own cells cannot. *Teach the ~150× ratio; the absolute gene count is
   catalogue-dependent, educator-only.* → `qin2010catalogue`
5. **Who's there — current names.** Two phyla dominate the healthy adult gut: **Bacillota** (formerly
   Firmicutes) and **Bacteroidota** (formerly Bacteroidetes), with smaller **Actinomycetota**
   (incl. *Bifidobacterium*), **Pseudomonadota** and Verrucomicrobiota. Teach the 2021 names, note the
   old ones once in parentheses. → `oren2021valid`
6. **Different people, similar jobs — "functional redundancy."** Two healthy people can carry very
   different *taxa* yet perform similar *functions* (fibre fermentation, SCFA production done by many
   genera). *"We share a functional core, not a taxonomic core."* The old "3 enterotypes" idea is best
   read as a **continuous gradient** (Bacteroides- vs Prevotella-leaning poles), not fixed categories.
   → `moya2016functional`, `arumugam2011enterotypes`, `knights2014rethinking`
7. **It assembles over a lifetime.** Sparse and variable at birth → shaped by delivery mode and, more
   durably, **feeding** (breast-milk **HMOs** selectively feed *Bifidobacterium*) → diversifies at
   weaning → **adult-like by about age 3** (present as a gradual range, not a hard switch) → gradual
   shifts, with **diversity loss in frail/institutionalised old age**. *Nuance:* C-section gives a
   *different early trajectory*, not permanent "damage," and "vaginal seeding" is **not** an
   evidence-based fix (ACOG). → `backhed2015dynamics`, `claesson2012elderly`, `acog2017vaginalseeding`
8. **Diversity is a loose marker, not a target.** Higher diversity broadly tracks with health, but
   there are clear counterexamples — treat it as *one context-dependent signal*, not something to
   maximise or buy in a bottle. → *(source flagged §11)*

**Part 2 — What they do #1: fibre → SCFA fermentation (the owned centrepiece).**

9. **Fermentation = anaerobic bacterial salvage of what our enzymes could not digest.** Non-digestible
   carbohydrate — dietary fibre, resistant starch, some oligosaccharides — escapes small-intestinal
   *enzymatic* digestion (Ch.5) and reaches the colon, where anaerobes **ferment** it. Bacterial
   metabolism, not host hydrolysis: the key contrast with Ch.5. → `koh2016scfa`
10. **The products are three short-chain fatty acids: acetate, propionate, butyrate** — in an
    approximate molar ratio of **~60:20:20** (a range that shifts with diet, substrate, colonic site
    and transit; teach "roughly 60:20:20, and it varies"). → `cummings1987scfa`
11. **Each SCFA has a fate.** **Butyrate is the colonocyte's preferred fuel** (supplies most of the
    colon lining's energy) — the single most robust fate claim. **Propionate** is taken up by the
    **liver** (portal vein) as a gluconeogenic substrate (hepatic detail → Ch.6, taught just before).
    **Acetate** is most
    abundant and most systemic — poorly extracted by the liver, it reaches peripheral tissues and
    other bacteria. → `roediger1982colonocyte`, `donohoe2011butyrate`, `louis2017formation`
12. **Fermentation also makes gas — this is the bloating mechanism Ch.5 deferred.** H₂, CO₂ and (in
    some people) CH₄ are by-products; excess fermentable substrate (undigested lactose — the Ch.5
    hook — or FODMAPs) → more gas → distension/bloating. **Methanogenic archaea** (e.g.
    *Methanobrevibacter smithii*) consume H₂ to make CH₄, keeping H₂ low so fermentation continues.
    → `chaudhary2018methanogens`
13. **Diversity matters because microbes feed each other (cross-feeding).** Many butyrate producers
    cannot break down fibre directly — they live on intermediates: *Bifidobacteria* ferment fibre to
    lactate + acetate, which butyrate producers (*Faecalibacterium prausnitzii*, *Roseburia*) convert
    to butyrate. One microbe's waste is another's food — so a *diverse* substrate feeds a *diverse,
    resilient* community. *(Genus names optional at first-year level — "primary degraders vs butyrate
    producers" is enough.)* → `riviere2016crossfeeding`, `louis2017formation`
14. **Why it matters locally.** SCFAs lower colonic pH (~5.6 proximally → ~6.6 distally), which
    suppresses acid-sensitive pathogens; butyrate fuels the epithelium and supports the **mucus
    barrier**. Framing: *"fibre feeds your gut bugs, and they pay you back in SCFAs."* When fibre runs
    short, the microbiota can turn to eroding the protective mucus layer (mechanistic/animal evidence).
    → `koh2016scfa`, `desai2016fibre`
15. **Substrate shapes the output.** Rapidly fermented soluble fibres (inulin, pectin, β-glucan) give
    prompt SCFA + gas; **resistant starch** is fermented more slowly and is relatively **butyrogenic**.
    Structure = Ch.3; *fermentability* = here. *(Educator aside: the SCFA receptors FFAR2/FFAR3 =
    GPR43/GPR41 exist — name only; the GLP-1/PYY satiety payoff is a Ch.8 callback and the metabolic signalling ties back to Ch.6 —
    not developed here.)* → `topping2001rs`, `ulven2012ffar`

**Part 3 — What they do #2: microbial vitamin synthesis.**

16. **Gut microbes make some vitamins — but "made in the gut" ≠ "available to you."** Bacteria
    synthesise vitamin **K2** (menaquinones) and several **B vitamins** (biotin, folate, riboflavin,
    B12). The bottleneck is anatomical: most synthesis is in the **colon**, *downstream* of the
    absorption sites. The clearest case is **B12** — most gut microbes actually *consume* B12, and the
    little they make is produced past the terminal ileum where (with intrinsic factor) B12 is absorbed
    (Ch.5). So **colonic microbial B12 is essentially unavailable to us; dietary (animal-source) B12
    is the functional supply.** K2 bioavailability is likewise limited (much is membrane-bound), and
    diet remains the principal vitamin-K source. Cross-ref Ch.4 for the vitamins themselves; do not
    quote a "gut makes X% of your needs" figure (unquantified). → `degnan2014b12`, `lai2022vitk`

**Part 4 — Feeding and shaping the microbiome (diet + the "-biotics").**

17. **Diet is the dominant, fast-acting, *reversible* lever.** Switching between animal-based and
    plant-based eating shifts the community within a **day** — but the shift **reverts within days** of
    returning to the usual diet, and individual identity largely persists. Diet is a powerful *ongoing*
    lever, *not* a one-time reprogramming. → `david2014diet`
18. **Fibre *diversity* feeds microbial diversity — "eat many plants."** People eating the widest range
    of plant types have the most diverse microbiomes; the memorable heuristic is **"~30 plant types a
    week"** (label it *associational*, a citizen-science observation, not a proven dose). Chronic
    low-fibre "Western" eating starves fibre-fermenters and lowers diversity (the dramatic
    *irreversible* diversity loss is a **mouse, across-generations** finding — teach the concept, label
    the model). Whole healthy **patterns** (e.g. Mediterranean) associate with more SCFA-producers and
    less frailty/inflammation (pattern teaching = Ch.9). → `mcdonald2018american`, `sonnenburg2016diet`,
    `ghosh2020mediterranean`
19. **Non-fibre modulators exist — but the human evidence is early; teach "plausible, watch this
    space," not settled.** Non-nutritive sweeteners can alter the microbiome and *modestly*,
    *person-specifically* affect glucose handling (do **not** say "cause diabetes"); emulsifiers
    perturb the microbiota in small/short human trials; broad-spectrum **antibiotics** are genuine
    disruptors with often-incomplete recovery. → `suez2022personalized`, `suez2014artificial`,
    `chassaing2022randomized`, `palleja2018recovery`
20. **The "-biotics" family (ISAPP consensus — learn the definitions precisely).**
    - **Probiotic** — "live microorganisms that, when administered in adequate amounts, confer a health
      benefit on the host." → `hill2014probiotic`
    - **Prebiotic** — "a substrate that is selectively utilized by host microorganisms conferring a
      health benefit" (broadened in 2017 beyond inulin/FOS/GOS). Prebiotic foods: onion, garlic, leek,
      chicory, slightly-green banana, wholegrains, legumes (fibre structure = Ch.3). → `gibson2017prebiotic`
    - **Synbiotic** (a probiotic + prebiotic combination) and **postbiotic** (a preparation of inanimate
      microbes and/or their components) — recognition level only. → `swanson2020synbiotic`, `salminen2021postbiotic`
21. **Fermented food ≠ probiotic.** A fermented food is defined by its *process* ("made through desired
    microbial growth and enzymatic conversions"), **not** by guaranteed live, defined, quantified
    microbes in the final product — many are pasteurised or cooked before eating. Fermented foods are
    good foods and *may* modestly benefit the microbiome (a small, short Stanford RCT found a
    high-fermented-food diet raised diversity and lowered inflammatory markers where high-fibre did not
    — hypothesis-generating, not a prescription), but most do not meet the probiotic bar. → `marco2021fermented`,
    `wastyk2021diet`

**Part 5 — Applied: recognise, don't manage.**

22. **Antibiotics disrupt the ecosystem.** Broad-spectrum antibiotics cause dysbiosis and
    **antibiotic-associated diarrhoea (AAD)**; the dangerous extreme is ***C. difficile* infection**.
    Co-administered probiotics give a *modest*, strain-dependent reduction in *C. diff* risk in
    higher-risk settings — but the counter-signal matters: some evidence shows probiotics can *delay*
    the native microbiome's recovery after antibiotics. State the strain/indication-specificity in a
    sentence; don't reproduce prescribing tables (Ch.13/clinical). *(Attribution care: the AGA
    guideline addresses* C. diff *prevention on antibiotics; the AAD benefit comes from Cochrane, not
    AGA — keep these separate.)* → `agaProbiotics2020`, `goldenberg2017cdi`, `suez2018probiotic`
23. **FMT — the striking "restore the ecosystem" proof-of-concept, for recurrent *C. diff* only.**
    Faecal microbiota transplant cures the large majority of recurrent *C. difficile* cases (a landmark
    RCT stopped early for benefit), and there are now two FDA-approved microbiota products (Rebyota,
    2022; Vowst, the first oral one, 2023). First-years should **recognise and refer** — and know FMT
    is **not** proven for obesity, IBS, autism or "general wellness," and carries real transmission risk
    (a 2019 FDA safety alert followed a fatal transmission). → `vannood2013fmt`, `fda2022rebyota`,
    `fda2023vowst`, `fmt2019safety`
24. **Commercial "microbiome test kits" are not clinically actionable for well people.** International
    consensus: no agreed "healthy" reference microbiome, poor reproducibility (the same stool sample
    can yield different results across companies), no proven clinical value without an indication. A
    clean myth-bust — and money better spent on more plants. → `porcari2024microbiometest`
25. **The gut–brain axis — real mechanism, cautious claims.** The microbiome *can* signal to the brain
    (vagus nerve, SCFAs, immune signalling, microbial neuroactive metabolites), and **~90–95% of the
    body's serotonin is made in the gut** — but that serotonin runs *digestion*, and peripheral
    serotonin **does not cross the blood–brain barrier**, so "gut serotonin = happiness" collapses on
    mechanism. Mood/behaviour ("psychobiotic") claims are mostly preclinical or small trials; keep them
    to a **cautious bracketed callback**, forward-referencing Ch.8. → `cryan2019microbiota`,
    `serotoninGutReview`

## 6. Singapore context

- **The wholegrain/fibre gap is the standout local hook.** Only **~4% of staples eaten in Singapore
  are wholegrain (NNS 2022)** against a ~30% target, and average fibre intake sits below the HPB
  recommendation (~20 g/day women, ~26 g/day men). This is the concrete, population-anchored way to say
  *most of us are under-feeding our microbiome* — and it dovetails with the Ch.5 low-wholegrain thread.
  Local high-fibre swaps: brown/red/mixed rice, wholemeal, legumes/dhal, thunder-tea rice, more veg.
  *(Use the ~4% wholegrain figure as the firm anchor; the "average ~13 g fibre/day" number is
  secondary/unverified — see §11.)* → `mohNNS2022`
- **Bridge from Ch.5's lactose hook.** Ch.5 teaches that ~95% of Singapore-Chinese adults are lactase
  non-persistent (`yap1989`). *Where does the undigested lactose go?* Into the colon, where bacteria
  ferment it → gas, bloating, osmotic diarrhoea. That is this chapter's mechanism in miniature — the
  microbiome eating what the small intestine could not. Reuse the hook; do not re-teach lactose
  digestion. → `yap1989` *(Ch.5-owned)*
- **Culturally familiar fermented foods — with an honest live-culture distinction.** Anchor the
  fermented-foods teaching on foods students know, and use them to teach *fermented ≠ live probiotic*:
  **live-culture** (eaten raw/unheated): kimchi, unpasteurised achar, **Yakult/cultured-milk drinks**,
  kombucha; **cooked → few/no live microbes** (still good foods): tempeh, taucheo/fermented soybean
  paste, belacan, fish sauce, and **miso once boiled** (add off the heat to keep cultures). Organism
  classes (background): tempeh = *Rhizopus*; natto = *Bacillus subtilis* (a notable K2/MK-7 source —
  ties to the vitamin point); miso/taucheo = *Aspergillus oryzae* (koji); kimchi = lactic-acid
  bacteria; **tapai** = mould + yeast (contains **~3–5% alcohol — flag for a Muslim-majority regional
  context**). **Drop "yong tau foo" — it is not fermented.** → `marco2021fermented`, `ferment_organisms`
- **Yakult is a daily SG habit — the "modest, not magic" teaching moment.** *L. casei* Shirota does
  survive transit and has some small-trial signals, but regulators (EFSA) have restricted broad
  immune/health claims. Use it to teach *marketed benefit ≠ authorised/proven benefit*. → `katokataoka2016lcs`,
  `efsa2010lcs`
- **Genuine SG cohort data exist — use them for variability + ageing.** The **HELIOS** study (a
  peer-reviewed multi-ethnic SG cohort, n=10,004) and the Yeo 2021 multi-ethnic cohort show **no single

  signatures — a strong local illustration of inter-individual/ethnic variability and the
  enterotype-as-continuum idea. A SG octogenarian metagenomics study anchors the older-age
  diversity-decline point. *(The ethnic-signature microbiome analysis is currently a 2026 preprint —
  cite the peer-reviewed cohort paper as the anchor and flag the preprint; see §11.)* → `helios2025cohort`,
  `yeo2021sgmultiethnic`, `narayanan2024sgocto`, `helios2026microbiome`
- **Vegetarian dietary patterns raise the B12 stakes locally.** Significant vegetarian/vegan practice
  in parts of SG's Indian and Buddhist communities makes the "you can't rely on gut bacteria for B12"

## 8. Clinical application & case ideas (branching, first-year altitude)

- **Case A — "I bought a gut-microbiome test kit online. What do I do with the results?"** A well
  24-year-old brings a colourful report saying her *Bifidobacterium* is "low," recommending supplements.
  Branches: (1, target) recognise DTC kits as non-actionable for the well — no agreed healthy
  reference, poor reproducibility, no clinical action (`porcari2024microbiometest`); redirect to fibre
  diversity; (2) don't buy the recommended supplements (upsell); (3) *but* if she has real symptoms,
  that warrants proper assessment, not a kit — refer. Beat: myth-bust + food-first + know when symptoms
  need evaluation.
- **Case B — "After my antibiotics my gut's been off. Do probiotics help?"** A patient has loose stools
  after antibiotics. Branches: (1, target) honest framing — probiotics give a *modest*, strain-dependent
  reduction in AAD/*C. diff* risk in higher-risk settings, may even *delay* natural recovery
  (`suez2018probiotic`), and most AAD is self-limiting; (2) red flags (severe/bloody diarrhoea, fever,
  dehydration, recent hospitalisation) → recognise possible *C. diff*, escalate/refer. Beat: modest
  honest evidence + recognise-and-refer.
- **Case C — "How do I actually 'eat for my gut'?"** A student wants a concrete plan after reading
  gut-health content online. Branches: (1) "which one superfood?" → none; the lever is *fibre
  diversity* (wholegrains, legumes/dhal, fruit, veg, nuts), remembering SG's ~4% wholegrain staples —
  swap white → brown/mixed rice sometimes, add tempeh/dhal/veg; (2) "do fermented foods count?" → yes,
  modestly, if they still have live cultures (raw kimchi, unpasteurised achar, cultured-milk drinks);
  cooked ones (tempeh, taucheo, boiled miso) are still fine foods, just not live-culture delivery. Beat:
  diversity + gradual fibre increase (with fluids) beats any product.
- **Case D — Myth-bust: "I'm going to do a juice cleanse / colon detox to reset my microbiome."**
  Branches: (1) "does a cleanse reset it?" → no toxin backlog to flush; a low-fibre juice cleanse can
  actually *under-feed* the microbiome (coordinate with Ch.5's detox/leaky-gut myth-bust — this chapter adds
  the microbiome angle); (2) "what actually 'resets' a gut ecosystem?" → the only clinical example is
  **FMT for recurrent *C. diff*** — a medical procedure with a narrow indication, not a wellness
  cleanse. Beat: distinguish marketing "detox" from the one real "ecosystem restore."
- **Case E (bridge/micro) — "Why do beans/onions/too much milk give me wind?"** Recognise normal colonic
  fermentation of fermentable carbohydrate (and undigested lactose — Ch.5 bridge) producing H₂/CH₄/CO₂;
  reassure and distinguish normal gas from alarm features. Ties the owned gas mechanism to a universal
  lived experience. *(Log candidates in `../cases/case-bank.md`.)*

## 9. Common misconceptions & pitfalls

1. **"There are 10 bacteria for every human cell."** No — it's roughly **1:1** (Sender 2016). The 10:1
   figure was a 1970s back-of-envelope estimate repeated uncited for decades.
2. **"You carry 1–2 kg of gut bacteria."** Overstated — supportable bacterial mass is **~0.2 kg**; the
   larger figure conflates bacteria with total colonic contents/stool.
3. **"The gut is your second brain / gut serotonin controls your mood."** A metaphor, not a mechanism.
   The enteric nervous system is a genuine neural network, and ~90–95% of serotonin is made in the gut —
   but that serotonin runs digestion and **doesn't cross the blood–brain barrier**.
4. **"Everyone belongs to one of three enterotypes."** Outdated — read as a **continuum**, not fixed
   categories (Knights 2014).
5. **"A C-section permanently damages the baby's microbiome."** Overstated — real early differences are
   largely transient; feeding mode is a stronger, more durable driver. "Vaginal seeding" is not an
   evidence-based fix (ACOG).
6. **"More diverse is always healthier."** Oversimplified — diversity is a loose, context-dependent
   marker, not a target to maximise.
7. **"Your gut can't digest fibre, so it's useless / just for regularity."** Your *enzymes* can't, but
   your *bacteria ferment* it into SCFAs that fuel the colon lining and maintain the mucus barrier —
   fibre's bigger job.
8. **"Butyrate is the most abundant SCFA because it's the most important."** Butyrate is the *least*
   abundant of the three yet the colonocyte's preferred fuel; acetate is most abundant. Abundance ≠
   local importance. And oral **butyrate supplements** have poor bioavailability — most "butyrate cures
   X" claims are preclinical.
9. **"Gut bacteria make all the vitamin B12 (or K) you need."** No — colonic synthesis sits downstream
   of the absorption sites; for B12 the microbes are net *consumers*. Dietary (animal-source) B12 is the
   functional supply.
10. **"Fermented foods are probiotics."** Not necessarily — many are cooked/pasteurised or contain
    undefined, unquantified organisms, so they fail the probiotic bar (defined strain, adequate dose,
    demonstrated benefit).
11. **"Probiotics are good for everyone and fix any gut problem."** Effects are strain- and
    indication-specific and often modest; evidence is insufficient/against for routine use in healthy
    people (AGA), and probiotics can even delay recovery after antibiotics.
12. **"Take probiotics routinely after every course of antibiotics."** Nuanced — a modest preventive
    signal for *C. diff* in higher-risk settings, but not a blanket green light (Suez 2018).
13. **"FMT / a stool transplant is a wellness or weight-loss treatment."** Proven and approved only for
    recurrent *C. diff*; unproven for obesity/IBS/autism, and it carries real transmission risk.
14. **"A microbiome test kit tells you how healthy your gut is."** No agreed healthy reference, poor
    reproducibility, not clinically actionable for well people (Porcari 2024).
15. **"A detox/cleanse resets your microbiome."** There is no toxin backlog to flush; a low-fibre
    cleanse can under-feed your microbes. The only real "ecosystem reset" is FMT, for a narrow medical
    indication.
16. **"Your diet permanently rewires your microbiome."** Diet shifts the community within a day but it
    *reverts* within days; diet is a powerful *ongoing* lever, not a one-time reprogramming.
17. **"Artificial sweeteners destroy your gut / give you diabetes."** Overstated — human evidence is
    small, short and person-specific, with *modest* glycaemic effects; not "destruction," not "diabetes."
18. **"The microbiome causes depression/autism."** Unproven in humans — the striking behavioural
    rescues are mouse models; psychobiotic efficacy is not yet convincingly demonstrated in patients.
19. **"Gas/bloating means something is wrong with my microbiome."** Colonic fermentation of fibre and
    undigested lactose normally produces gas — usually a sign the system is working, not failing.
20. **"First-year means memorising every genus and metabolite."** No — recognise the community, the
    fibre→SCFA→fate mechanism, the "-biotics" definitions, and the honest evidence; species-level
    detail is for later.
